# Episode 14: Building a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline

## Overview
Episode 14 explores how small podcast teams can build reliable production pipelines that go beyond just clean audio to ensure consistent quality across all outputs: transcripts, show notes, titles, descriptions, and social content.

## Key Insights

### Core Problem
Most podcast teams don't need more AI—they need fewer invisible handoffs. When transcripts, descriptions, and clips live in different places, publishing quality becomes luck-based rather than systematic.

### Transcript Philosophy
A transcript is "rich input, not final truth." Raw transcripts contain filler, false starts, and unverified references that shouldn't be treated as publish-ready content.

### Pipeline Stages
1. **Capture**: Recording + companion materials (guest bios, sponsor copy, links)
2. **Post-production**: Audio cleanup and editorial decisions
3. **Content extraction**: Transcripts, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapters
4. **Review & approval**: Fact-checking, sponsor compliance, sensitive edits
5. **Distribution**: Final package to hosting, newsletter, social, archives

### Automation Mindset
"Use automation for acceleration, not abdication." Make the machine produce options; make the human approve what becomes public.

### Critical Checks Before Publishing
1. Names and affiliations (guest details)
2. Links and references (verify destinations)
3. Sponsor compliance (required language)
4. Sensitive edits (guest-requested removals)
5. Metadata consistency (title, description, transcript alignment)

## Practical Takeaways

### For Small Teams
- Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention
- Create a publish checklist with clear ownership
- Separate raw transcript from cleaned transcript
- Define required output bundle for every episode
- Maintain a lightweight episode log to track delays and errors

### Title Strategy
Generate multiple title candidates from different angles:
- Problem-oriented
- Guest-oriented  
- Outcome-oriented
- Curiosity-driven

Frequency is not strategy—the most repeated phrase is often not the most compelling framing.
