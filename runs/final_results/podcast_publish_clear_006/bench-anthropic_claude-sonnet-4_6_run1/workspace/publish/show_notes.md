# Episode 14 – Building a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline
## Show Notes

**Guest:** Maya Chen, media production systems advisor

---

### [00:00:00] Introduction — The Pipeline Is the Product

The host introduces the episode's focus: how a raw recording becomes a polished, distributed episode. Guest Maya Chen frames the core insight — the production pipeline is often invisible until something breaks, at which point everyone realizes it *is* the product.

---

### [00:01:20] The Five Stages of a Podcast Production Pipeline

Maya breaks down the five major stages:

1. **Capture** — Recording the conversation plus companion materials (guest bios, sponsor copy, links, references)
2. **Post-production** — Audio cleanup, trimming, and editorial decisions
3. **Content extraction** — Transcript generation, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, and clips
4. **Review and approval** — Fact-checking, sponsor language, names, links, and editorial/legal review
5. **Distribution** — Publishing to hosting platform, newsletter, social channels, and archives

The pipeline is compared to a software build system: source material → transformations → validation → packaging → deployment.

---

### [00:02:29] Where Small Teams Struggle First

Three common pain points for small teams:

- **Handoffs** — No single source of truth when different people handle recording, editing, and writing
- **Inconsistent naming and asset storage** — Version confusion ("final_v2_real_final.wav")
- **Transcript-driven tasks** — Raw transcripts are not automatically publish-ready copy

---

### [00:03:03] Transcripts: Rich Input, Not Final Truth

A raw transcript can contain filler, false starts, jokes that don't read well, corrected statements, and unverified placeholder references ("we'll put the link in the show notes"). Treating a transcript as clean structured truth leads to low-quality or misleading derivative content.

**What transcripts are good for:** First drafts of summaries, chapter suggestions, quote candidates, social post options, searchability, and repurposing.

**What still needs human review:** Facts, links, sponsor language, names, affiliations, dates, and any authoritative claims.

> *"The transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth."* — Maya Chen

---

### [00:04:15] Lightweight Pipeline Design for Small Teams

Recommended structure for a two- or three-person team:

- One folder or workspace per episode
- A clear naming convention
- A checklist with defined handoff points
- A **fixed output bundle** for every episode: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, and a publish log

**On transcripts:** Keep both the raw transcript (for traceability) and a cleaned transcript (for summarization and repurposing). The cleaned version removes disfluencies, fixes speaker labels, and formats the conversation for readability.

---

### [00:05:49] Automation: Acceleration, Not Abdication

Where teams overestimate automation:

- **Editorial judgment** — An agent can propose titles but doesn't know audience positioning unless it's been encoded
- **Exception handling** — Guest name corrections, legal review concerns, sensitive anecdotes, sponsor placement constraints; automated systems fail on edge cases

Healthy mindset: use automation to accelerate repetitive transforms (transcription cleanup, first-draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, asset packaging, metadata prefill) — but a human must still own correctness, tone, and release quality.

---

### [00:06:52] Titles: A Contract with the Listener

Generate several title candidates from different angles:

- Problem-oriented: *"Why Podcast Production Breaks Even When the Recording Is Great"*
- Outcome-oriented: *"From Raw Recording to Publish: The Systems Behind a Great Podcast"*
- Audience-oriented: *"How Small Teams Build a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline"*

Frequency of a phrase in the transcript is not a title strategy. Good titles come from understanding what the episode is helping the listener do.

**Workflow note:** Aim for three title candidates, one short description, one long description, and a final approved version logged before publish.

---

### [00:08:13] Pre-Publish Review Checklist

Five highest-value checks before an episode goes live:

1. **Names and affiliations** — Guest name, title, and company
2. **Links and references** — Verify every destination that was promised in the recording
3. **Sponsor compliance** — Required sponsor language is present and correctly placed
4. **Sensitive edits** — Confirm anything the guest wanted removed is actually removed
5. **Metadata consistency** — Title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy all describe the same episode

---

### [00:09:13] Practical Takeaways

Steps to improve your pipeline this month:

1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. Create a publish checklist that one person owns
3. Separate raw transcript and cleaned transcript
4. Define the required output bundle for every episode
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to surface delays, confusion, and repeated errors

**Bonus:** If experimenting with automation, start by automating drafts — not approvals. Make the machine produce options; make the human approve what becomes public.
