# Episode 14 – Building a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline

## [00:00:00] Why the production pipeline *is* the product
- How a show moves from raw recording to polished episode: audio, transcript, chapters, title, description, and metadata.
- Maya Chen joins to explain why teams only notice the pipeline when something breaks.
- The idea that editing, packaging, labeling, and distribution shape the listener experience as much as the conversation itself.

## [00:01:20] The five stages of a podcast production pipeline
- Capture: recording the conversation and gathering guest bios, sponsor copy, links, and references.
- Post-production: cleaning audio, trimming mistakes, and deciding what stays.
- Content extraction: transcripts, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, and clips.
- Review and approval: checking facts, sponsor language, names, links, and potential editorial or legal issues.
- Distribution: sending the final package to the hosting platform, newsletter, social channels, and archives.

## [00:02:33] Common pain points for small teams
- Handoffs without a single source of truth for assets.
- Messy file naming and storage (e.g., "final_v2_real_final.wav").
- Over-reliance on raw transcripts as if they were clean, publishable content.

## [00:03:09] How to treat transcripts as inputs, not final truth
- Why raw transcripts contain filler, false starts, and unverified promises like "we'll put the link in the show notes."
- Using transcripts as inputs for first drafts of summaries, chapters, quotes, and social copy.
- The rule that anything authoritative (facts, links, sponsor language, names, dates, claims) needs human review.
- Key idea: a transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth.

## [00:04:26] Designing a lightweight pipeline for a small team
- One folder or workspace per episode, with a clear naming convention.
- A simple checklist with defined handoff points.
- Standardizing the output bundle so every episode ships with: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail (if used), and a publish log.

## [00:04:54] Raw vs. clean transcripts
- Keeping the raw transcript for traceability and internal reference.
- Creating a cleaned transcript that fixes disfluencies, speaker labels, and formatting.
- Using the cleaned transcript as the preferred source for summaries and repurposed content.
- How separating raw and clean material reduces noise for downstream tools and teammates.

## [00:05:49] Where automation and agents help—and where they don’t
- Why teams overestimate automation around editorial judgment and exception handling.
- Examples of edge cases: guest name corrections, legal-review flags, sensitive anecdotes, sponsor placement constraints.
- Using automation to accelerate repetitive transforms (cleanup, first-draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, packaging, metadata prefill).
- Keeping humans responsible for correctness, tone, and release quality.

## [00:06:52] Crafting reliable episode titles
- Generating multiple title candidates from different angles: problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, and curiosity-driven.
- Thinking of the title as a contract with the listener, not just a label.
- Why frequency of phrases in a transcript doesn’t equal strategic positioning.

## [00:08:13] High-value checks before publishing an episode
- Verifying names and affiliations for the guest.
- Checking all links and references that the hosts promise to include.
- Ensuring sponsor language meets requirements and appears in the right places.
- Confirming that requested sensitive edits are actually removed.
- Making sure the title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy all describe the same episode.

## [00:09:13] Practical steps to improve your pipeline this month
- Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention.
- Create a publish checklist with a clear owner.
- Separate raw and cleaned transcripts for every episode.
- Decide on a fixed output bundle for each release.
- Maintain a lightweight episode log to spot delays, confusion, and recurring errors.
- When experimenting with automation, start by automating drafts—not approvals.
