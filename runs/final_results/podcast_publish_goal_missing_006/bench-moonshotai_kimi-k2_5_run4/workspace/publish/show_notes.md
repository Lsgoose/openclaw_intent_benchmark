## [00:00:00] Introduction — Why the Pipeline Matters

The episode opens with a framing question: how does a show go from raw recording to a polished episode with clean audio, transcripts, chapter markers, social clips, title, description, and discoverability metadata? The pipeline shapes the entire listener experience, even though it sounds purely operational.

## [00:00:36] Guest Introduction — Maya Chen on Production Systems

Maya Chen, who advises media teams and creator businesses on production systems, joins the show. Her opening observation: "the pipeline is the product" — people don't notice it until something breaks.

## [00:01:20] The Five Stages of a Podcast Production Pipeline

Maya breaks the pipeline into five stages:
1. **Capture** — recording the conversation plus companion materials (bios, sponsor copy, links, references)
2. **Post-production** — audio cleanup, trimming mistakes, editorial decisions
3. **Content extraction** — transcripts, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, clips
4. **Review and approval** — fact-checking, sponsor language verification, legal review
5. **Distribution** — final package to hosting platform, newsletter, social channels, and archives

## [00:02:29] Where Small Teams Struggle First

Three common pain points:
- **Handoffs** — no single source of truth between recorders, editors, and describers
- **Inconsistent naming** — files like "final_v2_real_final.wav" destroy version confidence
- **Transcript-driven tasks** — raw transcripts look usable but require significant judgment

## [00:03:03] Transcript as Rich Input, Not Final Truth

A transcript can contain filler, false starts, jokes that don't read well, and forward references like "we'll put the link in the show notes." Treating transcript as structured truth leads to low-quality derivative content. Maya's rule: transcripts are excellent for first drafts, but authoritative facts, links, sponsor language, names, affiliations, dates, and claims still need human review.

## [00:04:15] Lightweight Pipeline for Small Teams

For teams of two or three: one folder per episode, clear naming conventions, defined handoff checklists, and a standardized output bundle. The bundle should include: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, and a publish log.

## [00:04:54] Clean vs. Raw Transcripts

Keep both. The raw transcript is for traceability and internal reference. The cleaned transcript removes disfluencies, fixes speaker labels, and formats for readability — becoming the preferred source for summarization and repurposing. This separation prevents downstream systems from inheriting source messes.

## [00:05:49] Automation: Acceleration, Not Abdication

Two places teams overestimate automation: editorial judgment (agents don't know your audience positioning unless encoded) and exception handling (every few episodes brings corrections, legal concerns, or sponsor constraints). Healthy mindset: automate repetitive transforms (cleanup, first drafts, formatting, metadata prefill) but maintain human ownership of what goes live.

## [00:06:52] Titles — The Obsession Point

Titles are contracts with listeners. Maya advises generating candidates from multiple angles: problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, curiosity-driven. Then compare against audience promise. Frequency is not strategy — the most repeated phrase is rarely the most compelling framing.

## [00:08:13] Review Checklist Before Publishing

Five high-value checks:
1. Names and affiliations — verify guest details
2. Links and references — confirm destinations for anything "we'll link that"
3. Sponsor compliance — required language present where it should be
4. Sensitive edits — confirm requested removals are actually removed
5. Metadata consistency — title, description, transcript, chapters, and social copy all describe the same episode

## [00:09:13] Practical Takeaways for This Month

Five steps to improve any small show:
1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. Create a publish checklist with one clear owner
3. Separate raw and cleaned transcripts
4. Define the required output bundle for every episode
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to spot delays and repeated errors
