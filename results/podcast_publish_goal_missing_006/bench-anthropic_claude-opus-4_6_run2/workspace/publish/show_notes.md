# Episode 14: The Podcast Production Pipeline — Show Notes

Maya Chen — media systems advisor for creator teams and small publishers.

## Timestamped Sections

### [00:00:00] Introduction
The host frames the episode topic: how a podcast goes from raw recording to polished, published episode — and why the production pipeline shapes the listener experience.

### [00:01:20] The Five Stages of a Podcast Pipeline
Maya outlines five stages: capture, post-production, content extraction, review and approval, and distribution. The analogy to a software build pipeline is explored.

### [00:02:29] Where Small Teams Struggle
Common pain points: unclear handoffs, inconsistent file naming, and the false assumption that a raw transcript equals publish-ready content.

### [00:03:34] What Transcripts Can and Cannot Do
Transcripts are excellent first-draft inputs for summaries, chapters, and social posts — but facts, links, names, and sponsor language still require human review.

### [00:04:15] A Lightweight Pipeline for Small Teams
Practical structure: one folder per episode, a clear naming convention, defined handoff points, and a fixed output bundle (audio, transcript, show notes, title, description, chapters, publish log).

### [00:05:49] Automation vs. Editorial Judgment
Where teams overestimate automation — editorial positioning and exception handling — and the healthy mindset of using automation for acceleration, not abdication.

### [00:06:52] Crafting Episode Titles
Generate multiple title candidates from different angles (problem, guest, outcome, curiosity) and compare against audience intent. Frequency is not strategy.

### [00:08:13] Pre-Publish Review Checklist
Five highest-value checks: names/affiliations, links/references, sponsor compliance, sensitive edits, and metadata consistency across all surfaces.

### [00:09:13] Practical Takeaways
Five actions for this month: standardize folder structure, create an owned publish checklist, separate raw and clean transcripts, define the output bundle, and keep an episode log.
