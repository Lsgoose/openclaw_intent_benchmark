# Episode 14: Building a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline

## Show Notes

### [00:00:00] Introduction
Welcome to Episode 14, where we unpack how a podcast goes from raw recording to polished, distributed episode. Production pipelines shape the entire listener experience, even when audiences don't notice them until something breaks.
- Guest: Maya Chen, media production systems advisor for creator businesses <https://creators.example.com/guests/maya-chen>

### [00:01:20] The 5 Core Pipeline Stages
Maya breaks the production process into five repeatable stages:
1. **Capture**: Record conversation and collect companion materials (guest bios, sponsor copy, links)
2. **Post-production**: Clean audio, trim mistakes, finalize episode content
3. **Content extraction**: Generate transcripts, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, clips
4. **Review and approval**: Verify facts, sponsor language, names, links, and editorial/legal compliance
5. **Distribution**: Push final package to hosting platforms, newsletters, social channels, and archives

This framework mirrors software build pipelines: source material, transformations, validation, packaging, and deployment.

### [00:02:29] Common Small Team Pain Points
Most small teams struggle in three areas:
1. Handoff ambiguity between team members with no single source of truth
2. Inconsistent file naming and storage ("final_v2_real_final.wav" chaos)
3. Over-reliance on raw transcripts as publish-ready content

### [00:03:09] Transcript Best Practices
Raw transcripts contain filler, false starts, uncorrected statements, and unvetted references. Treat transcripts as rich input, not final editorial truth:
- Keep raw transcripts for traceability and internal reference
- Produce cleaned transcripts with disfluencies removed and speaker labels corrected for downstream use
- Use transcripts for first drafts of summaries, chapter suggestions, and quote candidates – always review facts, links, and claims before publication

### [00:04:15] Lightweight Pipeline Design for Small Teams
For teams of 2-3 people, keep the system simple:
- One folder/workspace per episode with standardized naming
- Clear checklist with defined handoff points
- Fixed output bundle for every episode: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, publish log
- Standardized outputs simplify all downstream automation and repurposing

### [00:05:49] Automation Boundaries
Teams often overestimate what can be fully automated:
- **Editorial judgment**: AI can propose titles but doesn't know your audience positioning unless explicitly encoded
- **Exception handling**: Edge cases (name corrections, legal concerns, sponsor constraints) break fully automated systems
- Healthy approach: Use automation to accelerate repetitive tasks (transcript cleanup, first-draft notes, formatting) not to abdicate editorial responsibility

### [00:06:52] Title Strategy
Titles are a contract with the listener, not just labels. Generate multiple candidates from different angles:
- Problem-oriented: "Why Podcast Production Breaks Even When the Recording Is Great"
- Outcome-oriented: "How Small Teams Build a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline"
- Curiosity-driven: "From Raw Recording to Publish: The Systems Behind a Great Podcast"
- Choose based on your audience promise, not just most repeated phrases

### [00:08:13] Pre-Publish High-Value Checks
Prioritize these five checks before going live:
1. Correct guest names, titles, and affiliations
2. Verified links and references (no broken show note links)
3. Sponsor compliance with required language placements
4. Confirmed sensitive edits are implemented as requested
5. Metadata consistency across title, description, transcript, chapters, and social copy

### [00:09:13] Practical First Steps
If you want to improve your pipeline this month:
1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. Create a publish checklist owned by one person
3. Separate raw and cleaned transcript workflows
4. Define your required output bundle for every episode
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to track delays and repeated errors
