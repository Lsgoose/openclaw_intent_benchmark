# Show Notes

[00:00] Intro: the podcast production pipeline shapes the listener experience from raw recording to publish.

[00:27] Guest introduction: Maya Chen joins to talk about production systems for media teams.

[00:47] Why the pipeline matters: editing, packaging, labeling, and distribution all affect the product.

[01:20] Major stages: capture, post-production, content extraction, review and approval, and distribution.

[02:29] Common small-team failures: handoffs, inconsistent naming, and overestimating transcript quality.

[03:09] Transcript caution: transcripts contain filler, corrections, and unverified references, so they are rich inputs, not final truth.

[03:40] What can be extracted: summaries, chapters, quotes, and social drafts can start from transcripts, but facts, links, names, sponsor language, and claims need review.

[04:26] Lightweight pipeline: one episode workspace, clear naming, defined handoffs, and a fixed output bundle.

[04:59] Raw vs cleaned transcripts: keep both; use the cleaned transcript for repurposing and the raw transcript for traceability.

[05:57] Automation limits: titles and exception handling still need human judgment.

[06:31] Healthy automation use: automate repetitive transforms, but keep human ownership of correctness and release quality.

[06:57] Title strategy: create multiple title candidates from different angles and choose based on audience promise.

[08:20] Pre-publish review: verify names, links, sponsor compliance, sensitive edits, and metadata consistency.

[09:23] Practical next steps: standard folder structure, a publish checklist, raw and cleaned transcript separation, a required output bundle, and a lightweight episode log.

[09:58] Closing principle: let the machine draft options; let the human approve what goes live.
