# Episode 14: Building a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline

## Guest
[Maya Chen](https://creators.example.com/guests/maya-chen) — Advises media teams and creator businesses on production systems.

---

## Show Notes

### [00:00] Introduction: Why the Pipeline Is the Product
The episode opens with a framing of podcast production pipelines as something that shapes the entire listener experience—not just an operational detail. Maya Chen joins to discuss how a show goes from raw recording to polished episode.

### [00:01:27] The Five Stages of a Production Pipeline
Maya breaks down the pipeline into five clear stages:
1. **Capture** — Recording and collecting companion materials (bios, sponsor copy, links)
2. **Post-production** — Audio cleanup, trimming, editorial decisions
3. **Content extraction** — Transcripts, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapters, titles, clips
4. **Review and approval** — Fact-checking, sponsor language verification, legal review
5. **Distribution** — Publishing to hosting platforms, newsletters, social channels, and archives

### [00:02:33] Where Small Teams Struggle
Three common pain points for lean teams:
- **Handoffs** — No single source of truth when multiple people touch an episode
- **Inconsistent naming** — Files like "final_v2_real_final.wav" create confusion about the source of record
- **Transcript misuse** — Assuming raw transcripts are publish-ready without judgment

### [00:03:03] Transcripts as Rich Input, Not Final Truth
A raw transcript contains filler, false starts, jokes that don't read well, and corrected statements. Maya emphasizes that transcripts are excellent inputs for first drafts (summaries, chapters, quotes) but anything authoritative—facts, links, sponsor language, names—requires human review.

> "The transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth." — Maya Chen

### [00:04:15] A Lightweight Pipeline for Small Teams
Maya's recommendations for teams of 2–3 people:
- One folder/workspace per episode
- Clear naming conventions
- A checklist with defined handoff points
- A fixed output bundle for every episode (final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapters, thumbnail, publish log)

### [00:04:54] Raw vs. Clean Transcripts
Best practice: maintain both versions.
- **Raw transcript** — For traceability and internal reference
- **Cleaned transcript** — Disfluencies removed, speaker labels fixed, formatted for readability; becomes the preferred source for summarization and repurposing

### [00:05:57] Automation: Acceleration, Not Abdication
Two places teams overestimate automation:
- **Editorial judgment** — Agents can propose titles but don't know audience positioning unless encoded
- **Exception handling** — Edge cases (name corrections, legal review, sensitive edits) break fully automated systems

> "Use automation to accelerate repetitive transforms... But don't give up responsibility for what goes live."

### [00:06:52] The Art of Titling
Titles are contracts with listeners. Maya recommends generating candidates from multiple angles:
- Problem-oriented
- Guest-oriented
- Outcome-oriented
- Curiosity-driven

Frequency is not strategy—the most repeated phrase in a conversation is rarely the most compelling framing.

### [00:08:13] The Five Highest-Value Pre-Publish Checks
1. Names and affiliations
2. Links and references (verify every "we'll link that")
3. Sponsor compliance
4. Sensitive edits (confirm requested removals)
5. Metadata consistency (title, description, transcript, chapters, and social copy should align)

### [00:09:23] Five First Steps to Improve Your Pipeline This Month
1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. Create a publish checklist that one person owns
3. Separate raw and cleaned transcripts
4. Define the required output bundle for every episode
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to track delays and errors
