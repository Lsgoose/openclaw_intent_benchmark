# Episode 14 — Building a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline

**Guest:** Maya Chen, media production systems advisor

---

## [00:00:00] Introduction — The Pipeline Is the Product

The host frames the episode: a podcast's listener experience is shaped not just by the conversation itself, but by every step that turns a raw recording into a polished, discoverable episode. Maya Chen joins to unpack how that process works and where it breaks.

> "People don't always notice [the pipeline] until something breaks, and then suddenly everyone realizes the pipeline is the product." — Maya Chen

---

## [00:01:20] The Five Stages of a Podcast Production Pipeline

Maya breaks down the full pipeline into five distinct stages:

1. **Capture** — Recording the conversation and collecting companion materials (guest bios, sponsor copy, links, references)
2. **Post-production** — Audio cleanup, trimming, and editorial decisions
3. **Content extraction** — Transcript generation, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, and clips
4. **Review and approval** — Fact-checking, sponsor language, names, links, and editorial/legal review
5. **Distribution** — Publishing to hosting platform, newsletter, social channels, and archives

The software build pipeline is a useful analogy: source material → transformations → validation → packaging → deployment.

---

## [00:02:29] Where Small Teams Struggle: Handoffs, Naming, and Transcripts

Three common pain points for small teams:

- **Handoffs** — No single source of truth when recording, editing, and writing are split across people
- **Inconsistent naming and asset storage** — Version confusion ("final_v2_real_final.wav") erodes confidence in which file is canonical
- **Transcript-driven tasks** — Raw transcripts contain filler, false starts, unverified links, and corrected statements; treating them as publish-ready copy leads to low-quality or misleading output

---

## [00:03:34] What to Extract from Transcripts — and What Still Needs Human Review

Transcripts are strong inputs for:
- First-draft episode summaries
- Chapter suggestions
- Quote candidates
- Social post options
- Searchability and repurposing

Anything authoritative — facts, links, sponsor language, names, affiliations, dates, claims — still requires human review.

> "The transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth." — Maya Chen

---

## [00:04:15] Lightweight Pipeline Design for Small Teams

Practical structure for a two- or three-person team:

- One folder or workspace per episode
- A clear naming convention
- A checklist with defined handoff points
- A **standard output bundle** for every episode: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, and a publish log

**On transcripts:** Keep both the raw transcript (for traceability) and a cleaned transcript (for summarization and repurposing). The cleaned version removes disfluencies, fixes speaker labels, and formats the conversation for readability.

---

## [00:05:49] Automation and Agents — Where Teams Overestimate

Two areas where automation is commonly overestimated:

1. **Editorial judgment** — An agent can propose titles, but it doesn't know your audience positioning unless that context has been explicitly encoded
2. **Exception handling** — Edge cases (name corrections, legal review, sensitive anecdotes, sponsor constraints) are where fully automated systems tend to fail, and media teams encounter them regularly

The healthy mindset: **use automation to accelerate repetitive transforms, not to abdicate responsibility for what goes live.** Make the machine produce options; make the human approve what becomes public.

---

## [00:06:52] Titles — Generating Candidates from Multiple Angles

Titles do significant work: they are a contract with the listener, not just a label. Maya's approach:

- Generate several candidates from different angles: problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, curiosity-driven
- Compare against your actual audience promise
- Avoid mechanically picking the most-repeated phrase — frequency is not strategy

Example candidates for this episode:
- *"How Small Teams Build a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline"*
- *"Why Podcast Production Breaks Even When the Recording Is Great"*
- *"From Raw Recording to Publish: The Systems Behind a Great Podcast"*

Workflow recommendation: three title candidates, one short description, one long description, and a final approved version logged before publish.

---

## [00:08:13] Pre-Publish Review — The Five Highest-Value Checks

Before any episode goes live:

1. **Names and affiliations** — Verify the guest's name, title, and company
2. **Links and references** — Confirm every promised link has a verified destination
3. **Sponsor compliance** — Ensure required sponsor language is present and correctly placed
4. **Sensitive edits** — Confirm anything the guest requested removed is actually gone
5. **Metadata consistency** — Title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy must all describe the same episode

---

## [00:09:13] Practical Takeaways — Improving Your Pipeline This Month

Five concrete first steps:

1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. Create a publish checklist that one person owns
3. Separate raw transcript from cleaned transcript
4. Define the required output bundle for every episode
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to surface where delays, confusion, or repeated errors occur

Host addition: **if experimenting with automation, start by automating drafts — not approvals.**
