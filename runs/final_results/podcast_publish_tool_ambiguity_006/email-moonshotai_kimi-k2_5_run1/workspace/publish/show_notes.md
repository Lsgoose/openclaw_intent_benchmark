# Episode 14: Building a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline

**Guest:** Maya Chen — Media systems advisor  
[https://creators.example.com/guests/maya-chen](https://creators.example.com/guests/maya-chen)

---

## Show Notes

### [00:00:00] Introduction: The Pipeline Is the Product
What sounds operational on the surface actually shapes the entire listener experience. This episode unpacks how a show goes from raw recording to polished episode—from clean audio and transcripts to chapter markers, metadata, and discoverability.

### [00:01:20] The Five Stages of a Podcast Production Pipeline
Maya breaks the pipeline into five clear stages:
1. **Capture** — Recording plus companion materials (bios, sponsor copy, links)
2. **Post-production** — Audio cleanup, trimming, editorial decisions
3. **Content extraction** — Transcripts, summaries, quotes, chapters, titles, clips
4. **Review and approval** — Fact checks, sponsor language, names, links, legal review
5. **Distribution** — Publishing to hosting platforms, newsletters, social channels, and archives

> "Just like software systems, podcast pipelines become fragile when the process depends too much on memory and heroics."

### [00:02:29] Where Small Teams Struggle
The first pain points teams encounter:
- **Handoffs** — No single source of truth when multiple people touch the episode
- **Inconsistent naming** — Files like "final_v2_real_final.wav" destroy confidence
- **Transcript assumptions** — Raw transcripts are not automatically publish-ready

### [00:03:03] Transcript Quality: Raw vs. Clean
Why treating transcripts as "clean structured truth" leads to low-quality derivative content. Maya recommends maintaining both a raw transcript (for traceability) and a cleaned transcript (for summarization and repurposing).

> "A transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth."

### [00:04:15] A Lightweight Pipeline for Small Teams
Key components for teams of 2–3 people:
- One folder/workspace per episode
- Clear naming conventions
- A checklist with defined handoff points
- A fixed output bundle (final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapters, thumbnail, publish log)

### [00:05:49] Automation: Acceleration vs. Abdication
What teams typically overestimate:
- **Editorial judgment** — Agents can propose titles but don't know your audience positioning
- **Exception handling** — Edge cases (name corrections, legal review, sensitive edits) break fully automated systems

Healthy mindset: Automate drafts, not approvals. Use automation for repetitive transforms (cleanup, first drafts, formatting) while humans retain responsibility for what goes live.

### [00:06:52] Titles: Strategy Over Frequency
Why the most repeated phrase in a conversation is rarely the best title. Maya advises generating candidates from multiple angles—problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, curiosity-driven—then comparing against your audience promise.

> "A title is not just a label. It's a contract with the listener."

### [00:08:13] The Pre-Publish Review Checklist
Five highest-value checks before going live:
1. Names and affiliations
2. Links and references (verify every "we'll link that")
3. Sponsor compliance
4. Sensitive edits (confirm guest-requested removals)
5. Metadata consistency across all surfaces

### [00:09:13] Practical Takeaways: Improve Your Pipeline This Month
Five steps for immediate improvement:
1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. Create a publish checklist with a single owner
3. Separate raw and cleaned transcripts
4. Define the required output bundle for every episode
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to track delays and errors

> "You don't need an elaborate system to get meaningful gains. You need consistency."
