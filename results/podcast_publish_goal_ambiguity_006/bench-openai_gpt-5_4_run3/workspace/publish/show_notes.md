# Show Notes

## [00:00:00] Why the pipeline shapes the listener experience
- The episode opens by reframing podcast production as part of the product, not just backstage operations.
- Maya Chen explains that titles, transcripts, chapter markers, and release discipline directly affect discoverability, navigation, and perceived quality.

## [00:01:20] The five stages of a podcast production pipeline
- Maya breaks the workflow into five stages: capture, post-production, content extraction, review and approval, and distribution.
- The conversation compares the pipeline to a software build flow, with source material, transformations, validation, packaging, and deployment.

## [00:02:29] Where small teams break first
- Early failures usually happen at handoffs, inconsistent naming, and weak asset storage practices.
- Maya warns that transcript-driven work looks easy but still requires judgment, because a transcript is not automatically publish-ready copy.

## [00:03:34] What transcripts are good for — and what still needs review
- Clean transcripts are useful for first drafts of summaries, chapter suggestions, quote pulls, and social options.
- Facts, links, names, affiliations, dates, sponsor language, and other authoritative details still need human review.
- Key idea: the transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth.

## [00:04:26] The minimum reliable output bundle
- For small teams, Maya recommends one workspace per episode, a clear naming convention, defined handoffs, and a fixed output bundle.
- That bundle should include final audio, a clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail if used, and a publish log.

## [00:04:54] Raw transcript vs. cleaned transcript
- The raw transcript should stay available for traceability and wording checks.
- A cleaned transcript should remove obvious disfluencies, fix speaker labels, and become the preferred source for summarization and repurposing.
- Separating the two reduces noise in downstream tools and teammate workflows.

## [00:05:49] What automation can and cannot own
- Maya says teams often overestimate automation in editorial judgment and exception handling.
- Automation is best used for repetitive transforms such as cleanup, first-draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, asset packaging, and metadata prefills.
- Human ownership is still required for correctness, tone, and release quality.

## [00:06:52] Better title development
- Title generation should produce several framing options: problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, and curiosity-driven.
- The strongest title is the one that matches the audience promise, not the most repeated phrase in the transcript.
- Suggested framing examples include:
  - How Small Teams Build a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline
  - Why Podcast Production Breaks Even When the Recording Is Great
  - From Raw Recording to Publish: The Systems Behind a Great Podcast

## [00:08:13] The highest-value review checks before release
- Maya prioritizes five checks before publication:
  1. Names and affiliations
  2. Links and references
  3. Sponsor compliance
  4. Sensitive edits
  5. Metadata consistency across title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy

## [00:09:13] The practical upgrade plan for a small show
- Start with a standard folder structure and naming convention.
- Give one person ownership of the publish checklist.
- Separate raw and cleaned transcripts.
- Define the required output bundle for every episode.
- Keep a lightweight episode log to spot recurring delays, confusion, and mistakes.
