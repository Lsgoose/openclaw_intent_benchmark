# Show Notes — Ep. 14: The Podcast Production Pipeline (with Maya Chen)

## [00:00:00] Introduction
Host introduces the episode topic: how a podcast goes from raw recording to a polished, distributed episode. Guest Maya Chen, who advises media teams and creator businesses on production systems, joins the conversation.

## [00:01:20] The Five Stages of a Podcast Production Pipeline
Maya breaks down the pipeline into five stages:
1. **Capture** — recording the conversation and collecting companion materials (guest bios, sponsor copy, links, references)
2. **Post-production** — cleaning audio, trimming mistakes, deciding what stays
3. **Content extraction** — transcript generation, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, clips
4. **Review and approval** — fact-checking, sponsor language, names, links, editorial and legal review
5. **Distribution** — publishing to hosting platform, newsletter, social channels, and archives

> "The pipeline is the product." — Maya Chen

## [00:02:29] Where Small Teams Struggle First
Three common pain points for small teams:
- **Handoffs** — no single source of truth when multiple people own different stages
- **Inconsistent naming and asset storage** — version confusion ("final_v2_real_final.wav")
- **Transcript-driven tasks** — raw transcripts are not automatically publish-ready copy

## [00:03:03] Transcripts: Rich Input, Not Final Truth
Raw transcripts contain filler, false starts, unverified links, and statements later corrected. Treating a transcript as clean structured truth leads to low-quality or misleading derivative content.

> "The transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth." — Maya Chen

**Good transcript uses:** first drafts of summaries, chapter suggestions, quote candidates, social post options, searchability, repurposing.

**Still requires human review:** facts, links, sponsor language, names, affiliations, dates, and any authoritative claims.

## [00:04:15] Lightweight Pipeline for Small Teams
Recommended structure for a two- or three-person team:
- One folder/workspace per episode
- Clear naming convention
- Checklist with defined handoff points
- Fixed **output bundle** for every episode: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, publish log

## [00:04:54] Raw vs. Clean Transcript
Keep both:
- **Raw transcript** — for traceability and internal reference
- **Clean transcript** — removes disfluencies, fixes speaker labels, formatted for readability; preferred source for summarization and repurposing

Separating them prevents downstream tools and teammates from inheriting noise from the source.

## [00:05:49] Automation: Where Teams Overestimate It
Two areas where automation is commonly overestimated:
1. **Editorial judgment** — agents can propose titles but don't know audience positioning unless it's encoded
2. **Exception handling** — edge cases (name corrections, legal concerns, sensitive anecdotes, sponsor constraints) are where fully automated systems fail

**Healthy mindset:** Use automation to accelerate repetitive transforms (transcription cleanup, first-draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, metadata prefill). Don't give up responsibility for what goes live.

## [00:06:52] Titles: A Contract with the Listener
Generate several title candidates from different angles:
- Problem-oriented
- Guest-oriented
- Outcome-oriented
- Curiosity-driven

Example candidates for this episode:
- "How Small Teams Build a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline"
- "Why Podcast Production Breaks Even When the Recording Is Great"
- "From Raw Recording to Publish: The Systems Behind a Great Podcast"

> "A title is not just a label. It's a contract with the listener." — Maya Chen

Frequency of a phrase in the transcript is not a strategy — the best title comes from understanding what the episode is helping the listener do.

## [00:08:13] Pre-Publish Review: Five High-Value Checks
1. **Names and affiliations** — verify guest name, title, and company
2. **Links and references** — confirm actual destinations for anything promised in the show
3. **Sponsor compliance** — required sponsor language is present where it should be
4. **Sensitive edits** — anything the guest wanted removed is actually removed
5. **Metadata consistency** — title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy all describe the same episode

## [00:09:13] Practical Takeaways for Small Shows
Steps to improve your pipeline this month:
1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. Create a publish checklist that one person owns
3. Separate raw transcript and cleaned transcript
4. Define the required output bundle for every episode
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to track delays, confusion, and repeated errors

Host addition: if experimenting with automation, start by automating drafts — not approvals.

> "Make the machine produce options. Make the human approve what becomes public." — Maya Chen
