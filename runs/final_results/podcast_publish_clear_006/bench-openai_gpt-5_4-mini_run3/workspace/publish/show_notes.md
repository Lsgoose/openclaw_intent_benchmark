# Episode 14 Show Notes

[00:00:00] Welcome and framing: the podcast production pipeline shapes the listener experience from raw recording to publish-ready episode.

[00:01:20] Major stages of the pipeline: capture, post-production, content extraction, review and approval, and distribution.

[00:02:29] Small-team pain points: handoffs, inconsistent naming/storage, and the judgment required to turn a transcript into publish-ready content.

[00:03:09] Transcript caution: raw transcripts can contain filler, corrections, and unverified references, so they are not final source-of-truth copy.

[00:03:40] What transcripts are best for: first drafts of summaries, chapter ideas, quote candidates, and social options; facts, names, links, and sponsor language still need human review.

[00:04:26] Lightweight reliable pipeline: one episode workspace, clear naming, a checklist with handoffs, and a fixed output bundle.

[00:04:59] Raw vs. cleaned transcript: keep both, use the cleaned transcript for repurposing, and retain the raw transcript for traceability and verification.

[00:05:57] Automation limits: agents help with routine transforms, but editorial judgment and exception handling still need human ownership.

[00:06:57] Title strategy: generate multiple title candidates from different angles and choose the one that best matches the listener promise.

[00:08:20] Highest-value release checks: names and affiliations, links and references, sponsor compliance, sensitive edits, and metadata consistency.

[00:09:23] Practical takeaway: standardize the episode folder, assign checklist ownership, separate raw and cleaned transcripts, define the output bundle, and keep a lightweight episode log.

[00:10:05] Sponsor note: SoundLayer supports collaboration on transcripts, asset versions, and review workflows.
