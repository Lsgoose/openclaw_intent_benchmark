## Show Notes

### [00:00] Introduction: The Pipeline Is the Product
Maya Chen joins the show to discuss how podcast production pipelines shape listener experience. The conversation covers why production discipline matters even for small teams—vague titles reduce clicks, sloppy transcripts degrade downstream content, and inconsistent checklists lead to compounding mistakes.

### [01:20] The Five Stages of Production
Maya outlines a five-stage framework:
1. **Capture** — Recording plus companion materials (bios, sponsor copy, links)
2. **Post-production** — Audio cleanup, trimming, editorial decisions
3. **Content extraction** — Transcripts, summaries, quotes, chapter markers, titles, clips
4. **Review and approval** — Fact-checking, sponsor compliance, legal review
5. **Distribution** — Publishing to hosting, newsletters, social channels, and archives

### [02:29] Where Small Teams Struggle
The first pain points are handoffs without a single source of truth, inconsistent file naming ("final_v2_real_final.wav"), and the assumption that raw transcripts equal publish-ready content.

### [03:34] Transcripts as Input, Not Truth
A raw transcript contains filler, false starts, jokes that don't read well, and unverified references like "we'll put the link in the show notes." Maya's guideline: transcripts are excellent for first drafts of summaries, chapters, and quotes, but authoritative claims, facts, links, names, and sponsor language require human review. *"The transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth."*

### [04:26] Lightweight Pipeline for Small Teams
For teams of 2–3 people: one folder per episode, clear naming conventions, defined handoff checkpoints, and a standardized output bundle (final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapters, thumbnail, publish log).

### [04:54] Raw vs. Clean Transcripts
Maintain both: raw for traceability and verification, cleaned for summarization and repurposing. Without this separation, downstream automation inherits every source mess—leading to awkward summaries and misleading snippets.

### [05:49] Automation: Acceleration, Not Abdication
Teams overestimate automation in two areas: editorial judgment (agents don't know your audience positioning) and exception handling (legal concerns, sensitive anecdotes, sponsor constraints). Healthy mindset: automate repetitive transforms (cleanup, first drafts, formatting) but keep humans responsible for what goes live.

### [06:52] Writing Good Titles
Generate candidates from multiple angles: problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, curiosity-driven. Compare against your audience promise—a title is a contract with the listener. Frequency is not strategy; the most repeated phrase is rarely the most compelling framing.

### [08:13] Pre-Publish Review Checklist
Five high-value checks before going live:
1. Names and affiliations
2. Links and references (verify every "we'll link that")
3. Sponsor compliance
4. Sensitive edits (confirm requested removals)
5. Metadata consistency across all surfaces

### [09:13] Action Items: Improve Your Pipeline This Month
1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. Create a publish checklist with a single owner
3. Separate raw and cleaned transcripts
4. Define the required output bundle for every episode
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to identify delays and repeated errors

Start by automating drafts, not approvals. Make the machine produce options; make the human approve what becomes public.
