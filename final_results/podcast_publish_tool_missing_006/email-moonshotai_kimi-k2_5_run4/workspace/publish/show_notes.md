# Building a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline

Guest: Maya Chen  
[Guest Bio](https://creators.example.com/guests/maya-chen)

---

## Show Notes

### [00:00] Introduction: The Pipeline Is the Product

Maya Chen joins the show to discuss podcast production pipelines—how a show goes from raw recording to polished episode. The opening establishes why production discipline matters: vague titles reduce clicks, sloppy transcripts hurt downstream content, inconsistent checklists lead to stacking mistakes.

### [01:27] The Five Stages of Production

Maya breaks the pipeline into five stages:
1. **Capture** – Recording + companion materials (bios, sponsor copy, links)
2. **Post-production** – Audio cleanup, trimming, editorial decisions
3. **Content extraction** – Transcripts, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapters, titles, clips
4. **Review and approval** – Fact-checking, sponsor language, names, legal review
5. **Distribution** – Publishing to hosting, newsletter, social, archives

### [02:29] Where Small Teams Struggle

Three common pain points:
- **Handoffs** – No single source of truth when multiple people touch the episode
- **Inconsistent naming/storage** – Files like "final_v2_real_final.wav" create confusion
- **Transcript-driven tasks** – Raw transcripts require judgment; they are not publish-ready copy

### [03:03] Transcript Quality: Raw vs. Clean

Transcripts contain filler, false starts, jokes that don't read well, and unverified references like "we'll put the link in the show notes." Maya recommends maintaining both:
- **Raw transcript** – For traceability and internal reference
- **Cleaned transcript** – Disfluencies removed, speaker labels fixed, formatted for readability

> "A transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth." — Maya Chen

### [04:26] A Lightweight Pipeline for Small Teams

For teams of 2–3 people:
- One folder/workspace per episode
- Clear naming convention
- Checklist with defined handoff points
- Fixed output bundle for every episode: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, publish log

### [05:49] Automation: What Works and What Doesn't

Two areas where teams overestimate automation:
- **Editorial judgment** – Agents can propose titles but don't know audience positioning
- **Exception handling** – Edge cases (name corrections, legal review, sensitive edits) break fully automated systems

Healthy mindset: use automation for acceleration, not abdication. Automate drafts; humans approve what goes live.

### [06:52] Writing Better Titles

Generate candidates from multiple angles:
- Problem-oriented
- Guest-oriented
- Outcome-oriented
- Curiosity-driven

Compare against audience promise. A title is a contract with the listener—not just a label.

> "Frequency is not strategy. The most repeated phrase is often not the most compelling framing." — Maya Chen

### [08:13] The Pre-Publish Review Checklist

Five high-value checks:
1. Names and affiliations
2. Links and references (verify actual destinations)
3. Sponsor compliance
4. Sensitive edits (confirm requested removals)
5. Metadata consistency (title, description, transcript, chapters, social copy all aligned)

### [09:13] Five Things to Do This Month

1. Define standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. Create a publish checklist with a clear owner
3. Separate raw and cleaned transcripts
4. Define required output bundle for every episode
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to track delays and repeated errors

> Bonus tip: If experimenting with automation, start by automating drafts—not approvals.
