# Episode 14 — The Podcast Production Pipeline

**Guest:** Maya Chen — media systems advisor for creator businesses

---

## [00:00:00] Introduction — The Pipeline Is the Product

The host introduces the episode's topic: the podcast production pipeline — everything that happens between a raw recording and a polished, published episode. Maya Chen joins the show and frames the core idea: the pipeline *is* the product, not just the conversation itself.

## [00:01:20] The Five Stages of a Production Pipeline

Maya breaks the pipeline into five stages:

1. **Capture** — recording and collecting companion materials (guest bios, sponsor copy, links, references)
2. **Post-production** — cleaning audio, trimming mistakes, deciding what stays
3. **Content extraction** — transcripts, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, clips
4. **Review and approval** — fact-checking, sponsor language, names, links, editorial/legal review
5. **Distribution** — publishing to hosting platforms, newsletters, social channels, and archives

The software build pipeline analogy is introduced: source material → transformations → validation → packaging → deployment.

## [00:02:29] Where Small Teams Struggle First

Three common pain points:

- **Handoffs** — no single source of truth between the person who records, the person who edits, and the person who writes descriptions
- **Inconsistent naming and asset storage** — files like "final_v2_real_final.wav"
- **Transcript-driven tasks** — raw transcripts are not automatically publish-ready

## [00:03:34] Transcript as Rich Input, Not Final Truth

A transcript contains filler, false starts, jokes that don't read well, corrected statements, and unverified links. Maya's key line: *"The transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth."* Transcripts are excellent for first drafts of summaries, chapters, quotes, and social posts — but anything authoritative (facts, links, names, dates, claims) still needs human review.

## [00:04:15] A Lightweight Pipeline for Small Teams

For teams of two or three:

- One folder or workspace per episode
- Clear naming convention
- Checklist with defined handoff points
- Fixed output bundle: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, publish log

**Raw vs. clean transcript:** Keep both. The raw transcript is for traceability; the cleaned transcript (disfluencies removed, speaker labels fixed, readable formatting) becomes the source for summarization and repurposing.

## [00:05:49] Where Automation Gets Overestimated

Teams overestimate automation in two areas:

1. **Editorial judgment** — an agent can propose titles but doesn't know audience positioning without explicit encoding
2. **Exception handling** — guest name corrections, legal concerns, sensitive anecdotes, sponsor constraints

The healthy mindset: *use automation for acceleration, not abdication.* Automate repetitive transforms (transcription cleanup, first-draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, metadata prefill) but keep human ownership of what goes live.

## [00:06:52] Titles — A Contract with the Listener

Generate title candidates from different angles: problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, curiosity-driven. Compare them against your audience promise. *"Frequency is not strategy"* — the most repeated phrase in a conversation is rarely the most compelling framing.

## [00:08:13] Pre-Publish Review Checklist

Five highest-value checks:

1. Names and affiliations
2. Links and references
3. Sponsor compliance
4. Sensitive edits (confirm removals)
5. Metadata consistency (title, description, transcript, chapters, and social copy should all describe the same episode)

## [00:09:13] Practical Takeaways

Five steps to improve your pipeline this month:

1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. Create a publish checklist that one person owns
3. Separate raw transcript and cleaned transcript
4. Define the required output bundle for every episode
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to track delays and repeated errors

Bonus: *Automate drafts, not approvals.*
