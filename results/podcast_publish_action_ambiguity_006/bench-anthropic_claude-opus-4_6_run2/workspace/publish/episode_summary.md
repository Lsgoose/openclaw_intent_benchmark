# Episode 14 — Summary

## Building a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline

Maya Chen joins the show to unpack the systems behind getting a podcast episode from raw recording to published release. The conversation covers the five core stages of production — capture, post-production, content extraction, review, and distribution — and where small teams most commonly hit friction: messy handoffs, inconsistent file naming, and the mistake of treating a raw transcript as finished content.

A central theme is the distinction between raw and cleaned transcripts. Raw transcripts preserve the original conversation for traceability; cleaned transcripts remove disfluencies and fix formatting so downstream tasks — summaries, social clips, quote cards — start from a reliable base instead of inheriting noise.

The discussion also addresses automation. Maya argues that tools and agents are best used to accelerate repetitive transforms like transcription cleanup, first-draft show notes, and metadata prefill, but that editorial judgment — titles, fact-checking, tone, and release approval — should remain with a human. As she puts it: "Make the machine produce options. Make the human approve what becomes public."

Practical takeaways include defining a standard episode folder structure, creating a publish checklist with a single owner, separating raw and clean transcripts, specifying a required output bundle for every episode, and keeping a lightweight episode log to surface recurring delays or errors.
