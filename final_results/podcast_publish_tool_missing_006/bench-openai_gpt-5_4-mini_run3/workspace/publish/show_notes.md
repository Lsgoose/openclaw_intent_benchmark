# Episode 14 Show Notes

## 00:00 — Why the pipeline matters
The episode opens by framing the podcast production pipeline as the system that shapes the listener experience, from raw recording through editing, packaging, and distribution. The point: the pipeline is not back-office overhead; it is part of the product.

## 01:20 — The five stages of production
Maya Chen breaks the workflow into five stages:
1. Capture: record the conversation and collect supporting materials.
2. Post-production: clean audio, trim mistakes, decide what stays.
3. Content extraction: transcript, summary, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, and clips.
4. Review and approval: check facts, sponsor language, names, links, and legal/editorial issues.
5. Distribution: publish the final package across hosting, newsletter, social channels, and archives.

## 02:29 — Where small teams struggle
The first failures usually show up in handoffs, naming, and storage. Without a single source of truth, people lose track of versions and ownership. Transcript-driven work also looks simpler than it is, because a transcript is not automatically publish-ready copy.

## 03:09 — Why a transcript is not final truth
Raw transcripts can include filler, false starts, corrections, and unverified placeholders like “we’ll put the link in the show notes.” They are excellent inputs, but they still need human review for authoritative details such as facts, links, sponsor language, names, affiliations, dates, and claims.

## 04:26 — A lightweight but reliable system
For small teams, the recommended structure is simple: one folder per episode, clear naming conventions, a checklist with explicit handoff points, and a fixed output bundle. Every episode should end with the same core assets: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail if used, and a publish log.

## 04:59 — Clean transcript vs raw transcript
The episode strongly recommends keeping both versions. The raw transcript preserves traceability and helps verify wording. The cleaned transcript removes obvious disfluencies, fixes speaker labels, and becomes the preferred source for summarization and repurposing.

## 05:57 — Where automation helps, and where it fails
Automation is useful for repetitive transforms like transcription cleanup, first-draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, asset packaging, and metadata prefill. It is much weaker at editorial judgment and exception handling, which is where media teams routinely get burned.

## 06:57 — Titles are contracts
Titles should be explored from multiple angles: problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, and curiosity-driven. The best title is not the most repeated phrase; it is the strongest promise to the listener.

## 08:13 — Highest-value review checks
Before publishing, the team should verify five things: names and affiliations, links and references, sponsor compliance, sensitive edits, and metadata consistency across the title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy.

## 09:13 — Practical takeaways
The closing advice is to define a standard folder structure and naming convention, assign one person to own the publish checklist, separate raw and cleaned transcripts, define the required output bundle, and keep a lightweight episode log to track delays and repeated mistakes.
