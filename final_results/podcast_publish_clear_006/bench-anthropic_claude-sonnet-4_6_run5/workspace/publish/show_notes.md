### [00:00:27] Meet the Guest
Maya Chen joins to discuss production systems for media teams and creator businesses. Key framing: *"The pipeline is the product."*

### [00:01:20] The Five Stages of a Podcast Production Pipeline
Maya breaks down the five core stages:

1. **Capture** – Record the conversation; collect companion materials (guest bios, sponsor copy, links, references)
2. **Post-production** – Clean audio, trim mistakes, decide what stays
3. **Content extraction** – Transcript generation, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, clips
4. **Review and approval** – Fact-check, verify sponsor language, names, links, and editorial/legal concerns
5. **Distribution** – Push the final package to hosting platform, newsletter, social channels, and archives

### [00:02:29] Where Small Teams Struggle First
Three common pain points:
- **Handoffs** – No single source of truth when multiple people own different stages
- **Inconsistent naming and asset storage** – Version confusion ("final_v2_real_final.wav")
- **Transcript-driven tasks** – Raw transcripts are not automatically publish-ready copy

### [00:03:03] Transcripts: Rich Input, Not Final Truth
A raw transcript can contain filler, false starts, jokes that don't read well, corrected statements, and unverified links. Treating it as clean structured truth leads to low-quality or misleading derivative content.

> *"The transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth."* — Maya Chen

### [00:04:15] Lightweight Pipeline for Small Teams (2–3 People)
Practical recommendations:
- One folder or workspace per episode
- Clear naming convention
- Checklist with defined handoff points
- **Standard output bundle** for every episode: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, publish log

### [00:04:54] Raw vs. Clean Transcript
Keep **both**:
- **Raw transcript** – for traceability and internal reference
- **Clean transcript** – removes disfluencies, fixes speaker labels, formatted for readability; becomes the preferred source for summarization and repurposing

### [00:05:49] Automation: Where Teams Overestimate It
Two areas where automation falls short:
1. **Editorial judgment** – Agents can propose titles but don't know audience positioning unless it's encoded
2. **Exception handling** – Edge cases (name corrections, legal concerns, sensitive anecdotes, sponsor constraints) require human judgment

Healthy mindset: *"Use automation to accelerate repetitive transforms. Don't give up responsibility for what goes live."*

### [00:08:13] Pre-Publish Review: Five High-Value Checks
1. **Names and affiliations** – Verify guest name, title, company
2. **Links and references** – Confirm actual destinations for anything mentioned
3. **Sponsor compliance** – Required language present in the right places
4. **Sensitive edits** – Confirm removals the guest requested are actually gone
5. **Metadata consistency** – Title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy all describe the same episode

### [00:09:13] Practical Takeaways for Small Shows
Steps to improve your pipeline this month:
1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. Create a publish checklist with a single owner
3. Separate raw and clean transcripts
4. Define the required output bundle for every episode
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to surface delays and repeated errors

**Bonus:** If experimenting with automation, start by automating *drafts*, not *approvals*. Make the machine produce options; make the human approve what becomes public.
