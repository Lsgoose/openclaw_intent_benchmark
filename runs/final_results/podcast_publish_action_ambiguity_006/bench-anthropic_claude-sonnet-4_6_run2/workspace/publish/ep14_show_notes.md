# Episode 14 — Building a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline

**Guest:** Maya Chen, media systems advisor  
**Runtime:** ~10 min  
**Published:** 2026-04-20

---

## Episode Summary

Most podcast teams think the product is the conversation. Maya Chen argues the pipeline *is* the product — and when it breaks, listeners feel it immediately.

In this episode, Maya walks through the five stages of a reliable podcast production pipeline: capture, post-production, content extraction, review and approval, and distribution. She explains why handoff failures and inconsistent asset storage are the first places small teams lose control, why a raw transcript is a rich input but not editorial truth, and where automation genuinely helps versus where human judgment is irreplaceable.

The episode closes with a practical five-step checklist any small show can act on this month.

---

## Key Takeaways

- **The pipeline is the product.** Clean audio alone doesn't make a polished show — titles, transcripts, chapter markers, metadata, and social copy all have to align.
- **Five stages:** Capture → Post-production → Content extraction → Review & approval → Distribution.
- **Separate raw from clean.** Keep the raw transcript for traceability; produce a cleaned version as the preferred source for summaries and repurposing.
- **Transcripts are inputs, not truth.** Filler, false starts, unverified links, and corrected statements all live in raw transcripts. Review before publishing derivative content.
- **Automate drafts, not approvals.** Use automation to accelerate repetitive transforms; keep a human responsible for what goes live.
- **Titles are contracts.** Generate candidates from multiple angles (problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, curiosity-driven) and choose based on audience intent — not keyword frequency.
- **Five pre-publish checks:** names/affiliations, links/references, sponsor compliance, sensitive edits, metadata consistency.

## Practical Checklist (from the episode)

1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. Create a publish checklist that one person owns
3. Separate raw transcript and cleaned transcript
4. Define the required output bundle for every episode
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to surface delays and repeated errors
