## [00:00:00] Introduction: The Pipeline Is the Product

Host opens with the central theme: podcast production pipelines shape the entire listener experience. The discussion covers how a show goes from raw recording to polished episode—including clean audio, transcripts, chapter markers, social clips, titles, descriptions, and metadata.

> "If the title is vague, people don't click. If the transcript is sloppy, your downstream content generation gets worse. If the chapter markers are off, listeners can't navigate." — Maya Chen

## [00:01:20] The Five Stages of Production

Maya breaks the pipeline into five distinct stages:
1. **Capture** – Recording and collecting companion materials (bios, sponsor copy, links, references)
2. **Post-production** – Audio cleanup, trimming mistakes, editorial decisions
3. **Content extraction** – Transcripts, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, clips
4. **Review and approval** – Fact-checking, sponsor language verification, legal review
5. **Distribution** – Publishing to hosting platform, newsletter, social channels, and archives

> "It's almost like a software build pipeline... source material, transformations, validation steps, packaging, and deployment." — Host

## [00:02:29] Where Small Teams Struggle

Three common pain points for small teams:
- **Handoffs** – No single source of truth when multiple people touch the episode
- **Inconsistent naming** – Files like "final_v2_real_final.wav" create confusion about source of record
- **Transcript assumptions** – Raw transcripts are not automatically publish-ready copy

## [00:03:03] Raw vs. Clean Transcripts

The critical distinction between raw and cleaned transcripts:
- **Raw transcript** – Keep for traceability and internal reference
- **Clean transcript** – Remove disfluencies, fix speaker labels, format for readability; becomes the preferred source for summarization and repurposing

> "The transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth." — Maya Chen

## [00:04:15] Building a Lightweight Pipeline

For teams of 2-3 people: one folder per episode, clear naming conventions, checklists with defined handoff points, and a fixed output bundle (final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapters, thumbnail, publish log).

## [00:05:49] Automation: Acceleration, Not Abdication

Where teams overestimate automation:
- **Editorial judgment** – Agents can propose titles but don't know your audience positioning
- **Exception handling** – Most episodes are routine, but edge cases (name corrections, legal review, sensitive edits) require human judgment

> "Use automation for acceleration, not abdication... Make the machine produce options. Make the human approve what becomes public." — Maya Chen

## [00:06:52] Titles as Audience Contracts

Maya recommends generating multiple title candidates from different angles:
- Problem-oriented
- Guest-oriented
- Outcome-oriented
- Curiosity-driven

> "A title is not just a label. It's a contract with the listener." — Maya Chen

## [00:08:13] The Pre-Release Checklist

Five highest-value checks before going live:
1. Names and affiliations – Verify guest details
2. Links and references – Confirm every "we'll link that" promise
3. Sponsor compliance – Check required language is present
4. Sensitive edits – Confirm requested removals are complete
5. Metadata consistency – Title, description, transcript, chapters, and social copy should align

## [00:09:13] Practical Takeaways for This Month

Five steps to improve your pipeline immediately:
1. Define standard folder structure and naming convention
2. Create a publish checklist with a single owner
3. Separate raw and cleaned transcripts
4. Define required output bundle for every episode
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to track delays and errors
