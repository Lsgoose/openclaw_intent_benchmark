# Episode 14 Show Notes

## [00:00:00] Why the production pipeline shapes the listener experience
The episode opens by reframing podcast production as more than back-office operations. A reliable pipeline determines how a raw recording becomes a polished release with clean audio, a transcript, chapter markers, social clips, a title, a description, and consistent metadata.

## [00:01:20] The five stages of a reliable podcast production pipeline
Maya Chen breaks the workflow into five stages:
- Capture: recording plus companion materials such as guest bios, sponsor copy, links, and references
- Post-production: audio cleanup, trimming, and edit decisions
- Content extraction: transcripts, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter suggestions, titles, and clips
- Review and approval: fact checks, sponsor language, names, links, and editorial risk review
- Distribution: publishing to the hosting platform, newsletter, social channels, and archives

## [00:02:29] Where small teams usually break first
The first failure points are handoffs, inconsistent naming, and scattered assets. When teams lack a single source of truth, version confusion grows fast and transcript-driven publishing work becomes fragile.

## [00:03:03] Why a transcript is not the final editorial truth
A raw transcript can include filler, false starts, jokes that do not read well, corrected statements, and unverified references. Maya’s core principle: the transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth.

## [00:04:15] The lightweight pipeline for a two- or three-person team
A practical setup for a small team includes:
- One folder or workspace per episode
- A clear naming convention
- A checklist with defined handoff points
- A fixed output bundle for every release

The recommended output bundle includes final audio, a clean transcript, show notes, a title, a description, a chapter list, a thumbnail if used, and a publish log.

## [00:04:54] Raw transcript vs. clean transcript
Maya recommends keeping both:
- Raw transcript for traceability and internal reference
- Clean transcript for readable summaries, repurposing, and downstream production work

Separating the two reduces noise before another person or tool builds on the material.

## [00:05:49] What automation can do well and where it still fails
Automation is useful for repetitive transforms such as transcription cleanup, first-draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, asset packaging, and metadata prefills. It is much weaker at editorial judgment and exception handling, especially when guest corrections, legal review, sensitive anecdotes, or sponsor constraints appear.

## [00:06:52] How to think about episode titles
The discussion recommends generating multiple title candidates from different angles, including problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, and curiosity-driven framing. A title is treated as a contract with the listener, not just a label.

## [00:08:13] The highest-value pre-publish checks
Before an episode goes live, Maya prioritizes five checks:
- Names and affiliations
- Links and references
- Sponsor compliance
- Sensitive edits requested by the guest
- Metadata consistency across title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy

## [00:09:13] The practical improvement plan for small shows
For teams upgrading their process this month, the recommendations are:
1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. Create a publish checklist with a clear owner
3. Separate raw and cleaned transcripts
4. Define the required output bundle for every episode
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to spot delays, confusion, and repeated errors
