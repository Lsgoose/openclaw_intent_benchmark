# Show Notes

## 00:00:00 — Why the pipeline is the product
Today’s episode frames podcast production as more than the conversation itself: the pipeline shapes discovery, quality, and listener experience.

## 00:01:20 — The five stages of production
The discussion breaks the workflow into five stages: capture, post-production, content extraction, review and approval, and distribution.

## 00:02:29 — Handoffs, naming, and transcript judgment
Small teams often struggle with unclear ownership, inconsistent file naming, and the false assumption that a raw transcript is publish-ready.

## 00:03:34 — Transcript as input, not truth
A clean transcript is a strong source for summaries, chapters, and quote pulls, but facts, links, sponsor language, names, dates, and claims still need human review.

## 00:04:26 — A lightweight output bundle
A reliable small-team pipeline should standardize the final bundle: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail if used, and a publish log.

## 00:04:59 — Raw transcript and cleaned transcript
Keep raw transcripts for traceability and cleaned transcripts for repurposing. Separating the two reduces noise and protects downstream outputs.

## 00:05:49 — Automation has limits
Automation can accelerate routine work like cleanup, formatting, and chapter suggestions, but editorial judgment and exception handling still require a human owner.

## 00:06:57 — Titles as contracts
Good titles are built from audience promise, not just repeated phrases. The episode recommends generating multiple title angles and choosing the one that best matches listener intent.

## 00:08:13 — Review checks that matter most
The highest-value prepublish checks are names and affiliations, links and references, sponsor compliance, sensitive edits, and metadata consistency.

## 00:09:13 — Practical improvements for this month
The episode closes with a simple plan: define folder structure, assign checklist ownership, separate raw from cleaned transcripts, standardize outputs, and keep a lightweight episode log.
