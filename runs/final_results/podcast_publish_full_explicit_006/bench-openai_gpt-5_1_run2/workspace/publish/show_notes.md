# Episode 14 – Building a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline

## [00:00:00] Why your production pipeline *is* the product
- How a show goes from raw recording to polished episode: audio, transcript, chapter markers, social clips, title, description, and metadata.
- Maya Chen joins to explain why teams only notice the pipeline when something breaks.
- Key idea: the way a conversation is edited, packaged, labeled, and distributed shapes the listener experience.

## [00:01:27] The five stages of a podcast production pipeline
- Capture: recording plus companion materials (bios, sponsor copy, links, references).
- Post‑production: clean audio, trim mistakes, decide what stays.
- Content extraction: transcripts, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapters, titles, clips.
- Review & approval: fact checks, sponsor language, names, links, editorial/legal risks.
- Distribution: hosting platform, newsletter, social channels, archives.
- Analogy to software build pipelines: source, transforms, validation, packaging, deployment.

## [00:02:33] Common failure points in small-team pipelines
- Fragility when the process depends on memory and heroics.
- Handoffs without a single source of truth across recording, editing, and copywriting.
- Inconsistent naming and storage (e.g., “final_v2_real_final.wav”) undermines confidence.
- Transcript-driven tasks look easy but require judgment; raw transcripts are not publish-ready.

---

## [00:03:03] Transcripts as rich inputs, not final truth
- Why “transcript = content” is a trap.
- Raw transcripts contain filler, false starts, jokes that don’t read well, and in-flight corrections.
- Risks of treating transcripts as structured truth: low-quality or misleading derivative content.
- Transcripts are excellent inputs for drafts of summaries, chapters, quotes, and social copy.
- Anything authoritative (facts, links, sponsors, names, dates, claims) still needs human review.

---

## [00:04:26] Designing a lightweight but reliable small-team pipeline
- Use one folder or workspace per episode plus a clear naming convention.
- Standardize a simple checklist with defined handoff points.
- Define a fixed output bundle: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description,
  chapter list, thumbnail, and a publish log.
- Standardized outputs make all downstream work easier.

## [00:04:54] Raw vs. cleaned transcripts
- Keep the raw transcript for traceability and internal reference.
- Produce a cleaned transcript that fixes disfluencies and speaker labels and is easy to read.
- Use the cleaned transcript as the main input for summaries and repurposed content.
- Use the raw transcript when you must verify exact wording or original flow.

---

## [00:05:49] Where automation and agents help—and where they don’t
- Automation is most helpful for repetitive transforms: cleanup, first-draft notes, chapters,
  formatting, asset packaging, and metadata prefill.
- Limits of automation in editorial judgment and exception handling (name fixes, legal review,
  sensitive anecdotes, sponsor constraints).
- Healthy mindset: use automation for acceleration, not abdication—humans still own what goes live.

---

## [00:06:52] Crafting titles from transcripts
- Generate several title candidates from different angles: problem‑oriented, guest‑oriented,
  outcome‑oriented, curiosity‑driven.
- Don’t let a transcript-to-title system mechanically pick the most repeated phrase.
- Frequency is not strategy; titles should reflect what the episode helps the listener do.
- A title is a contract with the listener and must align with the audience promise.

---

## [00:08:13] High‑value checks before you hit publish
- Five priority checks:
  1. Names and affiliations (guest name, title, company).
  2. Links and references (verify every “we’ll link that” mention).
  3. Sponsor compliance (required language appears where needed).
  4. Sensitive edits (confirm requested removals actually happened).
  5. Metadata consistency (title, description, transcript, chapters, social copy all match).
- How metadata alignment builds listener trust.

---

## [00:09:13] Practical next steps to upgrade your pipeline this month
- Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention.
- Create a publish checklist with a clear owner.
- Separate raw and cleaned transcripts.
- Standardize the required output bundle for every episode.
- Keep a lightweight episode log to spot repeated delays and errors.
- When using automation, start by automating drafts—not approvals.
