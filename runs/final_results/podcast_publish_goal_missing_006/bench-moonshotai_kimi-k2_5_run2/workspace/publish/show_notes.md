## [00:00] Introduction: The Pipeline Is the Product

Maya Chen joins the show to discuss how podcast production pipelines shape listener experience. What happens between raw recording and published episode—editing, packaging, labeling, distribution—often goes unnoticed until something breaks.

> "This is one of those topics people don't always notice until something breaks, and then suddenly everyone realizes the pipeline is the product." — Maya Chen

---

## [00:47] The Five Stages of a Production Pipeline

Maya breaks podcast production into five distinct stages:

1. **Capture** — Recording the conversation plus companion materials (bios, sponsor copy, links, references)
2. **Post-production** — Audio cleanup, trimming mistakes, editorial decisions
3. **Content extraction** — Transcripts, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapters, titles, clips
4. **Review and approval** — Fact checks, sponsor language verification, legal/editorial review
5. **Distribution** — Publishing to hosting platform, newsletter, social channels, and archives

> "If the title is vague, people don't click. If the transcript is sloppy, your downstream content generation gets worse." — Maya Chen

---

## [02:29] Where Small Teams Struggle

Three common pain points for small teams:

- **Handoffs** — Multiple people touch the episode with no single source of truth
- **Inconsistent naming/storage** — Files become "final_v2_real_final.wav" with no confidence in the source of record
- **Transcript-driven tasks** — Raw transcripts require judgment; they're not automatically publish-ready

---

## [03:03] Transcripts: Rich Input, Not Final Truth

The transcript is a starting point, not the destination. Maya explains what requires human review:

**Good for first drafts:** Summaries, chapter suggestions, quote candidates, social options, searchability

**Requires verification:** Facts, links, sponsor language, names, affiliations, dates, claims

> "The transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth." — Maya Chen

---

## [04:15] Building a Lightweight Pipeline

For teams of 2–3 people, Maya recommends:

- One folder/workspace per episode
- Clear naming conventions
- A checklist with defined handoff points
- A fixed output bundle for every episode

**Standard output bundle:** Final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, publish log

> "If you standardize outputs, everything downstream gets easier." — Maya Chen

---

## [04:55] Raw vs. Clean Transcripts

Keep both versions:

- **Raw transcript** — For traceability, internal reference, and verifying original wording
- **Clean transcript** — Removes disfluencies, fixes speaker labels, readable formatting for summarization and repurposing

> "If you don't separate raw from cleaned material, your downstream system inherits every little mess from the source." — Maya Chen

---

## [05:50] Automation: Acceleration, Not Abdication

Two places teams overestimate automation:

1. **Editorial judgment** — Agents can propose titles but don't know your audience positioning
2. **Exception handling** — Edge cases (name corrections, legal reviews, sensitive anecdotes) break fully automated systems

> "Use automation for acceleration, not abdication." — Maya Chen

**Automate:** Repetitive transforms (transcription cleanup, first drafts, formatting, metadata prefill)

**Human owns:** What goes live—correctness, tone, release quality

---

## [06:52] Titles: Your Contract with the Listener

Maya's advice for title generation:

Generate candidates from different angles:
- Problem-oriented
- Guest-oriented  
- Outcome-oriented
- Curiosity-driven

Compare against your actual audience promise. A title is not just a label—it's a contract.

**Example approaches for a production workflow episode:**
- "How Small Teams Build a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline"
- "Why Podcast Production Breaks Even When the Recording Is Great"
- "From Raw Recording to Publish: The Systems Behind a Great Podcast"

> "Frequency is not strategy. The most repeated phrase in a conversation is often not the most compelling framing." — Maya Chen

---

## [08:13] The Pre-Publish Review Checklist

Five highest-value checks before going live:

1. **Names and affiliations** — Verify guest name, title, company
2. **Links and references** — If the host says "we'll link that," verify the destination
3. **Sponsor compliance** — Confirm required language is present
4. **Sensitive edits** — Confirm anything the guest wanted removed is actually removed
5. **Metadata consistency** — Title, description, transcript, chapters, and social copy should all describe the same episode

> "Listeners feel that inconsistency immediately. It makes the production feel unreliable." — Maya Chen

---

## [09:13] First Steps to Improve Your Pipeline

If you want to improve your pipeline this month:

1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. Create a publish checklist with one owner
3. Separate raw and cleaned transcripts
4. Define the required output bundle for every episode
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to track delays and repeated errors

> "You don't need an elaborate system to get meaningful gains. You need consistency." — Maya Chen

> "If you're experimenting with automation, start by automating drafts, not approvals." — Host
