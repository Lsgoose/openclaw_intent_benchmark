# Episode 14: The Podcast Production Pipeline

## Show Notes

### [00:00:00] Introduction
Welcome and episode framing: how a raw recording becomes a polished, published episode.

### [00:00:27] Guest Introduction
Maya Chen joins the show — advisor to media teams and creator businesses on production systems.

### [00:00:47] Why the Pipeline Is the Product
Discussion on how editing, packaging, labeling, and distribution shape the listener experience beyond the conversation itself.

### [00:01:20] The Five Stages of a Podcast Pipeline
1. **Capture** — recording plus companion materials (bios, sponsor copy, links, references)
2. **Post-production** — audio cleanup, trimming, editorial decisions
3. **Content extraction** — transcripts, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapters, titles, clips
4. **Review and approval** — fact-checking, sponsor language, names, links, legal concerns
5. **Distribution** — publishing to hosting, newsletter, social, and archives

### [00:02:29] Where Small Teams Struggle
Common pain points: unclear handoffs, inconsistent file naming, and treating raw transcripts as publish-ready content.

### [00:03:03] Transcripts Are Not Automatically Content
Why raw transcripts contain filler, false starts, unverified claims, and broken references — and why they need editorial review before derivative content is created.

### [00:03:40] What to Extract vs. What Needs Human Review
Transcripts are excellent for first-draft summaries, chapter suggestions, quote candidates, and social post options. Facts, links, names, dates, and claims still require human verification.

### [00:04:15] A Lightweight Pipeline for Small Teams
Recommended structure: one folder per episode, clear naming conventions, a checklist with defined handoff points, and a fixed output bundle (final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapters, thumbnail, publish log).

### [00:04:54] Raw vs. Clean Transcripts
Keep both. The raw transcript preserves traceability; the cleaned version becomes the preferred source for summarization and repurposing.

### [00:05:49] Where Automation Gets Overestimated
Two common traps: editorial judgment (agents can't know audience positioning unless it's encoded) and exception handling (guest corrections, legal reviews, sensitive content, sponsor constraints).

### [00:06:26] Automation for Acceleration, Not Abdication
Use automation for repetitive transforms — transcription cleanup, first-draft notes, formatting, metadata prefill. Keep humans responsible for what goes live.

### [00:06:52] The Art of Titles
Generate multiple title candidates from different angles (problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, curiosity-driven) and compare against audience promise. Frequency is not strategy.

### [00:08:13] The Five Highest-Value Pre-Publish Checks
1. Names and affiliations
2. Links and references
3. Sponsor compliance
4. Sensitive edits confirmed removed
5. Metadata consistency across title, description, transcript, chapters, and social copy

### [00:09:13] Practical Takeaways
- Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention
- Create a publish checklist with clear ownership
- Separate raw and cleaned transcripts
- Define the required output bundle for every episode
- Keep a lightweight episode log to track delays and repeated errors
- If experimenting with automation, start by automating drafts, not approvals
