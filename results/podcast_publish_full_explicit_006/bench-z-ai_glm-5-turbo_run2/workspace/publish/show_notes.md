## Timestamps

### [00:00:00] Introduction — Why the Pipeline Is the Product
The host frames the episode: a podcast's value isn't just the conversation — it's how that conversation gets edited, packaged, labeled, and distributed. Maya's opening thesis: "The pipeline is the product."

### [00:01:20] The Five Stages of a Podcast Production Pipeline
Maya breaks production into five stages:
1. **Capture** — recording plus companion materials (guest bios, sponsor copy, links)
2. **Post-production** — audio cleanup, trimming, editorial decisions
3. **Content extraction** — transcripts, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, clips
4. **Review and approval** — fact-checking, sponsor language, names, links, editorial/legal review
5. **Distribution** — hosting platform, newsletter, social channels, archives

### [00:02:07] The Software Build Analogy
Maya compares podcast pipelines to software build pipelines: source material, transformations, validation, packaging, deployment. Pipelines become fragile when they depend on memory and heroics.

### [00:02:29] Three Common Pain Points for Small Teams
1. **Handoffs** — no single source of truth between recorder, editor, and writer
2. **Inconsistent naming and storage** — files like "final_v2_real_final.wav" erode confidence
3. **Transcript-driven tasks require judgment** — a raw transcript is not publish-ready copy

### [00:03:03] The Transcript Trap
A transcript contains filler, false starts, jokes that don't read well, corrected statements, and unverified link promises. Treating it as clean structured truth leads to low-quality or misleading derivative content.

### [00:03:34] What Transcripts Are Good For (and What They Aren't)
**Good inputs for:** first-draft summaries, chapter suggestions, quote candidates, social post options, searchability, repurposing.
**Still needs human review:** facts, links, sponsor language, names, affiliations, dates, claims.
> "The transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth."

### [00:04:26] A Lightweight but Reliable Pipeline for Small Teams
Maya's recommendations:
- One folder or workspace per episode
- Clear naming convention
- Checklist with defined handoff points
- Fixed output bundle: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, publish log

### [00:04:54] Raw Transcript vs. Clean Transcript
Keep both. The raw transcript preserves traceability and original flow. The cleaned transcript removes disfluencies, fixes speaker labels, and formats for readability — becoming the preferred source for summarization and repurposing.

### [00:05:49] Where Teams Overestimate Automation
Two common overestimates:
1. **Editorial judgment** — agents can propose titles but don't know audience positioning unless encoded
2. **Exception handling** — fully automated systems fail on edge cases (name corrections, legal concerns, sensitive anecdotes, sponsor constraints)

### [00:06:26] Automation for Acceleration, Not Abdication
Automate repetitive transforms: transcription cleanup, first-draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, asset packaging, metadata prefill. A human still owns correctness, tone, and release quality.

### [00:06:52] Title Strategy
Generate candidates from different angles: problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, curiosity-driven. Compare against audience promise. A title is a contract with the listener, not just a label. Frequency is not strategy.

### [00:08:13] Five High-Value Pre-Publish Checks
1. Names and affiliations
2. Links and references
3. Sponsor compliance
4. Sensitive edits (guest removal requests)
5. Metadata consistency across title, description, transcript, chapters, and social copy

### [00:09:13] Five Practical Steps to Improve Your Pipeline This Month
1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. Create a publish checklist that one person owns
3. Separate raw transcript and cleaned transcript
4. Define the required output bundle for every episode
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to spot delays, confusion, and repeated errors

**Bonus from the host:** If experimenting with automation, start by automating drafts, not approvals. Make the machine produce options; make the human approve what becomes public.
