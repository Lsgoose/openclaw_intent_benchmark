## 00:00 — Opening: why the production pipeline shapes listener experience
The episode opens by framing the production pipeline as more than operations: it directly shapes how the audience experiences the show, from raw recording to final publish.

## 00:01:20 — The five-stage pipeline
Maya breaks the process into five stages:
- capture: recording plus companion materials like guest bios, sponsor copy, links, and references
- post-production: cleaning audio, trimming mistakes, deciding what stays
- content extraction: transcript, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, and clips
- review and approval: checking facts, sponsor language, names, links, and legal/editorial risk
- distribution: sending the final package to hosting, newsletter, social, and archives

## 00:02:29 — Common failure points in small teams
The discussion turns to handoff failures, inconsistent naming and storage, and the false assumption that a transcript is automatically publish-ready content.

## 00:03:09 — Why transcripts need judgment
A transcript can contain filler, corrections, and unverified references. Maya emphasizes that transcripts are rich inputs for drafts, but facts, links, sponsor language, names, affiliations, dates, and claims still need human review.

## 00:04:26 — A lightweight but reliable workflow for small teams
For small teams, the recommendation is a dedicated episode folder, a clear naming convention, a defined checklist, and a fixed output bundle: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail if used, and a publish log.

## 00:04:54 — Raw transcript vs. cleaned transcript
Keep both versions. The raw transcript preserves traceability and the original flow; the cleaned transcript removes disfluencies, fixes speaker labels, and becomes the preferred source for summarization and repurposing.

## 00:05:57 — Automation should accelerate, not replace judgment
Automation is useful for transcription cleanup, first-draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, asset packaging, and metadata prefill. But editorial judgment, exception handling, correctness, tone, and release quality still need a human owner.

## 00:06:52 — Titles as a contract with the listener
The episode recommends generating multiple title candidates from different angles — problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, and curiosity-driven — and choosing based on audience promise rather than raw phrase frequency.

## 00:08:13 — Review checks before publish
The highest-value checks are:
- names and affiliations
- links and references
- sponsor compliance
- sensitive edits
- metadata consistency across title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy

## 00:09:13 — Practical takeaways for this month
The episode closes with a simple action list: define a folder structure, create a publish checklist owner, separate raw and cleaned transcripts, define the required output bundle, and keep a lightweight episode log to spot recurring delays and errors.
