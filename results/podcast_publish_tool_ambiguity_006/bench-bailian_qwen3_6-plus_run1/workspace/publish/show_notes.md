# Episode 14 — Building a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline

**Guest:** Maya Chen — advisor to media teams and creator businesses on production systems.

---

## [00:00:00] Introduction

The host introduces the topic: the podcast production pipeline — the often-invisible process that turns a raw recording into a polished, discoverable episode. Maya Chen joins the show.

## [00:01:20] The Five Stages of a Production Pipeline

Maya breaks the pipeline into five stages:

1. **Capture** — recording the conversation and collecting companion materials (guest bios, sponsor copy, links, references).
2. **Post-production** — cleaning audio, trimming mistakes, deciding what stays.
3. **Content extraction** — generating transcripts, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, and clips.
4. **Review and approval** — fact-checking, verifying sponsor language, names, links, and editorial/legal concerns.
5. **Distribution** — publishing to hosting platforms, newsletters, social channels, and archives.

The pipeline is compared to a software build pipeline: source material, transformations, validation, packaging, and deployment.

## [00:02:29] Where Small Teams Struggle

Three common pain points:

- **Handoffs** — no single source of truth between the person who records, the person who edits, and the person who writes descriptions.
- **Inconsistent naming and asset storage** — files like "final_v2_real_final.wav" create confusion about the source of record.
- **Transcript-driven tasks** — raw transcripts are not automatically publish-ready copy.

## [00:03:03] Transcript as Input, Not Final Truth

A transcript contains filler, false starts, jokes that don't read well, and statements that were later corrected. Maya's key insight: *"The transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth."* Transcripts are excellent for first drafts of summaries, chapter suggestions, and quote candidates — but anything authoritative (facts, links, names, dates, claims) still needs human review.

## [00:04:15] A Lightweight Pipeline for Small Teams

For a team of two or three:

- One folder or workspace per episode.
- A clear naming convention.
- A checklist with defined handoff points.
- A fixed **output bundle**: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, and a publish log.

## [00:04:54] Raw vs. Cleaned Transcripts

Keep both. The raw transcript provides traceability. The cleaned transcript — with disfluencies removed, speaker labels fixed, and formatting applied — becomes the preferred source for summarization and repurposing. Without this separation, downstream systems inherit every mess from the source.

## [00:05:49] Where Automation Is Overestimated

Teams overestimate automation in two areas:

- **Editorial judgment** — an agent can propose titles but doesn't know your audience positioning unless it's explicitly encoded.
- **Exception handling** — guest name corrections, legal review concerns, sensitive anecdotes, and sponsor placement constraints are edge cases where fully automated systems tend to fail.

The healthy mindset: *use automation for acceleration, not abdication.*

## [00:06:52] Title Strategy

Generate several title candidates from different angles: problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, and curiosity-driven. Then compare them against your actual audience promise. A title is not just a label — it's a contract with the listener. Frequency of a phrase in the transcript is not a strategy.

## [00:08:13] Pre-Publish Review Checklist

Five highest-value checks before going live:

1. **Names and affiliations** — get the guest's name, title, and company right.
2. **Links and references** — verify every destination the host promised to link.
3. **Sponsor compliance** — confirm required sponsor language is present.
4. **Sensitive edits** — ensure anything the guest wanted removed is actually removed.
5. **Metadata consistency** — title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy should all describe the same episode.

## [00:09:13] Practical Takeaways

Five steps to improve your pipeline this month:

1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention.
2. Create a publish checklist that one person owns.
3. Separate raw transcript and cleaned transcript.
4. Define the required output bundle for every episode.
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to track delays, confusion, and repeated errors.

Bonus: *If you're experimenting with automation, start by automating drafts, not approvals.*
