# Episode 14: Building a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline

## Episode Summary

In this episode, host and guest Maya Chen break down what it actually takes to build a podcast production pipeline that holds up under real-world conditions. Maya advises media teams and creator businesses on production systems, and she brings a clear-eyed view of where small teams succeed, where they struggle, and what discipline looks like at scale.

The conversation covers the five core stages of a production pipeline — capture, post-production, content extraction, review and approval, and distribution — and why each stage matters for the final listener experience. Maya explains why the transcript is a rich input but not the final source of editorial truth, how to think about automation without giving up responsibility for what goes live, and what the highest-value pre-publish checks actually are.

If you run a small show or manage content ops for a media team, this episode is a practical guide to getting your pipeline consistent and reliable.

## Key Takeaways

- A podcast pipeline has five stages: capture, post-production, content extraction, review and approval, and distribution.
- The pipeline is the product — if it breaks, listeners feel it even if the conversation itself was great.
- Keep both a raw transcript and a cleaned transcript. The cleaned version becomes the preferred source for summaries and repurposing; the raw version stays for traceability.
- Transcripts are excellent inputs for first drafts, but facts, links, names, affiliations, and sponsor language still require human review.
- Automate repetitive transforms (transcription cleanup, first-draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting). Don't automate approvals.
- A title is a contract with the listener — generate several candidates from different angles before choosing.
- Before publishing, check: names and affiliations, links and references, sponsor compliance, sensitive edits, and metadata consistency.
- For a small team: one folder per episode, a clear naming convention, a publish checklist, a standard output bundle, and a lightweight episode log.
