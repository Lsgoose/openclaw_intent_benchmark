# Show Notes

## [00:00:00] Why the production pipeline shapes the listener experience
- The episode opens by reframing podcast production as part of the product, not just back-office operations.
- Maya Chen argues that the pipeline affects discoverability, navigation, polish, and overall release quality.
- Core idea: "the pipeline is the product."

## [00:01:20] The five stages of a podcast production pipeline
- Capture: record the conversation and gather supporting materials.
- Post-production: clean audio, trim errors, and decide what stays.
- Content extraction: generate transcripts, summaries, notes, chapters, titles, and clips.
- Review and approval: check facts, names, links, sponsor language, and risks.
- Distribution: publish the final package across hosting, social, newsletters, and archives.

## [00:02:29] Where small teams break first: handoffs, naming, and transcript assumptions
- Small teams usually struggle with fragmented handoffs and missing sources of truth.
- Inconsistent file naming and asset storage create version confusion.
- Raw transcripts are useful, but they are not automatically publish-ready content.

## [00:03:34] What transcripts are good for — and what still needs human review
- Transcripts work well for first drafts of summaries, chapters, quote pulls, and social options.
- Human review is still required for facts, names, affiliations, dates, links, sponsor language, and claims.
- Key framing: the transcript is a rich input, not final editorial truth.

## [00:04:15] A lightweight pipeline for small teams
- Keep one folder or workspace per episode.
- Use a clear naming convention and defined handoff points.
- Standardize a fixed output bundle so every episode ends with the same core assets.

## [00:04:54] Why teams should keep both raw and cleaned transcripts
- The raw transcript supports traceability and source verification.
- The cleaned transcript becomes the preferred source for summarization and repurposing.
- Separating the two reduces downstream noise for both humans and automation.

## [00:05:49] Where automation helps — and where it fails
- Automation is strongest at repetitive transforms such as transcript cleanup, first drafts, chapter suggestions, formatting, packaging, and metadata prefill.
- It is much weaker at editorial judgment and exception handling.
- The recommended mindset is acceleration, not abdication.

## [00:06:52] How to think about episode titles
- Generate title options from different angles: problem, guest, outcome, or curiosity.
- Choose titles based on audience promise, not just repeated phrases from the transcript.
- A title should act as a contract with the listener.

## [00:08:13] The highest-value pre-publish checks
- Verify names and affiliations.
- Confirm links and references before publishing them.
- Check sponsor compliance and sensitive edits.
- Make sure the title, description, transcript, chapters, and social copy all align.

## [00:09:13] Practical improvements for any small show this month
- Standardize the episode folder structure and naming convention.
- Create a publish checklist with a clear owner.
- Separate raw and clean transcripts.
- Define the required output bundle for every release.
- Keep a lightweight episode log to spot repeated bottlenecks and mistakes.
