# Episode 14 Show Notes: Building a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline

[00:00:00] The episode opens by framing the production pipeline as the thing that shapes the listener experience from raw recording through distribution.

[00:01:27] Maya breaks the pipeline into five stages: capture, post-production, content extraction, review and approval, and distribution.

[00:02:33] The team discusses common small-team failures: handoff gaps, inconsistent naming and storage, and the false assumption that a transcript is automatically publish-ready.

[00:03:09] A transcript is described as a rich input, not final truth. It can support summaries, chapters, quotes, and social posts, but facts, links, names, sponsor language, and claims still need review.

[00:04:26] For small teams, the recommended foundation is a single episode workspace, clear naming conventions, a defined checklist, and a standard output bundle.

[00:04:59] Maya recommends keeping both raw and cleaned transcripts so teams can preserve traceability while using the cleaned version for repurposing.

[00:05:57] The conversation turns to automation limits: title suggestions and draft generation can be automated, but editorial judgment and exception handling still need people.

[00:06:31] Automation should accelerate repetitive transforms such as cleanup, formatting, packaging, and metadata prefill, while humans remain accountable for correctness and release quality.

[00:06:57] Titles matter because they act like a contract with the listener. The episode recommends generating several title angles rather than mechanically picking the most repeated phrase.

[00:08:20] The highest-value prepublish checks are names and affiliations, links and references, sponsor compliance, sensitive edits, and metadata consistency across title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy.

[00:09:23] The practical takeaway is to standardize episode folders, assign one owner to the publish checklist, separate raw and cleaned transcripts, define a required output bundle, and maintain a lightweight episode log.

[00:09:58] The episode closes on the principle that machines should produce options, while humans approve what becomes public.
