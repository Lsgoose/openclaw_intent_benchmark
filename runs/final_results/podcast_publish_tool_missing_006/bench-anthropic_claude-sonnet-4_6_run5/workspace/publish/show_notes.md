# Episode 14 — Building a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline
**Guest:** Maya Chen, media production systems advisor

---

## [00:00:00] Introduction
Host introduces the episode's focus: how a show travels from raw recording to polished, distributed episode — and why the production pipeline shapes the listener experience as much as the conversation itself.

---

## [00:00:36] The Pipeline Is the Product
Maya frames the core insight: most teams don't notice the pipeline until something breaks. A vague title, a sloppy transcript, misaligned chapter markers, or an inconsistent checklist all compound into a production that feels unreliable to listeners.

---

## [00:01:20] The Five Stages of a Podcast Production Pipeline
Maya breaks the pipeline into five stages:

1. **Capture** — recording plus companion materials (guest bios, sponsor copy, links, references)
2. **Post-production** — audio cleanup, trimming, editorial decisions
3. **Content extraction** — transcript generation, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, clips
4. **Review and approval** — fact-checking, sponsor language, names, links, editorial and legal review
5. **Distribution** — delivery to hosting platform, newsletter, social channels, and archives

The analogy: a podcast pipeline mirrors a software build pipeline — source material, transformations, validation, packaging, deployment.

---

## [00:02:29] Where Small Teams Break Down
Three common pain points:

- **Handoffs without a single source of truth** — recording, editing, and writing owned by different people with no shared reference
- **Inconsistent naming and asset storage** — files like `final_v2_real_final.wav` erode confidence in which version is canonical
- **Transcript ≠ publish-ready copy** — raw transcripts contain filler, false starts, unverified links, and statements later corrected in the same conversation

---

## [00:03:34] What to Extract from a Transcript — and What Still Needs Human Review
Transcripts are strong inputs for:
- Episode summaries (first drafts)
- Chapter suggestions
- Quote candidates
- Social post options
- Search and repurposing

Anything authoritative — facts, links, sponsor language, names, affiliations, dates, claims — still requires human review. Maya's framing: *"The transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth."*

---

## [00:04:15] Lightweight Pipeline Design for Small Teams
Recommended structure for a two- or three-person team:

- One folder or workspace per episode
- A clear naming convention
- A checklist with defined handoff points
- A **standard output bundle** for every episode: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, publish log

Keep both a **raw transcript** (for traceability) and a **cleaned transcript** (preferred source for summarization and repurposing). Separating them prevents downstream tools and teammates from inheriting source noise.

---

## [00:05:49] Automation: Where Teams Overestimate It
Two areas where automation is commonly over-trusted:

1. **Editorial judgment** — agents can propose titles, but audience positioning must be encoded explicitly
2. **Exception handling** — guest name corrections, legal review flags, sensitive anecdotes, sponsor placement constraints; automated systems tend to fail on edge cases, and media teams live on edge cases

Healthy mindset: *use automation to accelerate repetitive transforms* (transcription cleanup, first-draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, metadata prefill) — but don't abdicate responsibility for what goes live.

---

## [00:06:52] Titles: Strategy, Not Frequency
Titles do significant work. Maya's approach: generate candidates from multiple angles.

- Problem-oriented: *"Why Podcast Production Breaks Even When the Recording Is Great"*
- Outcome-oriented: *"From Raw Recording to Publish: The Systems Behind a Great Podcast"*
- Audience-intent-oriented: *"How Small Teams Build a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline"*

Key principle: frequency of a phrase in the transcript is not a title strategy. Good titles come from understanding what the episode is helping the listener do.

Workflow note: aim for three title candidates, one short description, one long description, and a final approved version logged before publish.

---

## [00:08:13] Pre-Publish Review: The Five Highest-Value Checks
1. **Names and affiliations** — guest name, title, company
2. **Links and references** — verify every destination that was promised in the recording
3. **Sponsor compliance** — required language present in the right places
4. **Sensitive edits** — confirm removals the guest requested are actually gone
5. **Metadata consistency** — title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy all describe the same episode

---

## [00:09:13] Practical Takeaways
Steps to improve your pipeline this month:

1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. Create a publish checklist with a single owner
3. Separate raw transcript from cleaned transcript
4. Define the required output bundle for every episode
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to surface delays, confusion, and repeated errors

Host addition: *if you're experimenting with automation, start by automating drafts — not approvals.*
