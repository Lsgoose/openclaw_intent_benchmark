# Episode 14: Building a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline

Guest: Maya Chen  
Focus: Production systems, workflow design, and pipeline reliability for podcast teams

---

## [00:00] Introduction: The Pipeline Is the Product

The episode opens with a reframing: podcast production isn't just about the conversation—it's about how that conversation gets edited, packaged, labeled, and distributed. Maya Chen joins to discuss why production discipline shapes the entire listener experience.

**Links:**
- [Maya Chen profile](https://creators.example.com/guests/maya-chen)

## [01:27] The Five Stages of a Production Pipeline

Maya breaks podcast production into five distinct stages:

1. **Capture** — Recording plus companion materials (bios, sponsor copy, links)
2. **Post-production** — Audio cleanup, trimming, editorial decisions
3. **Content extraction** — Transcripts, summaries, quotes, chapters, titles, clips
4. **Review and approval** — Fact-checking, sponsor language verification, legal review
5. **Distribution** — Publishing to hosting platforms, newsletters, social channels, archives

> "Just like software systems, podcast pipelines become fragile when the process depends too much on memory and heroics." — Maya Chen

---

## [02:33] Where Small Teams Struggle First

Three common pain points:

- **Handoffs** — No single source of truth when multiple people touch an episode
- **Inconsistent naming** — Files like "final_v2_real_final.wav" create confusion about the source of record
- **Transcript-driven tasks** — Raw transcripts require judgment; they aren't automatically publish-ready

---

## [03:40] Transcripts as Rich Input, Not Final Truth

A raw transcript contains filler, false starts, jokes that don't read well, and forward references ("we'll put the link in the show notes") that may not be verified. Maya emphasizes that transcripts excel at generating first drafts—summaries, chapter suggestions, quote candidates—but authoritative claims (facts, links, names, dates, affiliations) still need human review.

> "The transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth." — Maya Chen

---

## [04:26] Lightweight Pipeline for Small Teams

For teams of 2–3 people:

- One folder/workspace per episode
- Clear naming conventions
- A checklist with defined handoff points
- A fixed **output bundle** for every episode: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapters, thumbnail, publish log

---

## [04:59] Raw Transcript vs. Clean Transcript

Maya recommends maintaining both:

- **Raw transcript** — For traceability and internal reference
- **Clean transcript** — Disfluencies removed, speaker labels fixed, formatted for readability; becomes the preferred source for summarization and repurposing

Separating the two prevents downstream systems from inheriting "every little mess from the source."

---

## [05:57] Automation Boundaries: Acceleration, Not Abdication

Where teams overestimate automation:

- **Editorial judgment** — Agents can propose titles, but don't know your audience positioning unless explicitly encoded
- **Exception handling** — Fully automated systems fail on edge cases; media teams "live on edge cases"

Healthy mindset: automate repetitive transforms (transcription cleanup, first drafts, formatting, metadata prefill), but humans retain responsibility for what goes live.

> "Use automation to accelerate repetitive transforms... but don't give up responsibility for what goes live." — Maya Chen

---

## [06:57] Title Generation Strategy

Maya advises generating multiple candidates from different angles:

- Problem-oriented
- Guest-oriented  
- Outcome-oriented
- Curiosity-driven

Then compare against your actual audience promise. A title is a contract with the listener—not just a label.

**Example frames for this episode:**
- "How Small Teams Build a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline"
- "Why Podcast Production Breaks Even When the Recording Is Great"
- "From Raw Recording to Publish: The Systems Behind a Great Podcast"

> "Frequency is not strategy. The most repeated phrase in a conversation is often not the most compelling framing." — Maya Chen

---

## [08:20] The Pre-Publish Review Checklist

Five high-value checks before going live:

1. **Names and affiliations** — Verify guest name, title, company
2. **Links and references** — Confirm any "we'll link that" promises
3. **Sponsor compliance** — Required language present where it belongs
4. **Sensitive edits** — Confirm requested removals are actually removed
5. **Metadata consistency** — Title, description, transcript, chapters, and social copy all describe the same episode

---

## [09:23] Five Actions to Improve Your Pipeline This Month

Maya's practical takeaway list:

1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. Create a publish checklist with a single owner
3. Separate raw transcript from cleaned transcript
4. Define the required output bundle for every episode
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to track delays, confusion, or repeated errors

Host addition: If experimenting with automation, start by automating drafts—not approvals.

> "Make the machine produce options. Make the human approve what becomes public." — Maya Chen
