# Show Notes

## 00:00:00 — Opening: the pipeline is the product
The episode opens on the idea that the podcast production pipeline shapes the listener experience as much as the conversation itself.

## 00:00:27 — Maya Chen on why production discipline matters
Maya frames the pipeline as the product and explains how small failures compound when titles, transcripts, chapters, and publishing checks are inconsistent.

## 00:01:20 — The five stages of a podcast production pipeline
The discussion breaks the pipeline into capture, post-production, content extraction, review and approval, and distribution.

## 00:02:29 — Common failure points for small teams
The episode covers handoff issues, inconsistent asset naming, and the trap of treating a transcript as finished content.

## 00:03:40 — What transcripts can and cannot do
Clean transcripts are strong inputs for summaries, chapter suggestions, quotes, and social options, but facts, links, names, dates, and sponsor language still need human review.

## 00:04:26 — Building a lightweight but reliable workflow
Maya recommends one folder per episode, a clear naming convention, a checklist with defined handoffs, and a fixed output bundle.

## 00:04:59 — Raw transcript versus cleaned transcript
The raw transcript stays as traceability, while the cleaned transcript becomes the preferred source for summarization and repurposing.

## 00:05:49 — Where automation helps and where it breaks
Automation is useful for repetitive transforms, but editorial judgment and exception handling still need people.

## 00:06:57 — Titles as a contract with the listener
The conversation explores generating multiple title angles and choosing the one that best matches audience intent.

## 00:08:20 — The highest-value review checks
The episode highlights five checks before publish: names and affiliations, links and references, sponsor compliance, sensitive edits, and metadata consistency.

## 00:09:13 — Practical steps to improve this month
The episode closes with a simple action plan: standardize the folder structure, own the checklist, separate raw and cleaned transcripts, define the output bundle, and keep a lightweight episode log.

## 00:10:05 — Sponsor mention
SoundLayer is mentioned as a collaboration tool for small media teams managing transcripts, asset versions, and review workflows.
