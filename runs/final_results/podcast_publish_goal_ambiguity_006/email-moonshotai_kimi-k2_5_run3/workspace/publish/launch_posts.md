# Launch Posts

## Post 1: Key Takeaway

"The transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth."

This line from Maya Chen reframes how teams should treat automated transcripts. They're excellent for first drafts—summaries, chapters, quote pulls—but anything authoritative (facts, links, sponsor language, names, dates, claims) still needs human review. Raw transcripts contain filler, false starts, jokes that don't read well, and mentions of "we'll link that" that were never verified. If your downstream systems build directly on uncleaned sources, you inherit every mess. Clean first, then automate.

## Post 2: Discussion Question

Where does your podcast production pipeline usually break first?

Maya Chen identifies three common failure points for small teams: (1) handoffs between record/edit/write with no single source of truth, (2) inconsistent naming that produces files like "final_v2_real_final.wav," and (3) assuming transcripts are ready-to-publish when they're actually full of disfluencies, unverified links, and context that won't make sense to readers.

Which of these sounds most familiar—and what's the one fix you'd implement this week if you had to choose?
