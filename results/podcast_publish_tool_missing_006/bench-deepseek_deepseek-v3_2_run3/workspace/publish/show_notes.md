# Episode 14: Building a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline

## Show Notes

**[00:00:00] Introduction to Podcast Production Pipelines**
Host introduces the topic of podcast production pipelines, explaining how they shape the entire listener experience from raw recording to polished episode with all accompanying assets.

**[00:00:27] Guest Introduction: Maya Chen**
Maya Chen joins the show, an advisor to media teams and creator businesses on production systems. She emphasizes that "the pipeline is the product" - something people don't notice until it breaks.

**[00:01:20] The Five Stages of Podcast Production**
Maya breaks down the production pipeline into five key stages:
1. **Capture**: Recording conversation and collecting companion materials
2. **Post-production**: Audio cleaning and editing decisions
3. **Content extraction**: Transcript generation, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapters, titles, clips
4. **Review and approval**: Fact-checking, sponsor language, names, links, editorial/legal issues
5. **Distribution**: Final package deployment to hosting platforms and channels

**[00:02:33] Common Pain Points for Small Teams**
Maya identifies three major challenges:
1. **Handoffs**: Lack of single source of truth between team members
2. **Inconsistent naming and storage**: Files like "final_v2_real_final.wav" causing confusion
3. **Transcript limitations**: Raw transcripts not being publish-ready content

**[00:03:34] Transcripts: Rich Input vs. Final Truth**
Important distinction between raw transcripts and cleaned versions. Transcripts are excellent for first drafts of summaries, chapters, quotes, and social posts, but anything authoritative (facts, links, names, claims) requires human review.

**[00:04:26] Lightweight Pipeline for Small Teams**
Recommendations for teams of 2-3 people:
- One folder/workspace per episode
- Clear naming conventions
- Checklist with defined handoff points
- Fixed output bundle (final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, publish log)

**[00:04:54] Raw vs. Clean Transcripts**
Strong recommendation to maintain both:
- **Raw transcript**: For traceability and internal reference
- **Cleaned transcript**: Removes disfluencies, fixes speaker labels, formats for readability - becomes preferred source for summarization and repurposing

**[00:05:49] Automation: Where Teams Overestimate**
Two areas where automation is often overestimated:
1. **Editorial judgment**: Agents can propose titles but don't understand audience positioning
2. **Exception handling**: Edge cases (guest name corrections, legal concerns, sensitive content) require human oversight

**[00:06:52] Title Strategy**
Titles do significant work and should be generated from multiple angles:
- Problem-oriented
- Guest-oriented  
- Outcome-oriented
- Curiosity-driven
Frequency of phrases ≠ strategic framing. Good titles come from understanding what the episode helps listeners do.

**[00:08:13] High-Value Pre-Publish Checks**
Five critical checks before going live:
1. **Names and affiliations**: Guest details must be correct
2. **Links and references**: Verify all promised links
3. **Sponsor compliance**: Required sponsor language present
4. **Sensitive edits**: Confirm requested removals are executed
5. **Metadata consistency**: Title, description, transcript, chapters, social copy all align

**[00:09:13] Practical Takeaways for Improving Your Pipeline**
Maya's month-one improvement plan:
1. Define standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. Create a publish checklist with clear ownership
3. Separate raw and cleaned transcripts
4. Define required output bundle for every episode
5. Maintain lightweight episode log to track delays and errors

**[00:09:50] Automation Philosophy**
Key principle: "Make the machine produce options. Make the human approve what becomes public." Start by automating drafts, not approvals.
