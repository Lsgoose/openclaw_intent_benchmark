## Launch Post 1: Key Takeaway

The most dangerous assumption in podcast production: that a transcript is clean, structured truth ready for publishing.

In Episode 14, Maya Chen explains why raw transcripts contain forward references, disfluencies, and unverified claims that can poison your summaries, quotes, and social clips. The fix? Separate your raw transcript (for reference) from your cleaned transcript (for downstream use), and treat both as inputs—not approvals—for anything that goes live.

Your pipeline isn't just a sequence of tasks. It's the system that determines whether your final product matches the quality of your original conversation.

## Launch Post 2: Discussion Question

What's the single biggest friction point in your current production workflow—the place where episodes consistently stall, get confusing, or require rework?

In Episode 14, Maya Chen identifies handoffs as the first pain point for most small teams: one person records, another edits, someone else writes the description, and nobody owns a single source of truth. The solution she proposes is almost embarrassingly simple: a standard folder structure, a fixed output bundle for every episode, and a checklist with one named owner.

Where does your process currently break down? And would a lightweight system actually solve it, or is the friction coming from somewhere else entirely?
