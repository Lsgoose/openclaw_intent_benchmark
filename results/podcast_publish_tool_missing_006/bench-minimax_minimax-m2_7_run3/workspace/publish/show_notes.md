# Show Notes — Episode 14: Building a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline

## Timestamped Sections

**[00:00] Introduction**
Host introduces the episode topic — how a raw recording becomes a polished, distributed episode — and welcomes guest Maya Chen, an advisor to media teams and creator businesses.

**[00:27] What Is a Podcast Production Pipeline?**
Maya describes the pipeline as the hidden product: the system that shapes the listener experience more than most people realize. Host and guest agree that production discipline is often invisible until something breaks.

**[01:20] The Five Stages of a Production Pipeline**
Maya breaks down the pipeline into five stages:
- **Capture** — recording and collecting companion materials (guest bios, sponsor copy, links, references)
- **Post-production** — audio cleaning, mistake trimming, editorial decisions
- **Content extraction** — transcripts, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, clips
- **Review and approval** — fact-checking, sponsor language review, legal and editorial checks
- **Distribution** — publishing to hosting platform, newsletter, social channels, and archives

**[02:07] Analogy to Software Build Systems**
Maya draws a parallel to software pipelines: source material → transformations → validation → packaging → deployment. Both systems become fragile when they rely on memory and heroics instead of defined processes.

**[02:29] Where Small Teams Struggle Most**
Three primary pain points:
1. **Handoffs** — multiple people, no single source of truth
2. **Inconsistent naming** — files like "final_v2_real_final.wav" create confusion
3. **Transcript-driven tasks** — raw transcripts require editorial judgment and are not automatically publish-ready

**[03:03] Why Transcripts Are a Rich Input, Not Final Truth**
Maya explains that raw transcripts often contain filler, false starts, jokes that don't read well, and unverified verbal references ("we'll link that in the show notes"). Treating a transcript as clean truth leads to low-quality or misleading derivative content. Transcripts are excellent inputs for first drafts of summaries, chapter suggestions, quote candidates, and social posts — but anything authoritative (facts, links, sponsor language, names, affiliations) requires human review.

> *"A transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth."* — Maya Chen

**[04:15] A Lightweight but Reliable Pipeline for Small Teams**
Maya's recommended minimal structure: one folder per episode, clear naming convention, defined handoff checklist, and a fixed output bundle. Every episode should produce the same core assets: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, and publish log. Standardizing outputs makes everything downstream easier.

**[04:54] Raw vs. Cleaned Transcripts**
Maya strongly recommends keeping both versions. The raw transcript preserves traceability and internal reference. The cleaned version — stripped of disfluencies, corrected speaker labels, formatted for readability — becomes the preferred source for all summarization and repurposing. Separating raw from cleaned prevents downstream systems from inheriting every source imperfection.

**[05:49] What Small Teams Overestimate in Automation**
Two common overestimates:
1. **Editorial judgment** — agents can propose titles, but can't know your audience positioning unless it's encoded somewhere
2. **Exception handling** — every few episodes brings a name correction, legal concern, sensitive anecdote, or sponsor constraint that fully automated systems can't navigate

> *"Use automation to accelerate repetitive transforms, not to abdicate responsibility for what goes live."* — Maya Chen

**[06:52] How to Think About Episode Titles**
Maya advises generating title candidates from multiple angles: problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, curiosity-driven. Compare each against the actual audience promise. A title is a contract with the listener, not just a label. Frequency-based title selection (picking the most repeated phrase) is not strategy — good titles come from understanding what the episode helps the listener *do*.

Example titles for this same episode:
- "How Small Teams Build a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline"
- "Why Podcast Production Breaks Even When the Recording Is Great"
- "From Raw Recording to Publish: The Systems Behind a Great Podcast"

**[08:03] Pre-Publish Review Checklist**
Maya's five highest-value checks before an episode goes live:
1. Names and affiliations — confirm guest's name, title, and company are correct
2. Links and references — verify actual destinations when the host says "we'll link that"
3. Sponsor compliance — confirm required sponsor language is present
4. Sensitive edits — confirm anything the guest wanted removed is actually gone
5. Metadata consistency — title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy all describe the same episode

**[09:13] Practical Takeaways for Small Shows**
Maya's five-step starting plan:
1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. Create a publish checklist owned by one person
3. Separate raw transcript from cleaned transcript
4. Define the required output bundle for every episode
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to spot recurring delays and errors

Host adds: if you're experimenting with automation, **automate drafts, not approvals**.

> *"Make the machine produce options. Make the human approve what becomes public."* — Maya Chen
