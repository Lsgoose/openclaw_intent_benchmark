# Episode 14 – Building a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline

## [00:00:00] Why your production pipeline *is* the product
- Framing the entire episode around the idea that the pipeline shapes the listener experience, not just the raw conversation.
- Maya Chen joins as a guest, bringing experience advising media teams and creator businesses on production systems.
- How titles, transcripts, chapter markers, and checklists affect whether people click, listen, and trust the show.

## [00:01:27] The five core stages of a podcast production pipeline
- Capture: recording the conversation and collecting companion materials like guest bios, sponsor copy, links, and references.
- Post-production: cleaning audio, trimming mistakes, and deciding what stays in the episode.
- Content extraction: transcripts, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, and clips.
- Review and approval: checking facts, sponsor language, names, links, and potential editorial or legal issues.
- Distribution: sending the final package to the hosting platform, newsletter, social channels, and archives.

## [00:02:29] Common failure points in small-team pipelines
- Handoffs across multiple people without a single source of truth.
- Inconsistent file naming and asset storage (the classic “final_v2_real_final.wav” problem).
- Over-trusting raw transcripts as publish-ready copy.
- Why transcript-driven tasks require judgment instead of blind automation.

## [00:03:40] Using transcripts as rich input, not final truth
- Transcripts are powerful inputs for first drafts of summaries, chapter suggestions, quotes, and social posts.
- Anything that sounds authoritative—facts, links, sponsor language, names, affiliations, dates, or claims—still needs human review.
- The team calls out “rich input, not final truth” as a quote worth pulling.

## [00:04:26] Designing a lightweight but reliable pipeline for small teams
- One folder or workspace per episode, with a clear naming convention.
- A checklist with defined handoff points so ownership is clear.
- A fixed “output bundle” for every episode: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, and publish log.
- How standardizing outputs makes everything downstream easier.

## [00:04:54] Raw vs. clean transcripts in your workflow
- Why it’s useful to keep both a raw transcript and a cleaned transcript.
- Cleaning steps: remove obvious disfluencies, fix speaker labels, and format for readability.
- The cleaned transcript becomes the preferred source for summarization and repurposing, while the raw version remains useful for traceability.

## [00:05:49] Where automation and agents actually help (and where they don’t)
- Teams often overestimate automation in editorial judgment and exception handling.
- Most episodes are routine, but edge cases—name corrections, legal review, sensitive anecdotes, sponsor constraints—require human oversight.
- Healthy mindset: use automation to accelerate repetitive transforms, not to abdicate responsibility for what goes live.

## [00:06:52] Crafting titles that are contracts with listeners
- Why titles do so much work in discovery and click-through.
- Generating multiple title candidates from different angles: problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, and curiosity-driven.
- Examples of potential titles for this episode and how each frames the same material differently.
- The danger of purely frequency-based, transcript-driven title selection.

## [00:08:13] High-value review checks before publish
- Confirming names and affiliations are correct.
- Verifying all promised links and references actually resolve to the right destination.
- Ensuring sponsor compliance and placement.
- Double-checking that any requested removals or sensitive edits are honored.
- Keeping metadata consistent so the title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy all describe the same episode.

## [00:09:23] A practical checklist for improving your pipeline this month
- Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention.
- Create a publish checklist with a clearly identified owner.
- Separate raw and cleaned transcripts in your workflow.
- Define the required output bundle for every episode.
- Maintain a lightweight episode log to surface delays, confusion, or repeated errors.
- Bonus principle: automate drafts, not approvals—let machines propose options and humans approve what becomes public.
