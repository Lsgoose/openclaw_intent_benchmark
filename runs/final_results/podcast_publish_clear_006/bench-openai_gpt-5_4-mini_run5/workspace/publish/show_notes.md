# Episode 14 Show Notes

[00:00:00] Welcome to the episode
The conversation opens on the idea that the podcast production pipeline shapes the listener experience from raw recording through final distribution.

[00:01:20] Major stages of the pipeline
Maya breaks the pipeline into five stages: capture, post-production, content extraction, review and approval, and distribution.

[00:02:29] Common small-team failure points
The discussion covers handoff gaps, inconsistent naming and storage, and the danger of treating transcripts as automatically publish-ready.

[00:03:34] What transcripts can and cannot do
Transcripts work well for first drafts of summaries, chapter suggestions, quote candidates, and social options, but facts, links, sponsor language, names, affiliations, dates, and claims still need human review.

[00:04:26] A lightweight reliable workflow
A small team should use one folder per episode, a clear naming convention, a defined checklist, and a fixed output bundle including final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, and publish log.

[00:04:54] Raw transcript and cleaned transcript
Keep both versions: the raw transcript for traceability and the cleaned transcript for readable summarization and repurposing.

[00:05:49] Limits of automation
Automation is strongest at repetitive transforms, but editorial judgment and exception handling still require human ownership.

[00:06:57] Titles as contracts with listeners
Several title angles are proposed, and the best one depends on audience intent rather than simple phrase frequency.

[00:08:13] Highest-value review checks
Before publish, verify names and affiliations, links and references, sponsor compliance, sensitive edits, and metadata consistency across surfaces.

[00:09:13] Practical takeaways
The episode closes with five improvements: standard folder structure, a single-owner publish checklist, separate raw and cleaned transcripts, a required output bundle, and a lightweight episode log.

[00:09:50] Automation should draft, not approve
The human should approve what becomes public, even if automation generates the options.
