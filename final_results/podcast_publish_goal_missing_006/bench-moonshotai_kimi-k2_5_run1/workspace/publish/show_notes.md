# Episode 14: The Podcast Production Pipeline

Guest: Maya Chen

---

## Introduction: Why the Pipeline Is the Product

**[00:00:00 - 00:01:20]**

Host introduces the podcast production pipeline—the operational system that transforms raw recordings into polished, discoverable episodes. Maya Chen joins to discuss how production discipline shapes listener experience, covering everything from clean audio and transcripts to chapter markers, titles, and metadata.

> *"The pipeline is the product"* — if the title is vague, people don't click. If the transcript is sloppy, downstream content generation suffers. Small teams need production discipline.

---

## The Five Stages of Production

**[00:01:20 - 00:02:29]**

Maya breaks the pipeline into five stages:

1. **Capture** — Recording the conversation and collecting companion materials (bios, sponsor copy, links, references)
2. **Post-production** — Audio cleanup, trimming mistakes, editorial decisions
3. **Content extraction** — Transcript generation, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, clips
4. **Review and approval** — Fact-checking, sponsor language verification, legal/editorial review
5. **Distribution** — Publishing to hosting platform, newsletter, social channels, archives

> *"Just like software systems, podcast pipelines become fragile when the process depends too much on memory and heroics."*

---

## Where Small Teams Struggle

**[00:02:29 - 00:03:34]**

Three common pain points for small teams:

- **Handoffs** — No single source of truth when one person records, another edits, and someone else writes descriptions
- **Inconsistent naming and storage** — Files like "final_v2_real_final.wav" create version confusion
- **Transcript-driven tasks** — Raw transcripts require significant judgment and are not automatically publish-ready

---

## Transcripts: Input vs. Editorial Truth

**[00:03:34 - 00:04:26]**

The transcript contains filler, false starts, jokes that don't read well, and unverified references (e.g., "we'll put the link in the show notes"). While excellent for first drafts of summaries, chapters, quotes, and social posts, anything authoritative requires human review.

**What transcripts excel at:**
- First drafts of summaries, chapters, quotes, social posts
- Searchability and repurposing

**What still needs review:**
- Facts, links, sponsor language
- Names, affiliations, dates, claims

> *"The transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth."*

---

## Building a Lightweight, Reliable Pipeline

**[00:04:26 - 00:05:49]**

For teams of 2-3 people:

- One folder/workspace per episode
- Clear naming conventions
- Checklist with defined handoff points
- Fixed output bundle for every episode: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapters, thumbnail, publish log

**Clean vs. Raw Transcripts:**
- Keep raw transcripts for traceability
- Produce cleaned transcripts (remove disfluencies, fix speaker labels, format for readability)
- Cleaned versions become the preferred source for summarization and repurposing

> *"A good pipeline is partly about reducing noise before you ask another tool or another teammate to build on the result."*

---

## Automation: Acceleration, Not Abdication

**[00:05:49 - 00:06:52]**

Two areas where automation is overestimated:

1. **Editorial judgment** — Agents can propose titles but don't know audience positioning without explicit encoding
2. **Exception handling** — Edge cases (name corrections, legal review, sensitive edits, sponsor constraints) break fully automated systems

> *"Use automation to accelerate repetitive transforms... but don't give up responsibility for what goes live."*

---

## The Art of Episode Titles

**[00:06:52 - 00:08:20]**

Titles are contracts with the listener, not just labels. Generate candidates from multiple angles:

- Problem-oriented
- Guest-oriented
- Outcome-oriented
- Curiosity-driven

**Example framing variations for a production workflow episode:**
- "How Small Teams Build a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline"
- "Why Podcast Production Breaks Even When the Recording Is Great"
- "From Raw Recording to Publish: The Systems Behind a Great Podcast"

> *"Frequency is not strategy. The most repeated phrase is often not the most compelling framing."*

---

## The Pre-Publish Review Checklist

**[00:08:20 - 00:09:23]**

Five high-value checks before going live:

1. **Names and affiliations** — Verify guest name, title, company
2. **Links and references** — Confirm every "we'll link that" has a verified destination
3. **Sponsor compliance** — Required sponsor language present where it should be
4. **Sensitive edits** — Confirm guest-requested removals are actually removed
5. **Metadata consistency** — Title, description, transcript, chapters, and social copy all describe the same episode

---

## Practical Takeaways for Small Shows

**[00:09:23 - 00:10:05]**

Five actions to improve your pipeline this month:

1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. Create a publish checklist that one person owns
3. Separate raw transcript from cleaned transcript
4. Define the required output bundle for every episode
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to track delays and repeated errors

> *"You don't need an elaborate system to get meaningful gains. You need consistency."*
