# Episode 14 Show Notes

[00:00:00] Introduction: why the production pipeline shapes the listener experience
- Raw recordings become polished episodes through editing, packaging, labeling, and distribution.
- The pipeline matters because it affects audio quality, transcripts, chapters, clips, titles, descriptions, and discoverability.

[00:01:27] The five major pipeline stages
- Capture: record the conversation and gather guest bios, sponsor copy, links, and references.
- Post-production: clean audio, trim mistakes, and decide what stays.
- Content extraction: generate transcript, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, and clips.
- Review and approval: check facts, sponsor language, names, links, and legal/editorial issues.
- Distribution: publish to the hosting platform, newsletter, social channels, and archives.

[00:02:33] Common failure points for small teams
- Handoffs break down when no one owns a single source of truth.
- Inconsistent naming and asset storage create version confusion.
- Transcript-driven tasks still require judgment; a raw transcript is not publish-ready copy.

[00:03:09] Why transcripts need human review
- Transcripts can contain filler, false starts, jokes that do not read well, and corrected statements.
- Treat transcripts as rich inputs for drafts, not final editorial truth.
- Facts, links, sponsor language, names, affiliations, dates, and claims should be reviewed.

[00:04:26] A lightweight but reliable setup
- Use one folder or workspace per episode.
- Keep a clear naming convention and a checklist with defined handoff points.
- Standardize the output bundle: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, and publish log.

[00:04:59] Raw transcript vs. cleaned transcript
- Keep the raw transcript for traceability and verification.
- Produce a cleaned transcript for summarization and repurposing.
- Separate raw from cleaned material so downstream automation does not inherit source noise.

[00:05:57] Where automation helps, and where it does not
- Automation is useful for transcription cleanup, first-draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, asset packaging, and metadata prefill.
- Editorial judgment and exception handling still need human ownership.
- Fully automated systems fail on edge cases such as name corrections, legal review, sensitive anecdotes, and sponsor constraints.

[00:06:57] Titles as a listener contract
- Generate several title candidates from different angles: problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, and curiosity-driven.
- Do not rely on repetition alone; frequency is not strategy.
- A title should reflect what the episode helps the listener do.

[00:08:20] Highest-value pre-release checks
- Confirm names and affiliations.
- Verify links and references.
- Check sponsor compliance.
- Remove anything that was requested out.
- Make sure the title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy all describe the same episode.

[00:09:23] Practical takeaways for the month ahead
- Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention.
- Create a publish checklist owned by one person.
- Separate raw transcript and cleaned transcript.
- Define the required output bundle.
- Keep a lightweight episode log to track delays, confusion, and repeated errors.

[00:09:50] Automation principle to keep in mind
- Automate drafts, not approvals.
- Make the machine produce options.
- Make the human approve what becomes public.
