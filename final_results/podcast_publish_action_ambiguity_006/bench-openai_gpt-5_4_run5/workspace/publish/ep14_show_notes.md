# Episode 14: Building a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline

## Summary

In Episode 14, the show looks at what actually turns a raw podcast recording into a reliable listener experience. Maya Chen joins to explain why the pipeline itself becomes part of the product: titles, transcripts, chapter markers, metadata, links, and review discipline all shape what listeners get. The conversation covers the five major stages of production, common failure points for small teams, why cleaned transcripts matter, where automation helps, and the checks that matter most before release. The practical takeaway is straightforward: standardize the episode workspace, separate raw and cleaned transcript assets, define a fixed output bundle, and keep one human accountable for what goes live.

## Key points

- A podcast production pipeline can be treated like a media build pipeline: source material, transformations, validation, packaging, and deployment.
- The five stages discussed in the episode are capture, post-production, content extraction, review and approval, and distribution.
- Small teams usually struggle first with handoffs, inconsistent naming, and treating raw transcripts like publish-ready truth.
- A cleaned transcript should be the preferred source for summaries, titles, quotes, and repurposing, while the raw transcript stays available for traceability.
- Automation is most useful for repetitive draft work; humans should still own approvals, correctness, and release quality.
- High-value pre-publish checks include names, affiliations, links, sponsor language, sensitive edits, and metadata consistency across surfaces.

## Notable lines

- “The pipeline is the product.”
- “The transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth.”
- “Use automation to accelerate repetitive transforms ... But don’t give up responsibility for what goes live.”
- “A title is not just a label. It’s a contract with the listener.”
