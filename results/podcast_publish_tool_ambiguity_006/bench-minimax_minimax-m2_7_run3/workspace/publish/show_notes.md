**[00:00:47] The Pipeline Is the Product**
The product is not just the conversation — it's how that conversation gets edited, packaged, labeled, and distributed. If the title is vague, people don't click. If the transcript is sloppy, downstream content generation suffers.

**[00:01:20] The Five Pipeline Stages**
1. **Capture** — recording + companion materials (guest bios, sponsor copy, links, references)
2. **Post-production** — clean audio, trim mistakes, decide what stays
3. **Content extraction** — transcript, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, clips
4. **Review and approval** — facts, sponsor language, names, links, editorial/legal checks
5. **Distribution** — hosting platform, newsletter, social channels, archives

The pipeline-as-software-build analogy: source material → transformations → validation → packaging → deployment.

**[00:02:29] Where Small Teams Struggle First**
- **Handoffs**: no single source of truth when multiple people touch the episode
- **Naming and storage**: inconsistent filenames ("final_v2_real_final.wav")
- **Transcript judgment**: raw transcript is not automatically publish-ready

**[00:03:03] Transcript ≠ Clean Content**
A raw transcript contains filler, false starts, jokes that don't read well, self-corrected statements, and unverified references. Treating it as final truth produces low-quality or misleading derivative content.

**[00:03:40] What to Extract vs. What Needs Human Review**
- **Good transcript outputs**: episode summaries, chapter suggestions, quote candidates, social post options, searchability
- **Must review**: facts, links, sponsor language, names, affiliations, dates, claims

> *"A transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth."*

**[00:04:26] Lightweight but Reliable Pipeline for Small Teams**
- One folder per episode
- Clear naming convention
- Checklist with defined handoff points
- Fixed output bundle per episode: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, publish log

**[00:04:54] Raw vs. Clean Transcript**
Keep both. The raw transcript is for traceability and internal reference. The cleaned transcript — disfluencies removed, speaker labels fixed, readable formatting — is the preferred source for summarization and repurposing.

**[00:05:49] What Cannot Be Fully Automated**
Two places small teams overestimate automation:
1. **Editorial judgment**: an agent can propose titles, but can't know your audience positioning without encoded context
2. **Exception handling**: every few episodes brings a guest name correction, legal concern, sensitive anecdote, or sponsor constraint

> *"Use automation to accelerate repetitive transforms — not to abdicate responsibility for what goes live."*

Automation philosophy: **accelerate drafts, not approvals.**

**[00:06:52] Title Strategy**
Generate candidates from different angles — problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, curiosity-driven — then compare against your actual audience promise. Frequency is not strategy; the most repeated phrase is rarely the most compelling framing.

Title candidates discussed:
- *"How Small Teams Build a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline"*
- *"Why Podcast Production Breaks Even When the Recording Is Great"*
- *"From Raw Recording to Publish: The Systems Behind a Great Podcast"*

Standard output bundle per episode: three title candidates, one short description, one long description, final approved version logged before publish.

**[00:08:20] Five High-Value Pre-Publish Checks**
1. **Names and affiliations** — guest name, title, company spelled correctly
2. **Links and references** — every "we'll link that" is actually verified
3. **Sponsor compliance** — required language present where it should be
4. **Sensitive edits** — guest removal requests actually implemented
5. **Metadata consistency** — title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy all describe the same episode

**[00:09:13] Practical Takeaways for Small Shows**
1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. Create a publish checklist that one person owns
3. Separate raw transcript from cleaned transcript
4. Define the required output bundle for every episode
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to spot recurring delays, confusion, and errors

Bonus from the host: **start by automating drafts, not approvals.**

> *"Make the machine produce options. Make the human approve what becomes public."*
