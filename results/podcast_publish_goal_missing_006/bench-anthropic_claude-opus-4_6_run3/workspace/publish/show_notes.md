# Episode 14: The Podcast Production Pipeline — Show Notes

## Timestamps

### [00:00:00] Introduction & Framing
The host introduces the topic: how a podcast goes from raw recording to polished, published episode. The production pipeline shapes the entire listener experience.

### [00:00:27] Guest Introduction
Maya Chen joins the show. She advises media teams and creator businesses on production systems.

### [00:01:20] The Five Stages of a Podcast Pipeline
Maya breaks the pipeline into five stages:
1. **Capture** — recording + companion materials (bios, sponsor copy, links)
2. **Post-production** — audio cleanup, trimming, editorial decisions
3. **Content extraction** — transcripts, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapters, titles, clips
4. **Review & approval** — fact-checking, sponsor language, names, links, legal
5. **Distribution** — publishing to hosting, newsletter, social, archives

### [00:02:29] Where Small Teams Struggle
Common pain points: unclear handoffs, inconsistent file naming, and treating raw transcripts as publish-ready content.

### [00:03:03] Transcripts ≠ Content
Raw transcripts contain filler, false starts, unverified claims, and broken references. They are rich inputs for first drafts but not the final source of editorial truth.

### [00:04:15] A Lightweight Pipeline for Small Teams
Recommended setup: one folder per episode, clear naming conventions, a checklist with defined handoff points, and a fixed output bundle (final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapters, thumbnail, publish log).

### [00:04:54] Raw vs. Clean Transcripts
Keep both. The raw transcript preserves traceability; the cleaned version becomes the preferred source for summarization and repurposing.

### [00:05:49] Automation & Its Limits
Teams overestimate automation in editorial judgment and exception handling. Use automation to accelerate repetitive transforms — not to replace human approval of what goes public.

### [00:06:52] Titles as Listener Contracts
Generate multiple title candidates from different angles (problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, curiosity-driven). Frequency of a phrase is not strategy; good titles come from understanding what the episode helps the listener do.

### [00:08:13] Pre-Publish Review Checklist
Five highest-value checks: names/affiliations, links/references, sponsor compliance, sensitive edits, and metadata consistency across all surfaces.

### [00:09:13] Practical Takeaways
1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. Create a publish checklist owned by one person
3. Separate raw and cleaned transcripts
4. Define the required output bundle for every episode
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to spot delays and repeated errors
