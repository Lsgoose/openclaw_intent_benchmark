# Episode 14 – Show Notes
## The Podcast Production Pipeline

**Guest:** Maya Chen — media systems advisor to creator businesses and media teams

---

### [00:00:00] Introduction — The Pipeline Is the Product

The host frames the episode: a podcast's production pipeline shapes the entire listener experience. From raw recording to polished episode, every step — audio editing, transcription, chapter markers, social clips, titles, descriptions, and metadata — matters.

> "The pipeline is the product." — Maya Chen

---

### [00:01:20] The Five Stages of a Podcast Production Pipeline

Maya breaks down the standard pipeline:

1. **Capture** — Record the conversation; collect guest bios, sponsor copy, links, and references.
2. **Post-production** — Clean audio, trim mistakes, decide what stays.
3. **Content extraction** — Generate transcripts, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, and clips.
4. **Review and approval** — Fact-check names, links, sponsor language, and anything with editorial or legal risk.
5. **Distribution** — Deliver the final package to hosting platform, newsletter, social channels, and archives.

---

### [00:02:29] Where Small Teams Struggle First

Three common pain points:

- **Handoffs** — No single source of truth when recording, editing, and writing are split across people.
- **Inconsistent naming and asset storage** — Files like `final_v2_real_final.wav` erode confidence in which version is canonical.
- **Transcript-driven tasks** — Raw transcripts contain filler, false starts, unverified links, and corrected statements. Treating them as publish-ready copy leads to low-quality or misleading derivative content.

---

### [00:03:34] What to Extract from a Transcript — and What Still Needs Human Review

Transcripts are strong inputs for:
- Episode summaries (first drafts)
- Chapter suggestions
- Quote candidates
- Social post options
- Searchability and repurposing

Human review is still required for:
- Facts, links, sponsor language
- Names and affiliations
- Dates and specific claims

> "The transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth." — Maya Chen

---

### [00:04:15] Lightweight Pipeline Design for Small Teams

Recommended structure for a two- or three-person team:

- One folder per episode with a clear naming convention
- A checklist with defined handoff points
- A **standard output bundle** for every episode: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail (if used), and a publish log

**On transcripts:** Keep both raw and cleaned versions. The raw transcript preserves traceability; the cleaned transcript becomes the preferred source for summarization and repurposing.

---

### [00:05:49] Automation — Where Teams Overestimate It

Two areas where automation is commonly over-relied on:

1. **Editorial judgment** — An agent can propose titles, but it doesn't know your audience positioning unless that's been explicitly encoded.
2. **Exception handling** — Guest name corrections, legal review concerns, sensitive anecdotes, and sponsor placement constraints are edge cases that fully automated systems tend to fail on.

**Healthy mindset:** Use automation to accelerate repetitive transforms (transcription cleanup, first-draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, asset packaging, metadata prefill). Don't give up responsibility for what goes live.

> "Make the machine produce options. Make the human approve what becomes public." — Maya Chen

---

### [00:06:52] Titles — Strategy Over Frequency

Titles do significant work. Recommended approach:

- Generate several candidates from different angles: problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, curiosity-driven.
- Compare against your actual audience promise.
- Avoid picking the most-repeated phrase — frequency is not strategy.

Example title candidates for this episode:
- *How Small Teams Build a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline*
- *Why Podcast Production Breaks Even When the Recording Is Great*
- *From Raw Recording to Publish: The Systems Behind a Great Podcast*

Standard workflow output before publish: three title candidates, one short description, one long description, and a final approved version logged.

---

### [00:08:13] Pre-Publish Review Checklist

Five highest-value checks before an episode goes live:

1. **Names and affiliations** — Verify guest name, title, and company.
2. **Links and references** — Confirm every promised link resolves to the correct destination.
3. **Sponsor compliance** — Required sponsor language is present where it should be.
4. **Sensitive edits** — Anything the guest wanted removed is actually removed.
5. **Metadata consistency** — Title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy all describe the same episode.

---

### [00:09:13] Practical Takeaways

Steps to improve your pipeline this month:

1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention.
2. Create a publish checklist that one person owns.
3. Separate raw transcript and cleaned transcript.
4. Define the required output bundle for every episode.
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to surface where delays, confusion, or repeated errors occur.
6. If experimenting with automation: automate drafts, not approvals.
