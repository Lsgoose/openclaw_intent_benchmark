# Episode 14: Show Notes — Podcast Production Pipeline with Maya Chen

## [00:00:00] Introduction & Framing

The host introduces the topic: how a podcast goes from raw recording to polished episode. The production pipeline—not just the conversation—shapes the listener experience.

- Guest: [Maya Chen](https://creators.example.com/guests/maya-chen), advisor to media teams and creator businesses on production systems

## [00:01:20] The Five Stages of a Podcast Production Pipeline

Maya breaks down the pipeline into five stages:

1. **Capture** — recording plus companion materials (bios, sponsor copy, links, references)
2. **Post-production** — audio cleanup, trimming, editorial decisions
3. **Content extraction** — transcripts, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, clips
4. **Review and approval** — fact-checking, sponsor language, names, links, legal concerns
5. **Distribution** — publishing to hosting platform, newsletter, social channels, archives

## [00:02:29] Where Small Teams Struggle

Common pain points for small production teams:

- Handoff confusion with no single source of truth
- Inconsistent file naming and asset storage
- Treating raw transcripts as publish-ready content

## [00:03:03] Transcripts Are Inputs, Not Final Truth

Why raw transcripts should not be treated as clean structured truth:

- They contain filler, false starts, unverified links, and corrected statements
- Transcripts are excellent for first drafts of summaries, chapter suggestions, and quote candidates
- Anything authoritative (facts, links, names, dates, claims) still requires human review

## [00:04:15] A Lightweight Pipeline for Small Teams

Practical structure for a two-to-three person team:

- One folder or workspace per episode
- Clear naming convention
- Checklist with defined handoff points
- Standardized output bundle: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, publish log

## [00:04:54] Raw vs. Clean Transcripts

- Keep the raw transcript for traceability and internal reference
- Produce a cleaned transcript (disfluencies removed, speaker labels fixed, readable formatting)
- The cleaned version becomes the preferred source for summarization and repurposing

## [00:05:49] Automation and Its Limits

Where teams overestimate automation:

- **Editorial judgment** — agents can propose titles but lack audience positioning context
- **Exception handling** — guest name corrections, legal review, sensitive anecdotes, sponsor constraints

Healthy mindset: use automation for acceleration, not abdication.

## [00:06:52] Titles as Audience Contracts

- Generate multiple title candidates from different angles (problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, curiosity-driven)
- Compare against actual audience promise
- Frequency of a phrase is not strategy; good titles come from understanding listener intent

## [00:08:13] Pre-Publish Review Checklist

Five highest-value checks before going live:

1. Names and affiliations
2. Links and references — [Podcast Release Checklist](https://creators.example.com/checklists/podcast-release)
3. Sponsor compliance
4. Sensitive edits confirmed removed
5. Metadata consistency across title, description, transcript, chapters, and social copy

## [00:09:13] Practical Takeaways

Five actions for improving your pipeline this month:

1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. Create a publish checklist that one person owns
3. Separate raw transcript and cleaned transcript
4. Define the required output bundle for every episode
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to track delays and repeated errors
