# Episode 14 Show Notes

## [00:00:00] Why the production pipeline matters
The episode opens with the idea that a podcast production pipeline is not just back-office process. It directly shapes the listener experience through audio quality, transcripts, metadata, chapter markers, descriptions, and distribution consistency.

## [00:01:20] The five stages of a reliable pipeline
Maya Chen breaks the workflow into five stages:
- Capture
- Post-production
- Content extraction
- Review and approval
- Distribution

She compares the process to a software build pipeline: source material, transformations, validation, packaging, and deployment.

## [00:02:29] Where small teams break first
The first weak points are usually:
- messy handoffs between people
- no single source of truth
- inconsistent file naming and storage
- assuming transcript-driven tasks are automatically publish-ready

## [00:03:03] Why a transcript is not editorial truth
A major theme in this section: transcripts are useful, but they are not clean structured truth by default. Maya explains that transcripts often contain filler, false starts, corrections, and unverified references. Her key framing: the transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth.

## [00:04:15] A lightweight pipeline for small teams
For teams of two or three, Maya recommends keeping the system simple:
- one folder or workspace per episode
- a clear naming convention
- a checklist with defined handoff points
- a fixed output bundle for every episode

That output bundle should consistently include final audio, a clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail if used, and a publish log.

## [00:04:54] Raw transcript vs. clean transcript
The conversation then focuses on why both transcript versions matter:
- raw transcript for traceability and wording verification
- cleaned transcript for summarization, repurposing, and readable downstream work

Separating the two reduces noise and improves the quality of anything built from the transcript later.

## [00:05:49] What automation can and cannot own
The episode shifts to automation and agent workflows. Maya argues that teams often overestimate automation in two places:
- editorial judgment
- exception handling

Automation is best used for repetitive transforms such as transcript cleanup, first-draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, asset packaging, and metadata prefills. Human review still owns correctness, tone, and release quality.

## [00:06:52] Better episode titles
Maya recommends generating multiple title candidates from different angles:
- problem-oriented
- guest-oriented
- outcome-oriented
- curiosity-driven

The point is not to choose the most repeated phrase in the transcript. The point is to match the audience promise and help the listener understand what the episode will do for them.

## [00:08:13] The highest-value pre-publish checks
Before an episode goes live, Maya prioritizes five review checks:
- names and affiliations
- links and references
- sponsor compliance
- sensitive edits
- metadata consistency across all surfaces

## [00:09:13] The practical improvement plan
To improve a production pipeline this month, the episode recommends:
1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention.
2. Create a publish checklist with clear ownership.
3. Separate raw and cleaned transcripts.
4. Define a required output bundle for every episode.
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to spot delays and repeated errors.

The host adds one more principle: automate drafts, not approvals.
