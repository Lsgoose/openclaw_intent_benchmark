# Episode 14 — Building a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline

**Guest:** Maya Chen, media production systems advisor

---

## Timestamps

### [00:00:00] Introduction
Welcome and framing: why the production pipeline shapes the listener experience, not just the conversation itself.

### [00:01:20] The Five Stages of a Podcast Production Pipeline
Maya breaks down the full pipeline:
1. **Capture** — recording plus companion materials (guest bios, sponsor copy, links, references)
2. **Post-production** — audio cleanup, trimming, editorial decisions
3. **Content extraction** — transcripts, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, clips
4. **Review and approval** — fact-checking, sponsor language, names, links, editorial and legal review
5. **Distribution** — hosting platform, newsletter, social channels, archives

### [00:02:29] Where Small Teams Struggle First
Three common pain points:
- **Handoffs** — no single source of truth when multiple people own different stages
- **Inconsistent naming and asset storage** — version confusion ("final_v2_real_final.wav")
- **Transcript overconfidence** — treating a raw transcript as publish-ready copy

### [00:03:03] Transcripts: Rich Input, Not Final Truth
Raw transcripts contain filler, false starts, uncorrected statements, and unverified references. They are excellent first-draft inputs for summaries, chapter suggestions, quote candidates, and social options — but anything authoritative (facts, links, names, dates, claims) still requires human review.

> *"The transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth."* — Maya Chen

### [00:04:15] Lightweight Pipeline Design for Small Teams
Practical structure for a two- or three-person team:
- One folder per episode with a clear naming convention
- A checklist with defined handoff points
- A **standard output bundle** for every episode: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, publish log

### [00:04:54] Raw vs. Clean Transcripts
Keep both:
- **Raw transcript** — traceability and internal reference
- **Clean transcript** — disfluencies removed, speaker labels fixed, formatted for readability; this becomes the preferred source for summarization and repurposing

### [00:05:49] Automation: Acceleration, Not Abdication
Where teams overestimate automation:
- **Editorial judgment** — agents can propose titles but don't know audience positioning unless it's encoded
- **Exception handling** — guest name corrections, legal concerns, sensitive anecdotes, sponsor constraints

Healthy use of automation: transcription cleanup, first-draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, asset packaging, metadata prefill. A human still owns correctness, tone, and release quality.

### [00:06:52] Titles: A Contract with the Listener
Generate multiple candidates from different angles — problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, curiosity-driven — then evaluate against your audience promise. Frequency of a phrase in the transcript is not a title strategy.

Example framings for this episode:
- "How Small Teams Build a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline"
- "Why Podcast Production Breaks Even When the Recording Is Great"
- "From Raw Recording to Publish: The Systems Behind a Great Podcast"

### [00:08:13] Pre-Publish Review: Five High-Value Checks
1. **Names and affiliations** — guest name, title, company
2. **Links and references** — verify every destination mentioned
3. **Sponsor compliance** — required language present in the right places
4. **Sensitive edits** — confirm removals the guest requested
5. **Metadata consistency** — title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy all describe the same episode

### [00:09:13] Practical Takeaways
Steps to improve your pipeline this month:
1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. Create a publish checklist with a single owner
3. Separate raw and clean transcripts
4. Define the required output bundle for every episode
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to surface delays and repeated errors

**Bonus:** If experimenting with automation, start by automating drafts — not approvals.
