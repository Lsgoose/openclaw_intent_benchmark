# Show Notes — Ep. 14: The Podcast Production Pipeline (with Maya Chen)

Maya Chen, a production systems advisor for media teams and creator businesses, joins the show to break down what a reliable podcast pipeline actually looks like — from raw recording to published episode.

---

## [00:00:00] Introduction & Why the Pipeline Is the Product

The host frames the episode: everything that happens *after* the recording — editing, transcription, chapter markers, metadata, distribution — shapes the listener experience just as much as the conversation itself. Maya's opening observation sets the tone: "The pipeline is the product."

---

## [00:01:20] The Five Stages of a Podcast Production Pipeline

Maya outlines the five core stages:

1. **Capture** — recording plus companion materials (guest bios, sponsor copy, links, references)
2. **Post-production** — audio cleanup, trimming, editorial decisions
3. **Content extraction** — transcripts, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, clips
4. **Review & approval** — fact-checking, sponsor language, names, links, editorial/legal review
5. **Distribution** — hosting platform, newsletter, social channels, archives

The software build pipeline analogy: source material → transformations → validation → packaging → deployment.

---

## [00:02:29] Where Small Teams Break Down

Three common pain points:

- **Handoffs without a single source of truth** — one person records, another edits, someone else writes the description, and nothing is coordinated
- **Inconsistent naming and asset storage** — "final_v2_real_final.wav" syndrome
- **Assuming transcript = publish-ready content** — raw transcripts contain filler, false starts, unverified links, and statements that were corrected later in the conversation

---

## [00:03:34] What to Extract from a Transcript — and What Still Needs Human Review

Transcripts are strong inputs for:
- Episode summaries (first drafts)
- Chapter suggestions
- Quote candidates
- Social post options
- Searchability and repurposing

Still requires human review:
- Facts, links, sponsor language
- Names and affiliations
- Dates and specific claims

> "The transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth." — Maya Chen

---

## [00:04:15] A Lightweight Pipeline for Small Teams (2–3 People)

Practical structure:
- One folder per episode with a clear naming convention
- A checklist with defined handoff points owned by one person
- A **standard output bundle** for every episode: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, publish log

On transcripts: keep **both** raw and cleaned versions. Raw = traceability and verification. Clean = preferred source for summarization and repurposing.

---

## [00:05:49] Automation: Where Teams Overestimate It

Two areas where automation tends to fall short:

1. **Editorial judgment** — an agent can propose titles, but it doesn't know your audience positioning unless that's been encoded somewhere
2. **Exception handling** — guest name corrections, legal review concerns, sensitive anecdotes, sponsor placement constraints; automated systems fail on edge cases, and media teams live on edge cases

The healthy mindset: **use automation to accelerate, not to abdicate**. Automate drafts, not approvals.

---

## [00:06:52] Titles: A Contract with the Listener

Generate candidates from multiple angles:
- Problem-oriented: *"Why Podcast Production Breaks Even When the Recording Is Great"*
- Guest-oriented
- Outcome-oriented: *"From Raw Recording to Publish: The Systems Behind a Great Podcast"*
- Curiosity-driven: *"How Small Teams Build a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline"*

> "Frequency is not strategy. The most repeated phrase in a conversation is often not the most compelling framing."

Recommended workflow: three title candidates + one short description + one long description, with a final approved version logged before publish.

---

## [00:08:13] The Five Highest-Value Pre-Publish Checks

1. **Names and affiliations** — get the guest's name, title, and company right
2. **Links and references** — verify every destination that was promised in the episode
3. **Sponsor compliance** — confirm required language is present
4. **Sensitive edits** — confirm anything the guest wanted removed is actually gone
5. **Metadata consistency** — title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy should all describe the same episode

---

## [00:09:13] Practical Takeaways: Improve Your Pipeline This Month

Maya's five-step starter list:
1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. Create a publish checklist that one person owns
3. Separate raw transcript from cleaned transcript
4. Define the required output bundle for every episode
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to surface where delays and repeated errors occur

Host addition: **if you're experimenting with automation, start by automating drafts, not approvals.**
