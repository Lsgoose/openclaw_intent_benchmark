# Episode 14: Building a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline

## Timestamps

### [00:00:00] Introduction
Today we unpack how a podcast goes from raw recording to polished episode, covering the entire production pipeline that shapes the listener experience beyond just the conversation itself.

Guest: Maya Chen, production systems advisor for media teams and creator businesses.
- Maya Chen bio: <https://creators.example.com/guests/maya-chen>

### [00:01:20] Pipeline Core Stages
The five core stages of a production pipeline, analogous to software build pipelines:
1. **Capture**: Record conversation and collect companion materials (guest bios, sponsor copy, links)
2. **Post-production**: Clean audio, trim mistakes, finalize episode content
3. **Content extraction**: Generate transcripts, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, clips
4. **Review and approval**: Fact-checking, sponsor language validation, legal review
5. **Distribution**: Publish final package to hosting platforms, newsletters, social channels, archives

### [00:02:29] Common Small Team Pain Points
Three most frequent failure points for small teams:
1. Poor handoffs between team members without a single source of truth
2. Inconsistent naming and asset storage leading to version confusion
3. Overreliance on raw transcripts as publish-ready content

Transcripts are "rich input, not final truth" — they can contain filler, false starts, corrected statements, and unvetted references that require human review before being used for public content.

### [00:04:26] Lightweight Pipeline for Small Teams
For teams of 2-3 people, a reliable pipeline requires:
- One dedicated folder/workspace per episode
- Clear naming convention for all assets
- Defined handoff points with a shared checklist
- Standardized output bundle (final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, publish log)
- Separate raw and cleaned transcripts: keep raw for traceability, use cleaned for summarization and repurposing

### [00:05:49] Automation Best Practices
Where teams usually overestimate automation:
1. Editorial judgment (AI doesn't inherently understand your audience positioning)
2. Exception handling (edge cases like name corrections, legal concerns, and sponsor constraints break fully automated systems)

Healthy mindset: Use automation for acceleration, not abdication. Automate repetitive tasks (transcript cleanup, first-draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, metadata prefill) but keep human ownership of public-facing content correctness, tone, and quality.

- Podcast release checklist template: <https://creators.example.com/checklists/podcast-release>

### [00:06:52] Title Strategy
Titles are contracts with listeners, not just labels. Generate multiple candidates from different angles:
- Problem-oriented
- Guest-oriented
- Outcome-oriented
- Curiosity-driven

Avoid relying on frequency of phrases in transcripts — good titles come from understanding what the episode helps listeners do, not just what's mentioned most.

### [00:08:13] Pre-Publish Quality Checks
Five highest-value checks before going live:
1. Correct names and affiliations for guests and contributors
2. Verified links and references (confirm all mentioned links point to the right destination)
3. Sponsor compliance (required language included in correct positions)
4. Sensitive edits confirmed (all requested removals are complete)
5. Metadata consistency across title, description, transcript, chapters, and social copy

### [00:09:13] Actionable First Steps
If you want to improve your pipeline this month:
1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. Create a publish checklist owned by one person
3. Separate raw and cleaned transcript workflows
4. Define your required output bundle for every episode
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to track delays and repeated errors
