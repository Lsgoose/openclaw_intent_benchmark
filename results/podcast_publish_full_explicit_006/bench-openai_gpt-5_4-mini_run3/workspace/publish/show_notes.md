[00:00:00] Episode overview
The episode opens by framing the podcast production pipeline as the system that shapes the listener experience: moving from raw recording to polished episode assets, including clean audio, transcript, chapter markers, social clips, title, description, and metadata.

[00:00:27] Guest introduction
Maya Chen joins to discuss production systems for media teams and creator businesses.

[00:00:36] Why the pipeline matters
The pipeline is described as the product. Small failures in titles, transcripts, chapter markers, or publishing checklists compound quickly.

[00:01:20] Major pipeline stages
The five stages covered are capture, post-production, content extraction, review and approval, and distribution.

[00:02:29] Common small-team failures
Small teams often struggle with handoffs, inconsistent file naming, scattered asset storage, and the false assumption that transcript-driven work is automatically ready to publish.

[00:03:03] Transcript as input, not truth
A transcript can contain filler, corrections, unfinished thoughts, and unverified references. It is useful for drafts, but facts, links, sponsor language, names, dates, and claims still need human review.

[00:04:26] A lightweight reliable workflow
A practical setup uses one episode workspace, clear naming conventions, a defined checklist, and a fixed output bundle: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, and publish log.

[00:04:54] Raw transcript vs clean transcript
Keeping both versions is recommended. The raw transcript preserves traceability, while the cleaned transcript becomes the preferred source for summaries and repurposing.

[00:05:49] Where automation helps and where it fails
Automation is strongest for repetitive transforms like cleanup, first-draft notes, formatting, chapter suggestions, and metadata prefill. It is weaker at editorial judgment and exception handling.

[00:06:52] Title strategy
Good titles should be generated from multiple angles: problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, and curiosity-driven. Frequency alone is not strategy.

[00:08:13] Pre-publish review checks
The highest-value checks are names and affiliations, links and references, sponsor compliance, sensitive edits, and metadata consistency across title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy.

[00:09:13] Practical takeaways
The recommended improvements for a small show are a standard folder structure, a publish checklist with one owner, raw/clean transcript separation, a required output bundle, and a lightweight episode log. Automation should draft, not approve.
