# Episode 14: Building a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline

## Show Notes

### [00:00:00] Introduction to podcast production pipelines
Host introduces the topic of podcast production pipelines and how they shape the listener experience. Guest Maya Chen joins to discuss why the pipeline is the product, not just the conversation itself.

### [00:01:20] Defining the five stages of production
Maya breaks down podcast production into five key stages:
1. **Capture** - Recording and collecting companion materials
2. **Post-production** - Audio cleaning and editing
3. **Content extraction** - Transcripts, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapters, titles, clips
4. **Review and approval** - Fact-checking, sponsor compliance, legal review
5. **Distribution** - Publishing to hosting platform, newsletter, social channels, archives

### [00:02:29] Common pain points for small teams
Three main challenges:
1. **Handoffs** - Lack of single source of truth between team members
2. **Inconsistent naming and storage** - Files like "final_v2_real_final.wav"
3. **Transcript-driven tasks requiring judgment** - Raw transcripts aren't automatically publish-ready

### [00:03:34] Transcripts as rich input vs final truth
Key insight: "The transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth."
- Transcripts are excellent for first drafts of summaries, chapter suggestions, quotes, and social posts
- Anything authoritative (facts, links, sponsor language, names, claims) needs human review
- Recommendation: Keep both raw transcript (for traceability) and cleaned transcript (for repurposing)

### [00:05:49] Automation pitfalls and healthy mindset
Where teams overestimate automation:
1. **Editorial judgment** - Agents can propose titles but don't know audience positioning
2. **Exception handling** - Edge cases (name corrections, legal concerns, sensitive content) break fully automated systems

Healthy approach: "Use automation to accelerate repetitive transforms, not abdicate responsibility."

### [00:06:52] Title strategy and examples
Titles do a lot of work - they're "a contract with the listener."
Generate multiple title candidates from different angles:
- Problem-oriented: "Why Podcast Production Breaks Even When the Recording Is Great"
- Guest-oriented: "Maya Chen on Reliable Podcast Production Systems"
- Outcome-oriented: "How Small Teams Build a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline"
- Curiosity-driven: "From Raw Recording to Publish: The Systems Behind a Great Podcast"

### [00:08:03] Critical review checks before publishing
Five high-value checks:
1. **Names and affiliations** - Get guest details right
2. **Links and references** - Verify all mentioned destinations
3. **Sponsor compliance** - Ensure required language is present
4. **Sensitive edits** - Confirm requested removals are done
5. **Metadata consistency** - Title, description, transcript, chapters, social copy should all align

### [00:09:02] Practical takeaways for small teams
Maya's five-step improvement plan:
1. Define standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. Create a publish checklist with clear ownership
3. Separate raw transcript from cleaned transcript
4. Define required output bundle for every episode
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to track delays and errors

Host's addition: "If you're experimenting with automation, start by automating drafts, not approvals."
