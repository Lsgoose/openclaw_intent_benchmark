## [00:01:20] The Five Stages of a Production Pipeline

1. **Capture** — recording + companion materials (guest bios, sponsor copy, links, references)
2. **Post-production** — clean audio, trim mistakes, editorial decisions
3. **Content extraction** — transcript, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, clips
4. **Review and approval** — facts, sponsor language, names, links, editorial/legal checks
5. **Distribution** — hosting platform, newsletter, social channels, archives

*Analogy: like a software build pipeline — source material, transformations, validation, packaging, deployment.*

---

## [00:02:29] Where Small Teams Struggle Most

- **Handoffs** — no single source of truth when multiple people touch the episode
- **Inconsistent naming** — "final_v2_real_final.wav" chaos
- **Transcript as truth** — raw transcripts contain filler, false starts, unverified claims, and links that were never actually confirmed

*"A transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth."* — Maya Chen

---

## [00:04:15] Lightweight but Reliable Pipeline for Small Teams

- One folder per episode with a clear naming convention
- A defined publish checklist with owned handoff points
- A fixed output bundle for every episode: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, and publish log
- Keep raw and cleaned transcripts separate

---

## [00:05:49] What Can and Can't Be Automated

**Overestimated:**
- Editorial judgment — agents can propose titles but can't know your audience positioning unless you've encoded it
- Exception handling — guest corrections, legal reviews, sponsor constraints, sensitive anecdotes

**Healthy mindset:** Use automation to accelerate drafts, not approvals. *"Make the machine produce options. Make the human approve what becomes public."*

---

## [00:06:52] Titles Do Real Work

Generate candidates from different angles:
- Problem-oriented
- Guest-oriented
- Outcome-oriented
- Curiosity-driven

*Frequency is not strategy.* The most repeated phrase in a conversation is rarely the most compelling framing.

**Pre-publish output bundle:** three title candidates, one short description, one long description, final approved version logged.

---

## [00:08:13] High-Value Pre-Publish Checks

1. **Names and affiliations** — correct guest name, title, company
2. **Links and references** — verify every destination the episode promises
3. **Sponsor compliance** — required language present where required
4. **Sensitive edits** — guest-requested removals actually removed
5. **Metadata consistency** — title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy all describe the same episode

---

## [00:09:13] Practical Takeaways

**Start here this month:**
1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. Create a publish checklist owned by one person
3. Separate raw transcript and cleaned transcript
4. Define the required output bundle for every episode
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to spot delays, confusion, and repeated errors

**Automation rule:** Automate drafts, not approvals.
