# Episode 14 Show Notes

## [00:00:00] Opening: why the pipeline matters
The episode frames podcast production as a full pipeline, not just a recording. The listener experience depends on how audio, transcript, chapters, clips, metadata, and distribution all work together.

## [00:00:27] Guest intro and core thesis
Maya Chen joins to argue that the pipeline is the product: when one piece breaks, the whole release feels unreliable.

## [00:01:20] The five stages of production
The conversation breaks the workflow into five stages: capture, post-production, content extraction, review and approval, and distribution.

## [00:02:29] Common failure points in small teams
Small teams struggle with handoffs, inconsistent naming and storage, and assuming transcript-driven work is automatically ready to publish.

## [00:03:09] Why transcripts are inputs, not final truth
Raw transcripts contain filler, false starts, corrections, and unverified references. They can seed drafts, but facts, names, dates, sponsor language, and links still need review.

## [00:04:26] Building a lightweight reliable pipeline
A small team benefits from a clear episode folder, consistent naming, a defined checklist, and a fixed output bundle that includes final audio, cleaned transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, and publish log.

## [00:04:59] Raw transcript vs cleaned transcript
Keep both. The raw transcript supports traceability; the cleaned transcript becomes the preferred source for summaries, repurposing, and further automation.

## [00:05:57] Where automation helps and where it fails
Automation is best for repetitive transforms like transcription cleanup, first-draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, and metadata prefill. Editorial judgment and exception handling still need humans.

## [00:06:57] Titles as a contract with the listener
The guest recommends generating several title angles: problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, and curiosity-driven. Frequency is not strategy.

## [00:08:13] Review checks before publish
The highest-value checks are names and affiliations, links and references, sponsor compliance, sensitive edits, and metadata consistency across title, description, transcript, chapters, and social copy.

## [00:09:23] Practical takeaways for small teams
Standardize folder structure, assign checklist ownership, separate raw and cleaned transcripts, define a required output bundle, and keep a lightweight episode log. Automate drafts, not approvals.

## [00:10:05] Sponsor and close
The episode closes with a sponsor mention for SoundLayer and a reminder to share the episode with anyone handling editing, publishing, or content operations.
