# Episode 14 – Building a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline

**Guest:** Maya Chen, media production systems advisor

---

## Timestamped Sections

### [00:00:00] Introduction
Welcome and framing: the podcast production pipeline shapes the entire listener experience, from raw recording to polished, distributed episode.

### [00:00:36] The Pipeline Is the Product
Maya Chen joins to discuss why production systems matter — and why teams only notice them when something breaks.

### [00:01:20] The Five Stages of a Podcast Production Pipeline
1. **Capture** — Recording plus companion materials (guest bios, sponsor copy, links, references)
2. **Post-production** — Audio cleanup, trimming, editorial decisions
3. **Content extraction** — Transcripts, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, clips
4. **Review and approval** — Fact-checking, sponsor language, names, links, editorial/legal review
5. **Distribution** — Final package to hosting platform, newsletter, social channels, archives

### [00:02:07] The Software Build Analogy
A podcast pipeline mirrors a software build: source material → transformations → validation → packaging → deployment. Pipelines become fragile when they depend on memory and heroics.

### [00:02:29] Where Small Teams Struggle First
Three common pain points:
- **Handoffs** — No single source of truth across roles
- **Inconsistent naming and asset storage** — "final_v2_real_final.wav" syndrome
- **Transcript-driven tasks** — Raw transcripts are not automatically publish-ready copy

### [00:03:03] Transcripts Are Rich Inputs, Not Final Truth
Raw transcripts contain filler, false starts, unverified links, and corrected statements. Treating them as clean structured truth leads to low-quality or misleading derivative content.

### [00:03:40] What to Extract vs. What Needs Human Review
- **Good transcript inputs:** First-draft summaries, chapter suggestions, quote candidates, social post options, searchability
- **Always needs review:** Facts, links, sponsor language, names, affiliations, dates, authoritative claims

### [00:04:15] Lightweight Pipeline for Small Teams (2–3 People)
- One folder/workspace per episode
- Clear naming convention
- Checklist with defined handoff points
- Fixed output bundle per episode: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, publish log

### [00:04:54] Raw vs. Clean Transcript
Keep both:
- **Raw transcript** — for traceability and internal reference
- **Clean transcript** — disfluencies removed, speaker labels fixed, formatted for readability; preferred source for summarization and repurposing

### [00:05:49] Where Teams Overestimate Automation
1. **Editorial judgment** — Agents can propose titles but don't know audience positioning unless it's encoded
2. **Exception handling** — Edge cases (name corrections, legal concerns, sensitive anecdotes, sponsor constraints) require human judgment

### [00:06:26] Automation for Acceleration, Not Abdication
Use automation for: transcription cleanup, first-draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, asset packaging, metadata prefill. Human ownership remains essential for correctness, tone, and release quality.

### [00:06:52] Crafting Titles That Work
Generate multiple candidates from different angles:
- Problem-oriented
- Guest-oriented
- Outcome-oriented
- Curiosity-driven

A title is a contract with the listener — frequency of phrases in the transcript is not a strategy.

### [00:08:13] Pre-Publish Review Checklist
Five highest-value checks before going live:
1. Names and affiliations — verify guest name, title, company
2. Links and references — confirm actual destinations
3. Sponsor compliance — required language present and correctly placed
4. Sensitive edits — confirm removed content is actually removed
5. Metadata consistency — title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy all describe the same episode

### [00:09:13] Practical Takeaways for Small Shows
1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. Create a publish checklist with a single owner
3. Separate raw and clean transcripts
4. Define the required output bundle for every episode
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to surface delays and repeated errors
6. *(Host addition)* When experimenting with automation, automate drafts — not approvals
