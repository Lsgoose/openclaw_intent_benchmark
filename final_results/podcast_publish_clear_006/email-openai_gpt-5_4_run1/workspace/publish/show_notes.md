# Episode 14 Show Notes

## [00:00] Why the production pipeline matters
The episode opens by reframing podcast production as part of the listener experience, not just back-office operations. The conversation tracks how a show moves from raw recording to polished release, including audio cleanup, transcripts, chapter markers, descriptions, social assets, and metadata.

## [00:01] The five stages of a reliable podcast pipeline
Maya Chen breaks the workflow into five stages:
- capture
- post-production
- content extraction
- review and approval
- distribution

This framing treats podcast operations like a media build pipeline, with source material, transformations, validation, packaging, and deployment.

## [00:02] Where small teams usually break down
The first common failures are handoffs, inconsistent naming, and weak asset storage. When nobody owns a single source of truth, teams lose confidence in which files are current and mistakes start compounding.

## [00:03] Why transcripts are useful but not editorial truth
A raw transcript can include filler, false starts, outdated statements, and unverified references. The core takeaway: a transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth. It can drive summaries, chapter suggestions, quote candidates, and repurposing drafts, but facts, names, links, dates, affiliations, and sponsor language still need human review.

## [00:04] Clean transcript vs. raw transcript
The recommendation is to keep both:
- a raw transcript for traceability and wording checks
- a cleaned transcript for readable internal use, summarization, and downstream content generation

Separating the two reduces noise and improves the quality of derivative assets.

## [00:05] What a lightweight, reliable setup looks like
For a small team, Maya recommends:
- one folder or workspace per episode
- a clear naming convention
- a checklist with defined handoff points
- a fixed output bundle for every episode

That output bundle should consistently include final audio, a clean transcript, show notes, a title, a description, a chapter list, artwork if used, and a publish log.

## [00:05] Where automation helps—and where it does not
Automation works best for repetitive transforms: transcription cleanup, first-draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, asset packaging, and metadata prefills. It is much weaker at editorial judgment and exception handling, especially when episodes involve sensitive anecdotes, sponsor constraints, legal concerns, or corrections.

The operating principle is simple: use automation for acceleration, not abdication.

## [00:06] How to think about episode titles
Title generation should explore multiple angles, such as:
- problem-oriented
- guest-oriented
- outcome-oriented
- curiosity-driven

The point is not to mirror the transcript mechanically. A title is a contract with the listener, so it should reflect the actual audience promise rather than just the most repeated phrase.

## [00:08] The highest-value pre-publish review checks
The conversation highlights five checks before release:
- names and affiliations
- links and references
- sponsor compliance
- sensitive edits
- metadata consistency

Metadata consistency matters across every public surface, including the title, description, transcript, chapters, and social copy.

## [00:09] Practical first steps for improving a small-show pipeline
If a team wants to improve within a month, the suggested starting list is:
- define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention
- create a publish checklist with a single owner
- separate raw and cleaned transcripts
- define the required output bundle for every episode
- keep a lightweight episode log to spot delays and repeated errors

The broader message is that meaningful gains come from consistency more than complexity.
