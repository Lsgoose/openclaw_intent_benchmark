# Episode 14 — The Podcast Production Pipeline with Maya Chen

## [00:00:00] Introduction: The Pipeline Is the Product
The host introduces the episode's topic: the podcast production pipeline — the system that turns a raw recording into a polished, discoverable episode. Maya Chen joins the show to break down why production discipline matters as much as the conversation itself.

## [00:01:20] The Five Stages of a Production Pipeline
Maya outlines five core stages:
1. **Capture** — recording and collecting companion materials (bios, sponsor copy, links, references).
2. **Post-production** — cleaning audio, trimming mistakes, deciding what stays.
3. **Content extraction** — generating transcripts, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, and clips.
4. **Review and approval** — fact-checking, verifying sponsor language, names, links, and editorial/legal concerns.
5. **Distribution** — publishing to hosting platforms, newsletters, social channels, and archives.

## [00:02:29] Where Small Teams Struggle First
Three common pain points: handoffs between team members without a single source of truth, inconsistent naming and asset storage, and the misconception that raw transcripts are automatically publish-ready content.

## [00:03:03] Transcripts: Rich Input, Not Final Truth
Transcripts are excellent starting points for summaries, chapter suggestions, quote candidates, and social posts. But anything authoritative — facts, links, sponsor language, names, dates, claims — still requires human review. The transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth.

## [00:04:15] A Lightweight Pipeline for Small Teams
For teams of two or three: one folder per episode, clear naming conventions, a checklist with defined handoff points, and a fixed output bundle (final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, publish log).

## [00:04:54] Raw vs. Clean Transcripts
Keep both. The raw transcript provides traceability. The cleaned transcript — with disfluencies removed, speaker labels fixed, and formatting applied — becomes the preferred source for summarization and repurposing.

## [00:05:49] Where Automation Falls Short
Teams overestimate automation in two areas: editorial judgment (an agent doesn't know your audience positioning) and exception handling (edge cases like guest name corrections, legal reviews, sensitive anecdotes). Use automation to accelerate repetitive transforms, not to abdicate responsibility.

## [00:06:52] Title Strategy: A Contract with the Listener
Generate multiple title candidates from different angles — problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, curiosity-driven — then compare against your audience promise. Frequency is not strategy; the most repeated phrase is rarely the most compelling framing.

## [00:08:13] The Five Highest-Value Pre-Publish Checks
1. Names and affiliations
2. Links and references
3. Sponsor compliance
4. Sensitive edits
5. Metadata consistency across all surfaces

## [00:09:13] Practical Takeaways
Define a standard folder structure and naming convention. Create a publish checklist owned by one person. Separate raw and cleaned transcripts. Define a required output bundle. Keep a lightweight episode log to track delays and repeated errors. Automate drafts, not approvals.
