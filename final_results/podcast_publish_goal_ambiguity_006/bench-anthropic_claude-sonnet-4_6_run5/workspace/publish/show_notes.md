# Show Notes — Ep. 14: The Podcast Production Pipeline (with Maya Chen)

Maya Chen, who advises media teams and creator businesses on production systems, joins the show to break down what a reliable podcast pipeline actually looks like — from raw recording to published episode.

---

## [00:00:00] Introduction

The host frames the episode: a podcast's production pipeline shapes the entire listener experience. Maya's opening observation sets the tone — "the pipeline is the product."

---

## [00:01:20] The Five Stages of a Podcast Production Pipeline

Maya outlines the five core stages:

1. **Capture** — recording plus companion materials (guest bios, sponsor copy, links, references)
2. **Post-production** — audio cleanup, trimming, editorial decisions
3. **Content extraction** — transcripts, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, clips
4. **Review and approval** — fact-checking, sponsor language, names, links, editorial/legal review
5. **Distribution** — hosting platform, newsletter, social channels, archives

The software build pipeline analogy: source material → transformations → validation → packaging → deployment.

---

## [00:02:29] Where Small Teams Struggle First

Three common pain points:

- **Handoffs** — no single source of truth when multiple people own different stages
- **Inconsistent naming and asset storage** — "final_v2_real_final.wav" syndrome
- **Transcript-driven tasks** — raw transcripts are not publish-ready copy

---

## [00:03:03] Transcripts: Rich Input, Not Final Truth

A raw transcript can contain filler, false starts, jokes that don't read well, corrected statements, and unverified links. Treating it as clean structured truth leads to low-quality or misleading derivative content.

**What transcripts are good for:** first drafts of summaries, chapter suggestions, quote candidates, social post options, searchability, repurposing.

**What still needs human review:** facts, links, sponsor language, names, affiliations, dates, and any authoritative claims.

> "The transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth." — Maya Chen

---

## [00:04:15] A Lightweight Pipeline for Small Teams

For a two- or three-person team, Maya recommends:

- One folder or workspace per episode
- A clear naming convention
- A checklist with defined handoff points
- A **fixed output bundle** for every episode: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail (if used), and a publish log

**On transcripts:** keep both raw and cleaned versions. The raw transcript is for traceability; the cleaned transcript is the preferred source for summarization and repurposing.

---

## [00:05:49] Automation: Where Teams Overestimate It

Two areas where automation is commonly over-trusted:

1. **Editorial judgment** — an agent can propose titles, but it doesn't know your audience positioning unless that's been encoded somewhere
2. **Exception handling** — guest name corrections, legal review concerns, sensitive anecdotes, sponsor placement constraints; automated systems tend to fail on edge cases

The healthy mindset: **use automation to accelerate, not abdicate**. Automate repetitive transforms (transcription cleanup, first-draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, asset packaging, metadata prefill). Keep a human responsible for correctness, tone, and release quality.

---

## [00:06:52] Titles: A Contract with the Listener

Generate several candidates from different angles:

- Problem-oriented: *"Why Podcast Production Breaks Even When the Recording Is Great"*
- Guest-oriented
- Outcome-oriented: *"From Raw Recording to Publish: The Systems Behind a Great Podcast"*
- Curiosity-driven: *"How Small Teams Build a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline"*

Then compare against your actual audience promise. Frequency of a phrase in the transcript is not strategy — the best title comes from understanding what the episode is helping the listener do.

---

## [00:08:13] Pre-Publish Review: The Five Highest-Value Checks

1. **Names and affiliations** — get the guest's name, title, and company right
2. **Links and references** — verify every destination that was promised in the episode
3. **Sponsor compliance** — confirm required language is present where it should be
4. **Sensitive edits** — confirm anything the guest wanted removed is actually gone
5. **Metadata consistency** — title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy should all describe the same episode

---

## [00:09:13] Practical Takeaways

If you run a small show and want to improve your pipeline this month:

1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. Create a publish checklist that one person owns
3. Separate raw transcript and cleaned transcript
4. Define the required output bundle for every episode
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to surface delays, confusion, and repeated errors

Host addition: **if you're experimenting with automation, start by automating drafts, not approvals.**

> "Make the machine produce options. Make the human approve what becomes public." — Maya Chen
