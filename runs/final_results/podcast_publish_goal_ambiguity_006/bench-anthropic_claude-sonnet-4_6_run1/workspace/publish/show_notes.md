# Show Notes — Ep. 14: The Podcast Production Pipeline (with Maya Chen)

Maya Chen, advisor to media teams and creator businesses, joins the show to break down what a reliable podcast production pipeline actually looks like — and where small teams most often go wrong.

---

## [00:00:00] Introduction & Why the Pipeline Is the Product

The host frames the episode: a podcast isn't just the conversation — it's everything that turns a raw recording into a polished, discoverable episode. Maya's opening observation sets the tone: *"People don't always notice until something breaks, and then suddenly everyone realizes the pipeline is the product."*

---

## [00:01:20] The Five Stages of a Podcast Production Pipeline

Maya breaks the pipeline into five distinct stages:

1. **Capture** — recording plus companion materials (guest bios, sponsor copy, links, references)
2. **Post-production** — audio cleanup, trimming, editorial decisions
3. **Content extraction** — transcripts, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, clips
4. **Review and approval** — fact-checking, sponsor language, names, links, editorial/legal review
5. **Distribution** — hosting platform, newsletter, social channels, archives

The analogy to a software build pipeline comes up: source material → transformations → validation → packaging → deployment.

---

## [00:02:29] Where Small Teams Struggle: Handoffs, Naming, and Transcripts

Three common pain points for small teams:

- **Handoffs** — no single source of truth when different people own different stages
- **Inconsistent naming and asset storage** — the "final_v2_real_final.wav" problem
- **Transcript overconfidence** — treating a raw transcript as publish-ready copy

---

## [00:03:03] Transcripts: Rich Input, Not Final Truth

A raw transcript can contain filler, false starts, jokes that don't read well, corrected statements, and unverified references. Maya's key framing: *"The transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth."*

What transcripts are good for: first drafts of summaries, chapter suggestions, quote candidates, social post options, searchability, and repurposing.

What still needs human review: facts, links, sponsor language, names, affiliations, dates, and any authoritative-sounding claims.

---

## [00:04:15] Lightweight Pipeline Design for Small Teams

For a two-to-three person team, Maya recommends:

- One folder or workspace per episode
- A clear naming convention
- A checklist with defined handoff points
- A **fixed output bundle** for every episode: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, and a publish log

On transcripts specifically: keep **both** raw and cleaned versions. The raw transcript preserves traceability; the cleaned transcript becomes the preferred source for summarization and repurposing.

---

## [00:05:49] Automation: Where Teams Overestimate It

Two areas where automation is commonly over-trusted:

1. **Editorial judgment** — an agent can propose titles, but it doesn't know your audience positioning unless that's been encoded somewhere
2. **Exception handling** — edge cases (guest name corrections, legal concerns, sensitive anecdotes, sponsor constraints) are where fully automated systems tend to fail

The healthy mindset: *"Use automation to accelerate repetitive transforms… But don't give up responsibility for what goes live."*

---

## [00:06:52] Titles: A Contract with the Listener

Titles do a lot of work. Maya's approach: generate several candidates from different angles — problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, curiosity-driven — then compare against your actual audience promise.

Example framings for the same episode:
- *"How Small Teams Build a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline"*
- *"Why Podcast Production Breaks Even When the Recording Is Great"*
- *"From Raw Recording to Publish: The Systems Behind a Great Podcast"*

Key insight: frequency is not strategy. The most repeated phrase in a conversation is often not the most compelling framing.

---

## [00:08:13] Pre-Publish Review: The Five Highest-Value Checks

Before an episode goes live, Maya prioritizes:

1. **Names and affiliations** — get the guest's name, title, and company right
2. **Links and references** — verify every destination that was promised in the recording
3. **Sponsor compliance** — confirm required language is present where it should be
4. **Sensitive edits** — confirm anything the guest wanted removed is actually gone
5. **Metadata consistency** — title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy should all describe the same episode

---

## [00:09:13] Practical Takeaways: What to Do This Month

If you run a small show and want to improve your pipeline now:

1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. Create a publish checklist that one person owns
3. Separate raw transcript from cleaned transcript
4. Define the required output bundle for every episode
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to surface where delays and repeated errors occur

Host addition: *"If you're experimenting with automation, start by automating drafts, not approvals."*

Maya's close: *"Make the machine produce options. Make the human approve what becomes public."*
