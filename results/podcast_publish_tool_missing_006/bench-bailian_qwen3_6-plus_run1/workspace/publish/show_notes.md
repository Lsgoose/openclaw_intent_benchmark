# Episode 14: Building a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline

**Guest:** Maya Chen — advisor to media teams and creator businesses on production systems.

**Sponsor:** SoundLayer — a collaboration tool for small media teams that helps organize transcripts, asset versions, and review workflows. Learn more: <https://creators.example.com/tools/soundlayer-overview>

## Timestamps

**[00:00:00] — Introduction: The Pipeline Is the Product**
The host introduces the topic: how a raw recording becomes a polished episode. Maya Chen joins and frames the core idea — the production pipeline *is* the product, not just the conversation itself.

**[00:01:20] — The Five Stages of a Podcast Production Pipeline**
Maya breaks the pipeline into five stages: capture (recording + companion materials), post-production (audio cleanup and editing), content extraction (transcripts, summaries, chapter markers, titles, clips), review and approval (fact-checking, sponsor compliance, legal review), and distribution (hosting platform, newsletter, social channels, archives).

**[00:02:29] — Where Small Teams Struggle First**
Three common pain points: handoffs between team members without a single source of truth, inconsistent naming and asset storage ("final_v2_real_final.wav"), and the false assumption that raw transcripts are automatically publish-ready.

**[00:03:03] — Why Raw Transcripts Aren't Publish-Ready**
Transcripts contain filler, false starts, jokes that don't read well, statements later corrected, and unverified link references. Treating a transcript as clean structured truth leads to low-quality or misleading derivative content.

**[00:03:34] — What to Extract vs. What Needs Human Review**
Transcripts are excellent inputs for first drafts of summaries, chapter suggestions, quote candidates, and social posts. But anything authoritative — facts, links, sponsor language, names, affiliations, dates, claims — still requires human review. "The transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth."

**[00:04:15] — A Lightweight Pipeline for Small Teams**
One folder per episode, clear naming conventions, a checklist with defined handoff points, and a fixed output bundle: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, and a publish log.

**[00:04:54] — Raw Transcript vs. Cleaned Transcript**
Keep both. The raw transcript for traceability and internal reference. The cleaned transcript — with disfluencies removed, speaker labels fixed, and formatting applied — as the preferred source for summarization and repurposing.

**[00:05:49] — Where Teams Overestimate Automation**
Two areas: editorial judgment (an agent can propose titles but doesn't know your audience positioning) and exception handling (guest name corrections, legal concerns, sensitive anecdotes, sponsor constraints). "Use automation for acceleration, not abdication."

**[00:06:52] — Title Strategy: Framing Matters**
Generate multiple title candidates from different angles — problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, curiosity-driven — then compare against your audience promise. "A title is not just a label. It's a contract with the listener." Frequency is not strategy.

**[00:08:13] — The Five Highest-Value Pre-Publish Checks**
Names and affiliations, links and references, sponsor compliance, sensitive edits, and metadata consistency across all surfaces (title, description, transcript, chapter list, social copy).

**[00:09:13] — Practical Takeaways: Five Steps to Improve This Month**
Define a standard folder structure and naming convention, create a publish checklist with a single owner, separate raw and cleaned transcripts, define the required output bundle, and keep a lightweight episode log to track delays and repeated errors.

**[00:10:05] — Sponsor and Wrap-Up**
Thanks to sponsor SoundLayer. Maya's closing advice: "Make the machine produce options. Make the human approve what becomes public."
