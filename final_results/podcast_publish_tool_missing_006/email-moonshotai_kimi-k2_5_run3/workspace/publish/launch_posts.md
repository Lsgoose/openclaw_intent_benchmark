## Post 1: Key Takeaway

The most dangerous assumption in podcast production is that a raw transcript equals publish-ready content. It doesn't. Raw transcripts contain filler, false starts, corrected statements, and unverified links that can mislead your audience or break your downstream automation.

Media advisor Maya Chen's framework: maintain both versions. Keep the raw transcript for traceability and internal reference. Create a cleaned version—with disfluencies removed, speaker labels fixed, and formatting standardized—that becomes your source for summaries, quotes, and repurposing.

This separation isn't bureaucracy. It's noise reduction before you ask another tool or teammate to build on the result. The teams that ship consistently aren't the ones with the most elaborate systems. They're the ones that reduce ambiguity at every handoff.

Episode 14 breaks down the five-stage production pipeline, where small teams actually struggle, and how to improve your workflow this month without over-engineering it.

## Post 2: Discussion Question

Where does your podcast production pipeline actually break most often?

Not where you think it breaks—where it actually breaks. The handoff between recorder and editor? The moment someone has to write a title from a transcript? The final check before publish when nobody's sure if the sponsor language made it in?

Media advisor Maya Chen sees the same three failure patterns in small teams: chaotic handoffs with no single source of truth, inconsistent naming that makes files untraceable, and the assumption that transcripts don't need human review before becoming public-facing content.

The uncomfortable truth: listeners feel production friction immediately. Metadata that doesn't match, chapters that don't align, descriptions that promise something the episode doesn't deliver—it all signals unreliability.

Episode 14 maps the five stages from capture to distribution, the five checks that matter before going live, and a practical framework for tightening your pipeline this month. Worth a listen if you're the person who actually has to ship the episodes.
