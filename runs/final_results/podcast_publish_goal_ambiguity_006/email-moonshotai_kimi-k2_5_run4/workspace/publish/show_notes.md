# Podcast Production Pipeline — with Maya Chen

## Introduction to Production Pipelines
**[00:00:00 - 00:01:27]**

The episode opens with an overview of how a podcast moves from raw recording to polished episode — covering clean audio, transcripts, chapter markers, social clips, titles, descriptions, and discovery metadata. Maya Chen is introduced as an advisor to media teams and creator businesses on production systems.

> "The pipeline is the product."

---

## The Five Stages of a Production Pipeline
**[00:01:27 - 00:02:33]**

Maya breaks down podcast production into five stages:
1. **Capture** — recording the conversation plus companion materials (bios, sponsor copy, links, references)
2. **Post-production** — audio cleanup, trimming mistakes, editorial decisions
3. **Content extraction** — transcripts, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, clips
4. **Review and approval** — fact-checking, sponsor language, names, links, legal review
5. **Distribution** — delivery to hosting platforms, newsletters, social channels, and archives

The software build pipeline analogy is introduced: source material → transformations → validation → packaging → deployment.

---

## Common Pain Points for Small Teams
**[00:02:33 - 00:03:09]**

Three primary struggle areas for small teams:
- **Handoffs** — no single source of truth when multiple people touch an episode
- **Inconsistent naming and asset storage** — files like "final_v2_real_final.wav" create confusion
- **Transcript-driven tasks** — raw transcripts require more editorial judgment than expected

---

## Transcripts as Input, Not Truth
**[00:03:09 - 00:04:26]**

A deep dive on why raw transcripts are not publish-ready. Issues include filler, false starts, jokes that don't read well, statements later corrected, and unverified references. Maya explains that transcripts are excellent for first drafts (summaries, chapters, quotes, social posts) but anything authoritative — facts, links, sponsor language, names, affiliations, dates, claims — requires human review.

> "The transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth."

---

## Lightweight Pipeline Design
**[00:04:26 - 00:05:57]**

Practical recommendations for teams of 2-3 people:
- One folder/workspace per episode
- Clear naming conventions
- Checklist with defined handoff points
- Standardized **output bundle**: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, publish log

Maya explains the distinction between raw transcripts (for traceability) and cleaned transcripts (for summarization and repurposing). Without this separation, downstream automation inherits every source mess.

---

## Automation Boundaries
**[00:05:57 - 00:06:57]**

Where automation efforts typically overestimate capability:
- **Editorial judgment** — agents can propose titles but lack audience positioning context
- **Exception handling** — edge cases (name corrections, legal reviews, sensitive anecdotes, sponsor constraints) break fully automated systems

The healthy mindset: use automation for acceleration, not abdication. Automate repetitive transforms (transcription cleanup, first drafts, formatting, metadata prefill) but retain human ownership of what goes live.

---

## Titles as Audience Contracts
**[00:06:57 - 00:08:20]**

Titles do more work than most teams realize. Maya recommends generating candidates from multiple angles: problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, curiosity-driven. The title is a contract with the listener — frequency of phrases is not strategy.

From a workflow perspective: aim for three title candidates, one short description, one long description, and a final approved version logged before publish.

---

## Pre-Publish Review Checklist
**[00:08:20 - 00:09:23]**

Five high-value checks before going live:
1. Names and affiliations — verify guest details
2. Links and references — confirm every "we'll link that" promise
3. Sponsor compliance — required language present
4. Sensitive edits — confirm guest-requested removals
5. Metadata consistency — alignment across title, description, transcript, chapters, social copy

Inconsistency across surfaces makes production feel unreliable.

---

## Practical Takeaways for Small Shows
**[00:09:23 - 00:10:25]**

Five actions to improve your pipeline this month:
1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. Create a publish checklist with clear ownership
3. Separate raw transcript from cleaned transcript
4. Define the required output bundle for every episode
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to identify delays and repeated errors

> "Make the machine produce options. Make the human approve what becomes public."
