# Episode 14 – Building a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline

## [00:00:00] Why the production pipeline is part of the product
- How a show moves from raw recording to polished episode: clean audio, transcripts, chapters, social clips, titles, descriptions, and metadata.
- Maya frames the idea that “the pipeline is the product” because packaging and distribution shape the listener experience as much as the conversation.

## [00:01:20] The five core stages of a podcast production pipeline
- Maya breaks the pipeline into five stages: capture, post‑production, content extraction, review and approval, and distribution.
- Why even small teams benefit from thinking about assets and handoffs like a software build pipeline.
- How fragile systems emerge when the process relies on memory and heroics instead of clear structure.

**Links mentioned:**
- Podcast release checklist: https://creators.example.com/checklists/podcast-release

## [00:02:29] Common pain points: handoffs, naming, and transcripts
- Where small teams usually struggle first: messy handoffs, unclear ownership, and chaotic file naming (e.g., “final_v2_real_final.wav”).
- Why transcript-driven tasks look deceptively easy but demand judgment.
- The dangers of treating transcripts as publish-ready truth instead of a noisy input.

---

## [00:03:34] Using transcripts as rich input, not final truth
- What should be reliably extracted from a transcript: first‑draft summaries, chapter suggestions, quote candidates, and social post options.
- Why anything authoritative — facts, links, sponsor language, names, affiliations, dates, and claims — still needs human review.
- Producer’s note: “rich input, not final truth” as a guiding principle for transcript‑driven workflows.

---

## [00:04:15] A lightweight pipeline for a 2–3 person team
- Designing a simple, reliable pipeline with one folder or workspace per episode.
- Establishing a clear naming convention, defined handoff points, and a fixed output bundle.
- What goes into the standard output bundle: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, and a publish log.

**Links mentioned:**
- Example podcast release checklist: https://creators.example.com/checklists/podcast-release

---

## [00:04:54] Raw vs clean transcript and why it matters
- Why Maya recommends keeping both a raw transcript (for traceability) and a cleaned transcript (for readability and repurposing).
- How separating raw from cleaned material prevents downstream tools from inheriting every disfluency and error.
- The impact of transcript quality on summaries, quote cards, and clips.

---

## [00:05:49] Where automation helps—and where it breaks
- How teams overestimate automation around editorial judgment and exception handling.
- Using agents and tools to accelerate repetitive transforms (cleanup, first‑draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, metadata prefill).
- Keeping humans in the loop for correctness, tone, edge cases, and final release quality.

---

## [00:06:52] Crafting titles that act like a contract
- Why titles do so much work in discovery and listener expectations.
- Maya’s approach: generate title candidates from multiple angles (problem‑oriented, guest‑oriented, outcome‑oriented, curiosity‑driven).
- Example titles and why frequency of phrases in a transcript is not a strategy.

---

## [00:08:13] High‑value review checks before you publish
- Five checks before an episode goes live: names and affiliations, links and references, sponsor compliance, sensitive edits, and metadata consistency.
- How misaligned titles, descriptions, and transcripts erode listener trust.
- Treating metadata alignment as part of perceived production quality.

**Links mentioned:**
- Podcast release checklist (for review steps): https://creators.example.com/checklists/podcast-release

## [00:09:13] Practical steps to improve your pipeline this month
- Five concrete actions for small shows: define folder structure, create a publish checklist, separate raw vs clean transcripts, standardize the output bundle, and keep a lightweight episode log.
- Why consistency matters more than elaborate systems for most creator teams.
- Bonus mindset: automate drafts, not approvals—machines generate options, humans approve what becomes public.
