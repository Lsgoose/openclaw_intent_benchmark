# Episode 14: Building a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline

**Guest:** Maya Chen — media production systems advisor

---

## Timestamps

### [00:00:00] Introduction
Welcome and framing: the production pipeline shapes the listener experience just as much as the conversation itself.

### [00:00:36] The Pipeline Is the Product
Maya's core premise: production discipline is invisible until something breaks — and then everyone realizes the pipeline *is* the product. Vague titles, sloppy transcripts, misaligned chapter markers, and inconsistent checklists all compound into a degraded listener experience.

### [00:01:20] The Five Stages of a Podcast Production Pipeline
1. **Capture** — recording plus companion materials (guest bios, sponsor copy, links, references)
2. **Post-production** — audio cleanup, trimming, editorial decisions
3. **Content extraction** — transcript, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, clips
4. **Review and approval** — fact-checking, sponsor language, names, links, editorial/legal review
5. **Distribution** — hosting platform, newsletter, social channels, archives

### [00:02:29] Where Small Teams Struggle First
Three common pain points:
- **Handoffs** — no single source of truth when multiple people own different stages
- **Inconsistent naming and asset storage** — "final_v2_real_final.wav" syndrome
- **Transcript overconfidence** — assuming a raw transcript is publish-ready copy

### [00:03:03] Transcripts: Rich Input, Not Final Truth
Raw transcripts contain filler, false starts, uncorrected statements, and unverified references. Treating them as clean structured truth leads to low-quality or misleading derivative content. Transcripts are excellent first-draft inputs for summaries, chapter suggestions, and quote candidates — but facts, links, names, affiliations, and claims still require human review.

### [00:04:15] What a Lightweight Pipeline Looks Like for Small Teams
- One folder per episode with a clear naming convention
- A checklist with defined handoff points
- A **standard output bundle** for every episode: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, publish log

### [00:04:54] Raw vs. Clean Transcripts
Keep both. The raw transcript preserves traceability; the cleaned transcript (disfluencies removed, speaker labels fixed, readable formatting) becomes the preferred source for summarization and repurposing. Separating them prevents downstream tools and teammates from inheriting source noise.

### [00:05:49] Automation: Acceleration, Not Abdication
Where teams overestimate automation:
- **Editorial judgment** — agents can propose titles but don't know your audience positioning unless it's encoded
- **Exception handling** — guest name corrections, legal concerns, sensitive anecdotes, sponsor constraints

Healthy mindset: automate repetitive transforms (transcription cleanup, first-draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, metadata prefill). Don't automate approvals. Make the machine produce options; make the human approve what goes public.

### [00:06:52] Titles: A Contract with the Listener
Generate multiple candidates from different angles — problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, curiosity-driven — then evaluate against your actual audience promise. Frequency of a phrase in the transcript is not a title strategy.

Example framings for this episode:
- "How Small Teams Build a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline"
- "Why Podcast Production Breaks Even When the Recording Is Great"
- "From Raw Recording to Publish: The Systems Behind a Great Podcast"

### [00:08:13] The Five Highest-Value Pre-Publish Checks
1. **Names and affiliations** — guest name, title, company
2. **Links and references** — verify every destination before publishing
3. **Sponsor compliance** — required language present in the right places
4. **Sensitive edits** — confirm removals the guest requested are actually gone
5. **Metadata consistency** — title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy all describe the same episode

### [00:09:13] Practical Takeaways: Improve Your Pipeline This Month
1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. Create a publish checklist with a single owner
3. Separate raw and cleaned transcripts
4. Define the required output bundle for every episode
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to surface delays and repeated errors
6. *(Host addition)* When experimenting with automation, start by automating drafts — not approvals
