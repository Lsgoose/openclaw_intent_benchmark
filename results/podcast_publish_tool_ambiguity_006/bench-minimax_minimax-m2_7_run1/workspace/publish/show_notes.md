## [00:01:20] The Five Stages of a Podcast Production Pipeline

Maya breaks production into five stages:

1. **Capture** — recording and collecting companion materials (guest bios, sponsor copy, links, references)
2. **Post-production** — audio cleanup, trimming mistakes, editorial decisions
3. **Content extraction** — transcript generation, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, clips
4. **Review and approval** — fact-checking, sponsor language review, name/link verification, legal checks
5. **Distribution** — publishing to hosting platform, newsletter, social channels, and archives

Maya draws an analogy to a software build pipeline: source material → transformations → validation → packaging → deployment.

---

## [00:02:29] Where Small Teams Struggle First

Three common pain points:

- **Handoffs** — one person records, another edits, someone else writes the description, and there is no single source of truth
- **Inconsistent naming and asset storage** — files like `final_v2_real_final.wav` create confusion about which version is canonical
- **Transcript-driven tasks** — raw transcripts are not automatically publish-ready copy; they require editorial judgment

---

## [00:03:03] Transcript Quality and Editorial Judgment

Maya emphasizes that a transcript contains filler, false starts, jokes that do not read well, and statements corrected later. Treating a raw transcript as clean truth leads to low-quality or misleading derivative content.

**Key distinction:** A transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth. Facts, links, sponsor language, names, affiliations, and authoritative claims always need human review.

> "A transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth." — Maya Chen

---

## [00:04:15] A Lightweight but Reliable Pipeline for Small Teams

Maya's recommendation for teams of two to three:

- One folder or workspace per episode
- A clear naming convention
- A defined publish checklist with clear handoff points
- A fixed output bundle for every episode

**Standard output bundle per episode:**
- Final audio
- Clean transcript
- Show notes
- Title and description
- Chapter list
- Thumbnail (if used)
- Publish log

---

## [00:04:54] Raw Transcript versus Cleaned Transcript

Maya strongly recommends keeping both:

- **Raw transcript** — preserved for traceability and internal reference
- **Cleaned transcript** — removes disfluencies, fixes speaker labels, formats for readability; becomes the preferred source for summarization and repurposing

This separation matters especially when using automation or AI tools downstream.

---

## [00:05:49] Where Automation Gets Overestimated

Two areas where teams overestimate automation:

1. **Editorial judgment** — an agent can propose titles but cannot know audience positioning unless it has been explicitly encoded
2. **Exception handling** — most episodes are routine, but every few episodes bring a name correction, legal concern, sensitive anecdote, or sponsor constraint; fully automated systems fail on edge cases

**Healthy mindset:** Use automation to accelerate repetitive transforms (transcription cleanup, first-draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, asset packaging, metadata prefill). Do not abdicate responsibility for what goes live.

> "Make the machine produce options. Make the human approve what becomes public." — Maya Chen

---

## [00:06:52] Titles and Framing

Titles do significant work. Maya recommends generating candidates from different angles:

- Problem-oriented
- Guest-oriented
- Outcome-oriented
- Curiosity-driven

**A title is not just a label — it is a contract with the listener.** Frequency-based title generation (most repeated phrase) is not strategy; good titles come from understanding what the episode helps the listener actually do.

---

## [00:08:13] The Pre-Publish Review Checklist

Five highest-value checks before release:

1. **Names and affiliations** — guest name, title, and company are correct
2. **Links and references** — every mentioned link is verified and points to the right destination
3. **Sponsor compliance** — required sponsor language is present and placed correctly
4. **Sensitive edits** — anything the guest requested removed is actually absent
5. **Metadata consistency** — title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy all describe the same episode

---

## [00:09:13] Practical Takeaways for Small Show Operators

Maya's recommended first steps for improving a pipeline this month:

1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. Create a publish checklist owned by one person
3. Separate raw transcript and cleaned transcript
4. Define the required output bundle for every episode
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to spot recurring delays and errors

> "You do not need an elaborate system to get meaningful gains. You need consistency." — Maya Chen

Host addendum: If experimenting with automation, start by automating drafts, not approvals.
