# Episode 14 Show Notes

## [00:00:00] Why the production pipeline shapes listener experience
The episode opens by reframing podcast production as more than back-office operations. The pipeline determines how a recording becomes a polished release through audio cleanup, transcripts, chapter markers, descriptions, clips, and metadata. The core idea: the pipeline is not separate from the product experience.

## [00:01:20] The five stages of a reliable podcast production pipeline
Maya Chen breaks the workflow into five stages:
- Capture
- Post-production
- Content extraction
- Review and approval
- Distribution

The comparison to a software build pipeline is useful here: source material moves through transformations, validation, packaging, and deployment.

## [00:02:29] Where small teams usually break first
The first weak points are handoffs, inconsistent file naming, and messy asset storage. When no single source of truth exists, teams lose confidence in which version is correct. The conversation also warns against treating transcript-driven work as automatically publish-ready.

## [00:03:03] Why transcripts are inputs, not editorial truth
A raw transcript may include filler, false starts, superseded statements, and unverified promises such as “we’ll put the link in the show notes.” Maya’s rule is simple: transcripts are strong inputs for drafts, but they still require human review for facts, names, links, dates, sponsor language, and claims.

## [00:04:15] What a lightweight, reliable pipeline looks like for small teams
For a two- or three-person team, the recommended setup is simple:
- One workspace per episode
- A clear naming convention
- A checklist with explicit handoffs
- A fixed output bundle

That output bundle should consistently include final audio, a clean transcript, show notes, a title, a description, chapter markers, artwork if used, and a publish log. A practical release checklist example: [Podcast Release Checklist](https://creators.example.com/checklists/podcast-release)

## [00:04:54] Raw transcript vs. clean transcript
The episode makes a strong case for keeping both versions:
- Raw transcript for traceability and wording verification
- Clean transcript for summarization, repurposing, and readable internal use

Separating the two reduces downstream noise and improves the quality of summaries, quote pulls, and clips.

## [00:05:49] What automation helps with — and what it should not own
Automation works well for repetitive transforms such as transcription cleanup, first-draft notes, chapter suggestions, metadata prefills, and packaging. It works poorly as a substitute for editorial judgment or for handling exceptions. The recommended mindset is to automate drafts, not approvals.

## [00:06:52] Titles are positioning, not just labels
The title discussion focuses on audience promise. Rather than selecting the most repeated phrase in a conversation, teams should generate multiple title angles — problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, or curiosity-driven — and choose the one that best matches listener intent. As Maya puts it, a title is a contract with the listener.

## [00:08:13] The highest-value pre-publish review checks
Maya highlights five release checks:
- Names and affiliations
- Links and references
- Sponsor compliance
- Sensitive edits
- Metadata consistency

The metadata point matters across every surface: title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy should all describe the same episode.

## [00:09:13] The practical upgrade plan for this month
The closing takeaway is a five-step improvement plan for small shows:
1. Standardize the episode folder structure and naming convention
2. Create a publish checklist with one clear owner
3. Separate raw and cleaned transcripts
4. Define the required output bundle for every episode
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to spot delays and repeated errors
