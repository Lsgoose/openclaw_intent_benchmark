# Episode 14: Building a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline
## Guest
[Maya Chen](https://creators.example.com/guests/maya-chen) – Media production systems advisor for creator businesses and media teams.

---

## Timestamps & Topics
[00:00:00] Introduction
- What is a podcast production pipeline and why it shapes the entire listener experience, not just behind-the-scenes operations.
- Common pain points when pipeline systems fail, from bad titles to inconsistent publishing checklists.

[00:01:20] Core Pipeline Stages
1. Capture: Record conversations and collect all companion materials (bios, links, sponsor copy, references)
2. Post-production: Clean audio, trim mistakes, and finalize episode content
3. Content extraction: Generate transcripts, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, and clips
4. Review and approval: Fact-check links, names, sponsor language, and legal/editorial concerns
5. Distribution: Publish the final asset bundle to hosting platforms, newsletters, and social channels

[00:02:29] Common Small Team Pain Points
- Handoff gaps when multiple people work on different parts of the episode without a single source of truth
- Inconsistent file naming and storage leading to version confusion
- Over-reliance on raw transcripts as publish-ready content instead of structured inputs

[00:03:34] Transcript Best Practices
- Use transcripts as first-draft inputs for summaries, chapters, and social content, not final editorial truth
- Always review facts, links, names, and claims before publishing derivative content
- Maintain both raw transcripts (for traceability) and cleaned transcripts (for repurposing)
- "Rich input, not final truth" – transcripts are a starting point, not a finished product

[00:04:15] Lightweight Pipeline for Small Teams
- One dedicated folder/workspace per episode with a consistent naming convention
- Clear checklists with defined handoff points
- Standard output bundle: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, publish log
- Standardized outputs simplify all downstream publishing and repurposing workflows

[00:05:49] Automation Strategy
- Don't overestimate automation: editorial judgment and exception handling still require human input
- Use automation to accelerate repetitive tasks: transcript cleanup, first-draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, asset packaging, metadata prefill
- Use automation for acceleration, not abdication – humans still own content correctness, tone, and release quality

[00:06:52] Title Best Practices
- Generate multiple title candidates from different angles: problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, curiosity-driven
- Titles are a contract with listeners, not just labels – they should match audience intent and the episode's core value
- Avoid relying only on phrase frequency for title selection; focus on what the episode helps listeners do

[00:08:13] Pre-Publish Checks
1. Verify names and affiliations are correct
2. Confirm all referenced links work and point to the correct destination
3. Validate sponsor compliance and required language placement
4. Ensure all requested sensitive edits have been made
5. Check metadata consistency across title, description, transcript, chapters, and social copy

[00:09:13] Actionable First Steps for Improvement
1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. Create a publish checklist owned by a single team member
3. Separate raw and cleaned transcript workflows
4. Define required output assets for every episode
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to track delays and repeated errors
- Bonus: Start automation with draft generation, not final approvals
