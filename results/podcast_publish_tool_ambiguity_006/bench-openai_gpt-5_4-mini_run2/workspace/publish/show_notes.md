## 00:00:00 — Why the pipeline matters
The episode opens by framing the production pipeline as more than operations: it shapes the listener experience from raw recording through clean audio, transcript, chapter markers, clips, title, description, and metadata.

## 00:01:20 — The five-stage pipeline
Maya breaks the workflow into five stages: capture, post-production, content extraction, review and approval, and distribution. The analogy to a software build pipeline comes up here, with source material, transformations, validation, packaging, and deployment.

## 00:02:29 — Where small teams struggle
The biggest pain points are handoffs, inconsistent asset naming/storage, and overestimating how “easy” transcript-driven content is. A transcript is useful, but not automatically publish-ready.

## 00:03:40 — Transcript as input, not truth
Transcripts are strong inputs for drafts of summaries, chapter ideas, quote candidates, and social copy, but facts, links, sponsor language, names, affiliations, dates, and claims still need human review.

## 00:04:26 — A lightweight reliable system
For small teams, the advice is to use one folder per episode, clear naming, a checklist with defined handoff points, and a fixed output bundle: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, and publish log.

## 00:04:59 — Raw transcript vs cleaned transcript
Keep both. The raw transcript stays as a traceable reference, while the cleaned transcript becomes the preferred source for summarization and repurposing.

## 00:05:49 — Automation, limits, and judgment
Automation is best used for repetitive transforms like transcription cleanup, formatting, asset packaging, and metadata prefill. Editorial judgment and exception handling still need a human owner.

## 00:06:57 — Titles are a contract
Several title angles are recommended: problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, and curiosity-driven. The best title should match the actual promise to the listener, not just the most repeated phrase in the transcript.

## 00:08:13 — Pre-publish review checks
The highest-value checks are names and affiliations, links and references, sponsor compliance, sensitive edits, and metadata consistency across title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy.

## 00:09:23 — Practical takeaways
The closing advice is to standardize the folder structure, assign one owner for the checklist, separate raw and cleaned transcripts, define the required output bundle, and keep a lightweight episode log. Automation should draft; humans should approve what becomes public.
