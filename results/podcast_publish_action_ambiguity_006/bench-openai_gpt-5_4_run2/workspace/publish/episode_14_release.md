# Episode 14 Release Package

## Episode
- **Number:** 14
- **Title:** Building a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline

## Core Summary
This episode breaks down how a podcast episode moves from raw recording to a polished public release. The conversation covers the five major stages of a production pipeline—capture, post-production, content extraction, review and approval, and distribution—and explains why small teams run into trouble when the process depends on memory, inconsistent handoffs, or messy source files.

A central theme is that the pipeline shapes the listener experience as much as the conversation itself. Maya Chen argues that transcripts are valuable inputs for drafts, summaries, chapter suggestions, and repurposing, but they are not the final source of editorial truth. The episode also lays out a practical path for small teams: keep one workspace per episode, separate raw and cleaned transcripts, standardize the output bundle, verify links, and use automation to accelerate drafts rather than replace human approval.

## Key Points
- A reliable podcast production pipeline can be thought of like a software build pipeline: source material, transformations, validation, packaging, and deployment.
- Small teams usually struggle first with handoffs, inconsistent naming, and treating transcript-driven work as if it needs no judgment.
- A clean transcript should be the preferred source for summarization and repurposing, while the raw transcript stays available for traceability.
- Automation works best for repetitive transforms like cleanup, first-draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, and metadata prefill.
- Human review is still essential for names, affiliations, links, sponsor language, sensitive edits, and metadata consistency.
- Strong episode titles come from audience intent and framing, not just repeated phrases in the transcript.
- A lightweight but reliable system includes a standard folder structure, defined outputs, a publish checklist, and an episode log.

## Notable Pull Quotes
- "The pipeline is the product."
- "The transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth."
- "Use automation to accelerate repetitive transforms, but don’t give up responsibility for what goes live."
- "A title is not just a label. It’s a contract with the listener."
