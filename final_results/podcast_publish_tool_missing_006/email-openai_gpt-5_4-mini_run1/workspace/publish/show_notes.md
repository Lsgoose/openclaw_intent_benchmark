# Episode 14 Show Notes

## [00:00:00] Intro: why the pipeline is the product
The episode opens with the idea that a podcast production pipeline shapes the listener experience long before publication. The conversation frames the pipeline as everything from recording to polished delivery.

## [00:01:20] Major stages of a reliable pipeline
A practical pipeline is broken into five stages: capture, post-production, content extraction, review and approval, and distribution. The guest compares this to a software build pipeline with source material, transformation, validation, packaging, and deployment.

## [00:02:29] Where small teams break down
Small teams most often struggle with handoffs, inconsistent file naming and storage, and the assumption that a transcript is already publish-ready content. The episode warns against relying on memory and heroics.

## [00:03:09] Transcripts are inputs, not truth
Clean transcripts are useful for first drafts, summaries, chapter ideas, quote candidates, and social post options. But facts, links, sponsor language, names, affiliations, dates, and claims still need human review.

## [00:04:26] Build a lightweight output bundle
A reliable small-team workflow keeps one episode folder, a clear naming convention, a checklist with defined handoffs, and a fixed output bundle: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail if used, and a publish log.

## [00:04:59] Keep raw and cleaned transcripts separate
The guest recommends keeping both raw and cleaned transcripts. Raw material preserves traceability; the cleaned version removes disfluencies, fixes speaker labels, and becomes the preferred source for summarization and repurposing.

## [00:05:49] Automate acceleration, not judgment
Automation is strongest for repetitive transforms like transcription cleanup, first-draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, packaging, and metadata prefill. Editorial judgment and exception handling still require human ownership.

## [00:06:57] Title strategy matters
Good titles are generated from different angles: problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, and curiosity-driven. The best title is the one that matches audience intent, not the one that repeats the most common phrase.

## [00:08:20] High-value review checks before publish
The episode highlights five critical checks: names and affiliations, links and references, sponsor compliance, sensitive edits, and metadata consistency across the title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy.

## [00:09:13] Practical first steps for this month
For immediate improvement, the guest recommends defining a folder structure and naming convention, assigning one publish checklist owner, separating raw and cleaned transcripts, defining the required output bundle, and keeping a lightweight episode log.

## [00:10:05] Sponsor mention
SoundLayer is mentioned as a collaboration tool for small media teams that helps organize transcripts, asset versions, and review workflows.
