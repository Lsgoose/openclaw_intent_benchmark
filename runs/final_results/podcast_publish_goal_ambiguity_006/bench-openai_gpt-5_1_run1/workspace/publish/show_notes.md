# Episode Show Notes: Podcast Production Pipelines with Maya Chen

[00:00:00] **Why the Production Pipeline *Is* the Product**  
- How raw recordings become polished episodes: audio, transcripts, chapters, titles, descriptions, and metadata.  
- Maya Chen introduces the idea that audiences experience the entire pipeline, not just the conversation.  
- Common quality pitfalls: vague titles, sloppy transcripts, misaligned chapter markers, and inconsistent publishing checklists.

[00:01:20] **Five Stages of a Podcast Production Pipeline**  
- Capture: recording plus gathering guest bios, sponsor copy, and reference links.  
- Post-production: cleaning audio, trimming mistakes, and shaping the final conversation.  
- Content extraction: transcripts, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, and clips.  
- Review and approval: fact checks, name checks, link verification, sponsor language, and legal/editorial review.  
- Distribution: sending the final asset bundle to the host, newsletter, social channels, and archives.

[00:02:29] **Where Small Teams Feel the Pain First**  
- Handoffs without a clear owner or single source of truth.  
- Messy file naming and asset storage (the dreaded `final_v2_real_final.wav`).  
- Over-trusting raw transcripts as publish-ready content instead of inputs that still require editorial judgment.

[00:03:34] **Using Transcripts as Rich Inputs, Not Final Truth**  
- Why transcripts are great for first-draft summaries, quote candidates, and social copy.  
- The risks of treating transcripts as authoritative: filler, false starts, unverified links, and corrected statements.  
- The mantra: the transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth.

[00:04:26] **Designing a Lightweight Pipeline for a 2–3 Person Team**  
- One folder or workspace per episode, with a clear naming convention.  
- A simple checklist with defined handoff points between recording, editing, and publishing.  
- A fixed output bundle for every episode: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, and a publish log.

[00:04:54] **Raw vs. Cleaned Transcripts in Your Workflow**  
- Why to keep both raw and cleaned transcripts for each episode.  
- How a cleaned transcript (with disfluencies removed and labels fixed) becomes the preferred source for downstream content.  
- How separating raw from cleaned material reduces noise in summaries, quote cards, and clips.

[00:05:49] **Where Automation Helps—and Where It Fails**  
- Treating the pipeline like a media “build system” with transforms, validation, and deployment.  
- How teams overestimate automation on editorial judgment and edge-case handling.  
- A healthier mindset: use agents and tools to accelerate repetitive transforms while humans own correctness, tone, and release quality.

[00:06:52] **Crafting Titles That Act Like a Contract**  
- Why titles do so much work for discovery and expectations.  
- Generating multiple title candidates: problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, and curiosity-driven.  
- Avoiding purely mechanical transcript-to-title systems that chase repeated phrases instead of audience intent.

[00:08:13] **High-Value Checks Before You Hit Publish**  
- Five core review checks: names and affiliations, links and references, sponsor compliance, sensitive edits, and metadata consistency.  
- How misaligned titles, descriptions, and show notes erode listener trust.  
- Building a pipeline that keeps every surface—audio, text, and metadata—in sync.

[00:09:13] **A Five-Step Playbook to Improve Your Pipeline This Month**  
- Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention.  
- Create a publish checklist with a clearly owned role.  
- Separate raw and cleaned transcripts in your workflow.  
- Standardize a required output bundle for every episode.  
- Keep a lightweight episode log to spot delays, confusion, and recurring errors.

[00:09:50] **Closing Thoughts and Resources**
