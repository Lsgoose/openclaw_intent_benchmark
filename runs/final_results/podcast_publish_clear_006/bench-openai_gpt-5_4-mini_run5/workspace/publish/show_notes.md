# Episode 14 Show Notes

## [00:00:00] Opening: why the pipeline matters
The episode opens by framing the podcast production pipeline as more than an operations detail. It shapes the listener experience by turning a raw recording into a polished episode with clean audio, transcript, chapter markers, social clips, a title, a description, and the metadata that helps people discover the show.

## [00:01:20] Five stages of a reliable production flow
Maya breaks the pipeline into five stages: capture, post-production, content extraction, review and approval, and distribution. Capture includes recording the conversation and gathering companion materials. Post-production covers cleaning audio and trimming mistakes. Content extraction turns the episode into transcripts, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, and clips. Review checks facts, sponsor language, names, links, and legal or editorial risk. Distribution sends the final package to hosting, newsletter, social channels, and archives.

## [00:02:33] Common failure points for small teams
Small teams often struggle with handoffs, inconsistent asset naming, and unclear source-of-truth files. Maya notes that transcripts are especially tricky: a raw transcript is not automatically publish-ready copy.

## [00:03:09] Why transcripts need editing
A transcript can include filler, false starts, corrections, and unresolved references. Maya emphasizes that transcripts are excellent inputs for first drafts, but facts, links, sponsor language, names, affiliations, dates, and claims still need human review.

## [00:04:26] A lightweight but reliable output bundle
For a small team, Maya recommends one folder per episode, a clear naming convention, a defined checklist, and a fixed output bundle: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail if used, and a publish log. She also recommends keeping both raw and cleaned transcripts so the cleaned version can drive summarization and repurposing while the raw version remains available for traceability.

## [00:05:49] Where automation helps, and where it stops
Automation is useful for repetitive transforms like transcription cleanup, first-draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, asset packaging, and metadata prefill. It should not replace editorial judgment or exception handling, especially when guest name corrections, legal review, sensitive anecdotes, or sponsor constraints appear.

## [00:06:57] Titles as contracts with listeners
Maya advises generating title candidates from different angles: problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, and curiosity-driven. She cautions that frequency is not strategy; the most repeated phrase in a conversation is not necessarily the best framing for the episode.

## [00:08:13] Highest-value review checks before publish
The most important pre-publish checks are names and affiliations, verified links and references, sponsor compliance, sensitive edits, and metadata consistency. The title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy should all describe the same episode.

## [00:09:13] Practical takeaways for this month
Maya’s recommended starting points are: define a standard folder structure and naming convention, assign one person to own the publish checklist, separate raw and cleaned transcripts, define the required output bundle, and keep a lightweight episode log. The main theme is consistency, not complexity.

## [00:09:50] Final guidance on automation
The episode closes with a strong reminder: use machines to produce options, but make humans approve what becomes public.
