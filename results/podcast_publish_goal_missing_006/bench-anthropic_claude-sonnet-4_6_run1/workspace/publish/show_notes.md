# Episode 14 – The Podcast Production Pipeline
**Guest:** Maya Chen, media systems advisor

---

## [00:00:00] Introduction – The Pipeline Is the Product

Host introduces the episode's focus: how a raw recording becomes a polished, distributed episode. Guest Maya Chen joins to discuss production systems for media teams.

> "The pipeline is the product." — Maya Chen

---

## [00:01:20] The Five Stages of a Podcast Production Pipeline

Maya breaks down the full pipeline:

1. **Capture** – Recording plus companion materials (guest bios, sponsor copy, links, references)
2. **Post-production** – Audio cleanup, trimming, editorial decisions
3. **Content extraction** – Transcript, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, clips
4. **Review and approval** – Fact-checking, sponsor language, names, links, editorial/legal review
5. **Distribution** – Hosting platform, newsletter, social channels, archives

---

## [00:02:29] Where Small Teams Struggle First

Three common pain points:

- **Handoffs** – No single source of truth across roles
- **Inconsistent naming and asset storage** – Version confusion ("final_v2_real_final.wav")
- **Transcript-driven tasks** – Raw transcripts are not publish-ready copy

---

## [00:03:03] Transcripts: Rich Input, Not Final Truth

Raw transcripts contain filler, false starts, unverified links, and statements later corrected. Treating them as clean structured truth leads to low-quality or misleading derivative content.

> "The transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth." — Maya Chen

**Good uses for transcripts:** First drafts of summaries, chapter suggestions, quote candidates, social post options, searchability, repurposing.

**Still requires human review:** Facts, links, sponsor language, names, affiliations, dates, claims.

---

## [00:04:15] Lightweight Pipeline for Small Teams

Recommended structure for a two-to-three person team:

- One folder per episode with a clear naming convention
- A checklist with defined handoff points
- A **standard output bundle** for every episode: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, publish log

---

## [00:04:54] Raw vs. Clean Transcripts

Keep both:

- **Raw transcript** – Traceability and internal reference; useful for verifying original wording
- **Clean transcript** – Removes disfluencies, fixes speaker labels, formatted for readability; preferred source for summarization and repurposing

---

## [00:05:49] Automation: Acceleration, Not Abdication

Where teams overestimate automation:

1. **Editorial judgment** – Agents can propose titles but don't know audience positioning unless it's encoded
2. **Exception handling** – Edge cases (name corrections, legal concerns, sensitive anecdotes, sponsor constraints) require human judgment

**Healthy mindset:** Automate repetitive transforms (transcription cleanup, first-draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, metadata prefill). Don't give up responsibility for what goes live.

## [00:08:13] Pre-Publish Review Checklist

Five highest-value checks before an episode goes live:

1. **Names and affiliations** – Guest name, title, and company
2. **Links and references** – Verify all promised links
3. **Sponsor compliance** – Required language present in correct placement
4. **Sensitive edits** – Confirm requested removals are complete
5. **Metadata consistency** – Title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy all describe the same episode

---

## [00:09:13] Practical Takeaways

Steps to improve your pipeline this month:

1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. Create a publish checklist with a single owner
3. Separate raw and clean transcripts
4. Define the required output bundle for every episode
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to track delays and repeated errors

**Bonus:** If experimenting with automation, start by automating drafts — not approvals.
