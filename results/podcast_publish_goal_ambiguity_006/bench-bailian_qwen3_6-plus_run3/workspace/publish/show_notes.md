# Episode 14 — The Podcast Production Pipeline with Maya Chen

## [00:00:00] Introduction: The Pipeline Is the Product
The host introduces the topic of podcast production pipelines — the systems that take a raw recording through editing, packaging, labeling, and distribution. Guest Maya Chen joins to discuss how production discipline shapes the listener experience.

## [00:01:20] The Five Stages of a Production Pipeline
Maya breaks the pipeline into five stages:
1. **Capture** — recording and collecting companion materials (bios, sponsor copy, links, references)
2. **Post-production** — cleaning audio, trimming mistakes, deciding what stays
3. **Content extraction** — transcripts, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, clips
4. **Review and approval** — fact-checking, sponsor language, names, links, editorial and legal review
5. **Distribution** — publishing to hosting platforms, newsletters, social channels, and archives

## [00:02:29] Where Small Teams Struggle First
Three common pain points: handoffs between team members without a single source of truth, inconsistent naming and asset storage, and the false assumption that transcript-driven tasks are easy. Raw transcripts are not automatically publish-ready copy.

## [00:03:03] Transcript as Content: Why Raw Transcripts Aren't Publish-Ready
Transcripts contain filler, false starts, jokes that don't read well, statements later corrected, and unverified link references. Treating a transcript as clean structured truth leads to low-quality or misleading derivative content.

## [00:03:34] What to Extract vs. What Needs Human Review
Transcripts are excellent inputs for first drafts of summaries, chapter suggestions, quote candidates, and social post options. But anything authoritative — facts, links, sponsor language, names, affiliations, dates, claims — still needs human review. The transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth.

## [00:04:15] Lightweight Pipeline for Small Teams
For teams of two or three: one folder per episode, clear naming conventions, a checklist with defined handoff points, and a fixed output bundle (final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, publish log).

## [00:04:54] Clean Transcript vs. Raw Transcript
Keep both. The raw transcript provides traceability. The cleaned transcript — with disfluencies removed, speaker labels fixed, and readable formatting — becomes the preferred source for summarization and repurposing.

## [00:05:49] Automation and Agents: Where Teams Overestimate
Teams overestimate automation in two areas: editorial judgment (an agent doesn't know your audience positioning) and exception handling (edge cases like guest name corrections, legal concerns, sensitive anecdotes). Use automation for acceleration, not abdication.

## [00:06:52] Title Strategy and Framing
Generate multiple title candidates from different angles — problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, curiosity-driven — and compare against your audience promise. A title is a contract with the listener, not just a label. Frequency is not strategy.

## [00:08:13] Pre-Publish Review Checklist
Five highest-value checks: names and affiliations, links and references, sponsor compliance, sensitive edits, and metadata consistency across title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy.

## [00:09:13] Practical Takeaways
Five steps to improve your pipeline this month: define a standard folder structure and naming convention, create a publish checklist with one owner, separate raw and cleaned transcripts, define the required output bundle, and keep a lightweight episode log. Automate drafts, not approvals.
