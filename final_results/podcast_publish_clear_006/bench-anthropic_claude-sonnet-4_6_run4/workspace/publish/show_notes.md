## [00:01:20] The Five Stages of a Podcast Production Pipeline

Maya breaks down the full pipeline into five stages:

1. **Capture** — recording the conversation plus companion materials (guest bios, sponsor copy, links, references)
2. **Post-production** — audio cleanup, trimming, and editorial decisions
3. **Content extraction** — transcript generation, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, and clips
4. **Review and approval** — fact-checking, sponsor language, names, links, and editorial/legal review
5. **Distribution** — publishing to hosting platform, newsletter, social channels, and archives

The pipeline is compared to a software build system: source material, transformations, validation, packaging, and deployment.

---

## [00:02:29] Where Small Teams Struggle First

Three common pain points for small teams:

- **Handoffs** — no single source of truth when recording, editing, and writing are split across people
- **Inconsistent naming and asset storage** — version confusion ("final_v2_real_final.wav")
- **Transcript-driven tasks** — raw transcripts are not publish-ready; they require editorial judgment

---

## [00:03:03] Transcripts as Rich Input, Not Final Truth

A raw transcript can contain filler, false starts, uncorrected statements, and unverified references. Treating it as clean structured truth leads to low-quality or misleading derivative content.

> "The transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth." — Maya Chen

Transcripts are good first-draft inputs for: episode summaries, chapter suggestions, quote candidates, and social post options. Anything authoritative — facts, links, names, affiliations, dates, claims — still requires human review.

---

## [00:04:15] Lightweight Pipeline for Small Teams

Practical structure for a two- or three-person team:

- One folder or workspace per episode
- Clear naming convention
- Checklist with defined handoff points
- **Standard output bundle** for every episode: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, and a publish log

**On transcripts:** Keep both raw and cleaned versions. The raw transcript preserves traceability; the cleaned transcript (disfluencies removed, speaker labels fixed) becomes the preferred source for summarization and repurposing.

---

## [00:05:49] Automation: Where Teams Overestimate It

Two areas where automation is commonly overestimated:

1. **Editorial judgment** — agents can propose titles, but audience positioning must be encoded explicitly
2. **Exception handling** — guest name corrections, legal review concerns, sensitive anecdotes, sponsor placement constraints; automated systems tend to fail on edge cases

**Healthy mindset:** Use automation to accelerate repetitive transforms (transcription cleanup, first-draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, asset packaging, metadata prefill). Don't give up responsibility for what goes live.

> "Make the machine produce options. Make the human approve what becomes public." — Maya Chen

---

## [00:06:52] Titles: Strategy Over Frequency

Titles do significant work — they are a contract with the listener, not just a label. Recommended approach: generate several candidates from different angles.

Example title candidates for this episode:
- "How Small Teams Build a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline"
- "Why Podcast Production Breaks Even When the Recording Is Great"
- "From Raw Recording to Publish: The Systems Behind a Great Podcast"

The best choice depends on audience intent. Frequency of a phrase in the transcript is not a reliable signal for title quality.

---

## [00:08:13] Pre-Publish Review: Five High-Value Checks

Before any episode goes live:

1. **Names and affiliations** — verify guest name, title, and company
2. **Links and references** — confirm every promised link resolves to the correct destination
3. **Sponsor compliance** — required sponsor language is present where it should be
4. **Sensitive edits** — anything the guest wanted removed is actually removed
5. **Metadata consistency** — title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy all describe the same episode

---

## [00:09:13] Practical Takeaways

Steps to improve your pipeline this month:

1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. Create a publish checklist that one person owns
3. Separate raw transcript and cleaned transcript
4. Define the required output bundle for every episode
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to surface delays, confusion, and repeated errors

Host addition: if experimenting with automation, start by automating drafts — not approvals.
