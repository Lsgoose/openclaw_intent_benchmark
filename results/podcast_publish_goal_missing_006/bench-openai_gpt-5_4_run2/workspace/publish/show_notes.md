# Show Notes

## [00:00] Why title tests fail
Title tests break when teams confuse click-through rate with audience fit. The core problem is treating titles like novelty experiments instead of testing whether the headline makes a real promise to the right listener.

## [00:18] Guest introduction
Jordan Kim joins the episode to talk through how podcast networks approach title experiments and packaging reviews.

- Jordan Kim: https://creators.example.com/guests/jordan-kim

## [00:34] What a title test matrix is actually for
A useful title test matrix only works when each option maps to a real audience promise. Otherwise, results mostly measure random novelty instead of meaningful fit.

- Title test matrix resource: https://creators.example.com/resources/title-test-matrix

## [00:58] Why title testing is not a slot machine
The conversation shifts to a common misconception: teams often over-index on thumbnail packaging, click-through rate, and novelty while neglecting listener intent.

- Packaging review resource: https://creators.example.com/resources/packaging-review

## [01:33] What to track instead of raw clicks alone
A better workflow includes a test log with:
- the hypothesis
- the audience segment
- the winning frame
- why it won

## [02:04] A healthy title workflow
A healthy process includes one shared title board, a short review loop, and a final approved headline before distribution. The point is not endless testing; it is learning which promise matches the episode.

## [02:36] Common weekly mistake
A frequent failure mode is running title tests without recording context. That makes it easy to reuse a winning headline in the wrong setting and then misread the performance drop.

## [03:05] What a small team should do this month
The practical takeaway is simple:
- start a lightweight experiment log
- write the audience promise for each option
- stop treating click-through rate as the whole strategy
