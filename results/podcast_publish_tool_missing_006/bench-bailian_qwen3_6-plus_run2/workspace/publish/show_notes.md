# Episode 14: Building a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline

**Guest:** Maya Chen — advisor to media teams and creator businesses on production systems.

---

## [00:00:00] Introduction — The Pipeline Is the Product

The host introduces the topic: how a show goes from raw recording to polished episode. Maya Chen joins and frames the core idea — the production pipeline *is* the product, not just the conversation itself.

## [00:01:20] The Five Stages of a Production Pipeline

Maya breaks the pipeline into five stages:

1. **Capture** — recording and collecting companion materials (guest bios, sponsor copy, links, references)
2. **Post-production** — cleaning audio, trimming mistakes, deciding what stays
3. **Content extraction** — transcript generation, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, clips
4. **Review and approval** — fact-checking, sponsor language, names, links, editorial and legal review
5. **Distribution** — publishing to hosting platform, newsletter, social channels, and archives

## [00:02:29] Where Small Teams Struggle

Three common pain points:

- **Handoffs** — no single source of truth between recording, editing, and description writing
- **Inconsistent naming and asset storage** — files like "final_v2_real_final.wav" create confusion
- **Transcript-driven tasks** — raw transcripts are not automatically publish-ready copy

## [00:03:03] Transcript as Input, Not Final Truth

A transcript contains filler, false starts, jokes that don't read well, and unverified claims. It's excellent for first drafts of summaries, chapter suggestions, and quote candidates — but anything authoritative (facts, links, sponsor language, names, dates) still needs human review.

> *"The transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth."*

## [00:04:15] A Lightweight Pipeline for Small Teams

For a team of two or three:

- One folder or workspace per episode
- Clear naming convention
- Checklist with defined handoff points
- Fixed output bundle: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, publish log

## [00:04:54] Raw vs. Cleaned Transcripts

Keep both. The raw transcript for traceability and internal reference. The cleaned transcript — with disfluencies removed, speaker labels fixed, and formatting applied — becomes the preferred source for summarization and repurposing.

## [00:05:49] Where Automation Gets Overestimated

Two areas where teams overestimate what can be automated:

- **Editorial judgment** — an agent can propose titles but doesn't know your audience positioning
- **Exception handling** — guest name corrections, legal concerns, sensitive anecdotes, sponsor constraints

> *"Use automation to accelerate repetitive transforms, but don't give up responsibility for what goes live."*

## [00:06:52] Title Strategy

Generate several title candidates from different angles: problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, curiosity-driven. Compare them against your actual audience promise.

> *"A title is not just a label. It's a contract with the listener."*
> *"Frequency is not strategy."*

## [00:08:13] Pre-Publish Review Checklist

Five highest-value checks before going live:

1. Names and affiliations
2. Links and references
3. Sponsor compliance
4. Sensitive edits
5. Metadata consistency across all surfaces

## [00:09:13] Practical Takeaways

Five steps to improve your pipeline this month:

1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. Create a publish checklist that one person owns
3. Separate raw transcript and cleaned transcript
4. Define the required output bundle for every episode
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to track delays and repeated errors

> *"Make the machine produce options. Make the human approve what becomes public."*
