# Show Notes — Episode 14: Building a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline

## Timestamped Sections

**[00:00:00] Introduction**
Host introduces Maya Chen, media teams and creator businesses advisor on production systems.

**[00:01:20] The Five Stages of Production Pipeline**
Maya breaks down the five stages: capture (recording + companion materials), post-production (audio cleanup + editing decisions), content extraction (transcripts, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapters, titles), review and approval (facts, sponsor copy, names, links), and distribution (hosting, newsletter, social, archives).

**[00:02:29] Where Small Teams Struggle First**
Three common pain points: broken handoffs without a single source of truth; inconsistent naming and asset storage ("final_v2_real_final.wav"); and treating raw transcripts as publish-ready content when they contain filler, corrections, and unverified references.

**[00:04:15] A Lightweight but Reliable Pipeline for Small Teams**
One folder per episode, clear naming convention, a defined handoff checklist, and a fixed output bundle (audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapters, thumbnail, publish log). Maya recommends keeping raw and cleaned transcripts separate.

**[00:05:49] Automation: Acceleration, Not Abdication**
Automation excels at repetitive transforms: transcript cleanup, draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting. It fails on editorial judgment and edge cases like guest name corrections, legal reviews, or sponsor constraints. Maya's rule: automate drafts, not approvals.

**[00:06:52] Titles as Listener Contracts**
Generate title candidates from different angles: problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, curiosity-driven. Compare against audience intent. Frequency is not strategy — the most repeated phrase is rarely the most compelling framing.

**[00:08:13] The Five High-Value Pre-Publish Checks**
(1) Names and affiliations, (2) links and references, (3) sponsor compliance language, (4) sensitive edits removed, (5) metadata consistency across title, description, transcript, chapters, and social copy.

**[00:09:13] First Steps for Small Shows This Month**
(1) Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention. (2) Create a publish checklist owned by one person. (3) Separate raw and cleaned transcripts. (4) Define the required output bundle per episode. (5) Keep a lightweight episode log to spot recurring delays and errors.
