[00:00:00] Episode framing
- The episode opens on the idea that podcast production is more than editing audio; it shapes the listener experience from recording to release.
- The host and Maya Chen set up the core premise: the pipeline includes editing, packaging, labeling, and distribution.

[00:01:20] Major stages of the pipeline
- Capture: record the conversation and collect guest bios, sponsor copy, links, and references.
- Post-production: clean audio, trim mistakes, and decide what stays.
- Content extraction: create transcript, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, and clips.
- Review and approval: check facts, sponsor language, names, links, and legal or editorial issues.
- Distribution: send the finished package to hosting, newsletter, social, and archives.

[00:02:29] Where small teams struggle
- Handoffs become fragile when no one owns a single source of truth.
- File naming and asset storage can become inconsistent and confusing.
- Transcript-driven work still needs judgment; a transcript is not automatically publish-ready copy.

[00:03:09] Why transcripts need cleanup and review
- Raw transcripts can include filler, false starts, corrections, and unverified references.
- Transcripts work well for first-draft summaries, chapter suggestions, quote candidates, and social options.
- Facts, links, sponsor language, names, affiliations, dates, and claims still need human review.
- Quote pull: “rich input, not final truth.”

[00:04:26] A lightweight but reliable workflow
- Use one folder or workspace per episode.
- Keep a clear naming convention and a checklist with defined handoff points.
- Standardize the output bundle: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail if used, and a publish log.

[00:04:59] Raw transcript versus cleaned transcript
- Keep the raw transcript for traceability and internal reference.
- Produce a cleaned transcript for readability, speaker labels, and downstream summarization.
- Use the cleaned version as the preferred source for repurposing.

[00:05:57] Where automation helps, and where it doesn’t
- Automation is useful for transcription cleanup, first-draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, asset packaging, and metadata prefill.
- It is weak at editorial judgment and exception handling.
- Human ownership still matters for what goes live, especially in public-facing content.

[00:06:57] Title strategy
- Generate multiple title candidates: problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, and curiosity-driven.
- Titles should match audience intent, not just repeated phrases.
- A title is treated as a contract with the listener.

[00:08:20] Highest-value review checks before publish
- Verify names and affiliations.
- Verify links and references.
- Check sponsor compliance.
- Confirm sensitive edits were removed.
- Ensure metadata consistency across title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy.

[00:09:13] Practical next steps for a small show
- Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention.
- Create a publish checklist with one owner.
- Separate raw transcript and cleaned transcript.
- Define the required output bundle.
- Keep a lightweight episode log to spot delays and repeated errors.

[00:09:50] Closing takeaway
- Automate drafts, not approvals.
- Let the machine produce options, and keep the human responsible for what becomes public.
