## 2026-04-28 07:40 UTC — Episode opening and pipeline framing
- The host introduces the episode as a look at the podcast production pipeline: how a raw recording becomes a polished episode with clean audio, transcript, chapters, clips, title, description, and metadata.
- Maya Chen joins as a guide for production systems.
- Key framing: the pipeline is not just support work; it shapes the listener experience.

## 2026-04-28 07:40 UTC — What the pipeline includes
- Maya breaks the pipeline into five stages: capture, post-production, content extraction, review and approval, and distribution.
- Capture includes the recording plus companion materials such as guest bios, sponsor copy, links, and references.
- The process is compared to a software build pipeline: source material, transforms, validation, packaging, and deployment.

## 2026-04-28 07:40 UTC — Common failure points for small teams
- Small teams struggle with handoffs, inconsistent naming, and unclear asset storage.
- Transcript-driven tasks are especially fragile when teams treat the transcript as automatically publish-ready.
- Maya warns that raw transcripts include filler, corrections, jokes, and placeholder references that still need verification.

## 2026-04-28 07:40 UTC — Raw transcript vs cleaned transcript
- Maya recommends keeping both raw and cleaned transcripts.
- The raw transcript preserves traceability and lets the team verify wording.
- The cleaned transcript removes obvious disfluencies, fixes speaker labels, and becomes the preferred source for summarization and repurposing.
- The distinction matters even more when automation is involved, because downstream tools inherit source noise.

## 2026-04-28 07:40 UTC — What automation should and should not do
- Automation is useful for repetitive transforms: transcript cleanup, first-draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, asset packaging, and metadata prefill.
- It is weaker at editorial judgment and exception handling.
- Maya’s rule is to use automation for acceleration, not abdication: make the machine produce options and keep humans responsible for what goes live.

## 2026-04-28 07:40 UTC — Titles as audience contracts
- The conversation turns to title strategy, since titles do a lot of work in discovery.
- Maya recommends generating multiple candidates from different angles: problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, and curiosity-driven.
- She warns that frequency is not strategy; the most repeated phrase in a transcript is not necessarily the strongest framing.

## 2026-04-28 07:40 UTC — Review checklist before publish
- The highest-value prepublish checks are: names and affiliations, links and references, sponsor compliance, sensitive edits, and metadata consistency.
- The team should ensure the title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy all describe the same episode.
- A polished pipeline creates alignment across all public surfaces.

## 2026-04-28 07:40 UTC — Practical advice for small shows
- Maya’s recommended starting improvements: define a folder structure and naming convention, create an owned publish checklist, separate raw and cleaned transcripts, define a required output bundle, and keep a lightweight episode log.
- The closing takeaway is that consistency matters more than elaborate systems.
- The host adds a final principle: automate drafts, not approvals.
