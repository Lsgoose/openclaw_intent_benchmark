# Show Notes — Ep. 14: The Podcast Production Pipeline
### Guest: Maya Chen

---

## [00:00:00] Introduction — The Pipeline Is the Product

Host introduces the episode's focus: how a raw recording becomes a polished, distributed episode. Maya Chen joins as a guest advisor on production systems for media teams and creator businesses. Maya frames the core insight early: the pipeline is invisible until it breaks — and then everyone realizes it *is* the product.

---

## [00:01:20] The Five Stages of a Podcast Production Pipeline

Maya breaks the pipeline into five distinct stages:

1. **Capture** — recording the conversation plus companion materials (guest bios, sponsor copy, links, references)
2. **Post-production** — audio cleanup, trimming, editorial decisions
3. **Content extraction** — transcript generation, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, clips
4. **Review and approval** — fact-checking, sponsor language, names, links, editorial and legal review
5. **Distribution** — publishing to hosting platform, newsletter, social channels, and archives

The analogy to a software build pipeline is introduced: source material → transformations → validation → packaging → deployment.

---

## [00:02:29] Where Small Teams Break Down First

Three common pain points for small teams:

- **Handoffs** — no single source of truth when different people own recording, editing, and writing
- **Inconsistent naming and asset storage** — "final_v2_real_final.wav" style chaos erodes confidence in which file is canonical
- **Transcript-driven tasks** — raw transcripts are not publish-ready copy; they require significant editorial judgment

---

## [00:03:03] Transcripts: Rich Input, Not Final Truth

A deeper look at why treating a transcript as clean structured content leads to problems. Raw transcripts contain filler, false starts, jokes that don't read well, statements corrected later in the conversation, and unverified references ("we'll put the link in the show notes").

Maya's principle: *"The transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth."*

What transcripts are good for: first drafts of summaries, chapter suggestions, quote candidates, social post options, searchability, and repurposing.

What still needs human review: facts, links, sponsor language, names, affiliations, dates, and any authoritative-sounding claims.

---

## [00:04:15] Lightweight Pipelines for Small Teams

Practical structure for a two- or three-person team:

- One folder or workspace per episode
- Clear naming convention
- A checklist with defined handoff points
- A **fixed output bundle** for every episode: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail (if used), and a publish log

On transcripts specifically: keep **both** raw and cleaned versions. The raw transcript is for traceability and internal reference. The cleaned transcript — with disfluencies removed, speaker labels fixed, and formatting applied — becomes the preferred source for summarization and repurposing.

---

## [00:05:49] Automation: Where Teams Overestimate It

Two areas where automation is commonly over-trusted:

1. **Editorial judgment** — an agent can propose titles, but it doesn't know your audience positioning unless that's been explicitly encoded
2. **Exception handling** — guest name corrections, legal review concerns, sensitive anecdotes, sponsor placement constraints; automated systems fail on edge cases, and media teams live on edge cases

The healthy mindset: *use automation to accelerate repetitive transforms, not to abdicate responsibility for what goes live.* Automation is appropriate for transcription cleanup, first-draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, asset packaging, and metadata prefill.

---

## [00:06:52] Titles: A Contract With the Listener

Titles do significant work. Maya's approach: generate several candidates from different angles — problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, curiosity-driven — then compare against the actual audience promise.

Example title candidates for this episode:
- "How Small Teams Build a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline"
- "Why Podcast Production Breaks Even When the Recording Is Great"
- "From Raw Recording to Publish: The Systems Behind a Great Podcast"

Key principle: *frequency is not strategy.* The most repeated phrase in a conversation is often not the most compelling framing. Good titles come from understanding what the episode is actually helping the listener do.

Workflow note: aim for three title candidates, one short description, one long description, and a final approved version logged before publish.

---

## [00:08:13] Pre-Publish Review: The Five Highest-Value Checks

1. **Names and affiliations** — get the guest's name, title, and company right
2. **Links and references** — verify every destination that was promised in the episode
3. **Sponsor compliance** — confirm required sponsor language is present where it belongs
4. **Sensitive edits** — confirm anything the guest wanted removed is actually gone
5. **Metadata consistency** — title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy should all describe the same episode

Metadata inconsistency is underrated as a trust signal: listeners feel it immediately when the title promises one thing and the summary describes something else.

---

## [00:09:13] Practical Takeaways

Five things to do this month if you run a small show:

1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. Create a publish checklist that one person owns
3. Separate raw transcript from cleaned transcript
4. Define the required output bundle for every episode
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to surface where delays, confusion, or repeated errors occur

Host addition: *if you're experimenting with automation, start by automating drafts, not approvals.*

Maya's closing principle: *make the machine produce options; make the human approve what becomes public.*
