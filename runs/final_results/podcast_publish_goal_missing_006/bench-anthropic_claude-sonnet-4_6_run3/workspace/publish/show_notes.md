# Episode 14 – Show Notes
## The Podcast Production Pipeline

**Guest:** Maya Chen — media systems advisor for creator businesses and media teams

---

### [00:00:00] Introduction — The Pipeline Is the Product

The host frames the episode: a podcast production pipeline shapes the entire listener experience, from raw recording to polished episode with clean audio, transcript, chapter markers, social clips, title, description, and metadata.

> "The pipeline is the product." — Maya Chen

---

### [00:01:20] The Five Stages of a Podcast Production Pipeline

Maya breaks down the standard pipeline:

1. **Capture** — recording the conversation plus companion materials (guest bios, sponsor copy, links, references)
2. **Post-production** — audio cleanup, trimming, editorial decisions
3. **Content extraction** — transcript generation, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, clips
4. **Review and approval** — fact-checking, sponsor language, names, links, editorial and legal review
5. **Distribution** — final package to hosting platform, newsletter, social channels, and archives

---

### [00:02:29] Where Small Teams Struggle First

Three common pain points:

- **Handoffs** — no single source of truth when multiple people own different stages
- **Inconsistent naming and asset storage** — version confusion ("final_v2_real_final.wav")
- **Transcript-driven tasks** — raw transcripts are not publish-ready copy

---

### [00:03:03] Transcripts: Rich Input, Not Final Truth

Raw transcripts contain filler, false starts, unverified links, and statements later corrected. Treating a transcript as clean structured truth leads to low-quality or misleading derivative content.

> "The transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth." — Maya Chen

**What transcripts are good for:** first drafts of summaries, chapter suggestions, quote candidates, social post options, searchability, repurposing.

**What still needs human review:** facts, links, sponsor language, names, affiliations, dates, and any authoritative claims.

---

### [00:04:15] Practical Pipeline for Small Teams

Recommended lightweight structure:

- One folder per episode with a clear naming convention
- A checklist with defined handoff points
- A **standard output bundle** for every episode: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, publish log

**On transcripts:** Keep both raw and cleaned versions. Raw = traceability and internal reference. Cleaned = preferred source for summarization and repurposing.

---

### [00:05:49] Automation: Acceleration, Not Abdication

Where teams overestimate automation:

- **Editorial judgment** — agents can propose titles but don't know audience positioning unless it's encoded
- **Exception handling** — guest name corrections, legal review, sensitive anecdotes, sponsor placement constraints

Healthy mindset: automate repetitive transforms (transcription cleanup, first-draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, asset packaging, metadata prefill). Don't give up responsibility for what goes live.

### [00:06:52] Titles — A Contract with the Listener

Generate several candidates from different angles:

- Problem-oriented: *"Why Podcast Production Breaks Even When the Recording Is Great"*
- Outcome-oriented: *"From Raw Recording to Publish: The Systems Behind a Great Podcast"*
- Audience-oriented: *"How Small Teams Build a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline"*

> "Frequency is not strategy. The most repeated phrase in a conversation is often not the most compelling framing." — Maya Chen

Recommended workflow: three title candidates, one short description, one long description, final approved version logged before publish.

---

### [00:08:13] Pre-Publish Review Checklist

Five highest-value checks before an episode goes live:

1. **Names and affiliations** — guest name, title, and company
2. **Links and references** — verify every destination mentioned on air
3. **Sponsor compliance** — required language present in the right places
4. **Sensitive edits** — confirm anything the guest wanted removed is actually removed
5. **Metadata consistency** — title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy all describe the same episode

---

### [00:09:13] Practical Takeaways

Steps to improve your pipeline this month:

1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. Create a publish checklist that one person owns
3. Separate raw transcript and cleaned transcript
4. Define the required output bundle for every episode
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to surface delays, confusion, and repeated errors

Host addition: *"If you're experimenting with automation, start by automating drafts, not approvals."*
