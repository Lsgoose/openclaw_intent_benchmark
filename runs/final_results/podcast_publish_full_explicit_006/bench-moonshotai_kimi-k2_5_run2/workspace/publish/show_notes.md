## [00:00:00] Introduction: The Pipeline Is the Product
The episode opens with the core thesis: podcast production isn't just operational overhead—it shapes the entire listener experience. The pipeline transforms raw recordings into polished episodes with clean audio, transcripts, chapter markers, social clips, titles, descriptions, and metadata.

## [00:00:47] Why Production Discipline Matters
Even small teams benefit from production discipline. Poor titles reduce clicks, sloppy transcripts hurt downstream content generation, inaccurate chapter markers hinder navigation, and inconsistent publishing checklists let small mistakes stack up.

## [00:01:27] The Five Stages of a Production Pipeline
1. **Capture** – Recording and collecting companion materials (bios, sponsor copy, links)
2. **Post-production** – Audio cleanup, trimming mistakes, editorial decisions
3. **Content extraction** – Transcripts, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapters, titles, clips
4. **Review and approval** – Fact-checking, sponsor language verification, legal review
5. **Distribution** – Publishing to hosting platforms, newsletters, social channels, archives

## [00:02:29] Common Pain Points for Small Teams
- **Handoffs** – No single source of truth when multiple people touch the episode
- **Inconsistent naming** – Files like "final_v2_real_final.wav" create confusion
- **Transcript assumptions** – Raw transcripts are not automatically publish-ready copy

## [00:03:40] Transcripts as Input, Not Truth
Transcripts excel at generating first drafts (summaries, chapters, quotes, social posts) and enabling searchability. However, authoritative claims—facts, links, sponsor language, names, affiliations, dates—require human review. The transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth.

## [00:04:26] Lightweight Pipeline for Small Teams
Maya recommends: one folder per episode, clear naming conventions, defined handoff checklists, and a standardized output bundle (final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapters, thumbnail, publish log).

## [00:04:59] Raw vs. Clean Transcripts
Keep raw transcripts for traceability. Produce cleaned transcripts that remove disfluencies, fix speaker labels, and format conversations for readability. Cleaned versions become the preferred source for summarization and repurposing.

## [00:05:57] Automation Boundaries
Teams often overestimate automation in two areas: editorial judgment (agents don't know your audience positioning) and exception handling (edge cases like name corrections, legal reviews, sensitive anecdotes, and sponsor constraints). Automation accelerates repetitive transforms—don't abdicate responsibility for what goes live.

## [00:06:57] Crafting Effective Titles
Generate candidates from multiple angles: problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, curiosity-driven. Compare against your audience promise. A title is a contract with the listener—frequency is not strategy.

## [00:08:20] Pre-Publication Review Checklist
1. Names and affiliations
2. Links and references (verify actual destinations)
3. Sponsor compliance
4. Sensitive edits (confirm requested removals)
5. Metadata consistency across all surfaces

## [00:09:23] Practical Takeaways for This Month
- Define standard episode folder structure and naming
- Create a publish checklist with one owner
- Separate raw and cleaned transcripts
- Define required output bundle for every episode
- Keep a lightweight episode log to track delays and errors

## [00:09:50] Final Advice on Automation
Start by automating drafts, not approvals. Let machines produce options; let humans approve what becomes public.
