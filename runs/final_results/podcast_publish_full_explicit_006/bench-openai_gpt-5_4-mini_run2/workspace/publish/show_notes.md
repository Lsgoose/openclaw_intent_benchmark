# Episode 14 Show Notes

## [00:00:00] Opening: why the pipeline shapes the listener experience
The episode starts with the idea that the podcast production pipeline is more than operations — it shapes the whole listener experience, from raw recording to polished release.

## [00:00:27] Guest introduction
Maya Chen joins to discuss production systems for media teams and creator businesses.

## [00:00:47] The pipeline is the product
The conversation frames editing, packaging, labeling, and distribution as part of the product itself, not just the conversation.

## [00:01:20] The five major stages of a podcast pipeline
Maya breaks the pipeline into five stages:
- capture
- post-production
- content extraction
- review and approval
- distribution

## [00:02:29] Common small-team failure points
Small teams often struggle with handoffs, inconsistent file naming, and assuming transcript-driven work is automatically publish-ready.

## [00:03:03] Why transcripts are rich inputs, not final truth
Raw transcripts can include filler, false starts, corrected statements, and unverified link references, so they should not be treated as final editorial truth.

## [00:03:40] What transcripts are good for
Transcripts are strong inputs for first-draft summaries, chapter ideas, quote candidates, social options, searchability, and repurposing. Facts, links, names, sponsor language, and claims still need review.

## [00:04:26] A lightweight but reliable workflow
A dependable small-team system includes one episode workspace, clear naming, a checklist with handoffs, and a fixed output bundle: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, and publish log.

## [00:04:54] Clean transcript vs. raw transcript
Keep both. The raw transcript preserves traceability; the cleaned transcript becomes the preferred source for summarization and repurposing.

## [00:05:49] Where automation helps and where it fails
Automation is best for repetitive transforms like cleanup, first-draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, and metadata prefill. It is weaker at editorial judgment and exception handling.

## [00:06:26] Automation should accelerate, not replace ownership
Use automation to speed up repetitive work, but keep human ownership of correctness, tone, and release quality.

## [00:06:57] Title strategy
Generate several title candidates from different angles — problem, guest, outcome, and curiosity — then choose the one that best matches the audience promise.

## [00:08:13] Pre-publication review checklist
High-value checks before publishing include names and affiliations, links and references, sponsor compliance, sensitive edits, and metadata consistency across title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy.

## [00:09:23] Practical takeaways for the month ahead
The suggested first improvements are: define a standard folder structure, create an owned publish checklist, separate raw and cleaned transcripts, define the required output bundle, and keep a lightweight episode log.

## [00:09:50] Automate drafts, not approvals
The episode closes with the principle that machines should produce options, while humans approve what becomes public.
