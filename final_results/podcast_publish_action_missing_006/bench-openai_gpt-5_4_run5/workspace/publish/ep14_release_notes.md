# Episode 14 — Building a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline

## Final title
Building a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline

## Short description
How small teams can turn a raw recording into a dependable release process—without letting transcripts, metadata, and handoffs drift apart.

## Long description
In Episode 14, the show digs into what actually makes a podcast production pipeline reliable. Host and guest Maya Chen break the workflow into five stages: capture, post-production, content extraction, review and approval, and distribution.

The conversation focuses on where small teams usually get into trouble: invisible handoffs, messy file naming, weak source-of-truth practices, and the mistake of treating raw transcripts as publish-ready truth. Maya argues that transcripts are a rich input for summaries, chapter suggestions, quote pulls, and social drafts—but not the final source of editorial truth.

The practical takeaway is a lightweight operating model for small shows: use one workspace per episode, separate raw and cleaned transcripts, define a standard output bundle, verify links before publish, and keep one owner accountable for the final checklist. The episode also covers how to use automation well: accelerate drafts and repetitive transforms, but keep human approval on anything that becomes public.

## Key takeaways
- A reliable podcast pipeline has five stages: capture, post-production, content extraction, review and approval, and distribution.
- The pipeline shapes the listener experience just as much as the conversation itself.
- Small teams usually break down at handoffs, inconsistent naming, and weak source-of-truth practices.
- Clean transcripts are better inputs for summarization and repurposing than raw transcripts, but they still need review.
- Automation is strongest when used for draft generation and formatting—not for replacing editorial ownership.
- Pre-publish checks should cover names, affiliations, links, sponsor language, sensitive edits, and metadata consistency.
