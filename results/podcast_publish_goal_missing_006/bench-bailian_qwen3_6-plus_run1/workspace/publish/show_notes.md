## [00:00:00] Introduction — The Pipeline Is the Product

The host introduces the episode's topic: the podcast production pipeline — everything that happens between a raw recording and a published episode. Maya Chen joins and frames the core idea: the pipeline itself is the product, not just the conversation.

## [00:01:20] The Five Stages of a Podcast Pipeline

Maya breaks the pipeline into five stages:

1. **Capture** — recording and collecting companion materials (bios, sponsor copy, links, references)
2. **Post-production** — cleaning audio, trimming mistakes, deciding what stays
3. **Content extraction** — transcripts, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, clips
4. **Review and approval** — fact-checking, sponsor language, names, links, editorial and legal review
5. **Distribution** — publishing to hosting platforms, newsletters, social channels, and archives

The pipeline is compared to a software build pipeline: source material, transformations, validation, packaging, and deployment.

## [00:02:29] Where Small Teams Struggle

Three common pain points:

- **Handoffs** — no single source of truth between recording, editing, and description writing
- **Inconsistent naming and asset storage** — files like "final_v2_real_final.wav"
- **Transcript-driven tasks** — raw transcripts are not automatically publish-ready

## [00:03:03] Transcript as Content — Raw vs. Cleaned

Maya explains why treating a raw transcript as clean structured truth leads to low-quality or misleading derivative content. Transcripts contain filler, false starts, jokes that don't read well, and unverified links.

**Key quote:** "The transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth."

Recommendation: keep both raw and cleaned transcripts. The cleaned version becomes the preferred source for summarization and repurposing; the raw version remains useful for traceability.

## [00:04:15] Lightweight Pipeline for Small Teams

For teams of two or three:

- One folder or workspace per episode
- Clear naming convention
- Checklist with defined handoff points
- Fixed output bundle: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, publish log

## [00:05:49] Automation — Acceleration, Not Abdication

Where teams overestimate automation:

- **Editorial judgment** — agents can propose titles but don't know audience positioning
- **Exception handling** — guest name corrections, legal concerns, sensitive anecdotes, sponsor constraints

Healthy mindset: use automation for repetitive transforms (transcription cleanup, first-draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, asset packaging, metadata prefill), but keep human ownership of what goes live.

## [00:06:52] Title Strategy

Generate several title candidates from different angles: problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, curiosity-driven. Compare against audience promise.

**Key insight:** "Frequency is not strategy." The most repeated phrase in a conversation is often not the most compelling framing.

## [00:08:13] Pre-Publish Review Checklist

Five highest-value checks:

1. Names and affiliations
2. Links and references
3. Sponsor compliance
4. Sensitive edits
5. Metadata consistency — title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy should all describe the same episode

## [00:09:13] Practical Takeaways

Five steps to improve a podcast pipeline this month:

1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. Create a publish checklist that one person owns
3. Separate raw transcript and cleaned transcript
4. Define the required output bundle for every episode
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to track delays, confusion, or repeated errors

**Additional takeaway:** If experimenting with automation, start by automating drafts, not approvals. "Make the machine produce options. Make the human approve what becomes public."
