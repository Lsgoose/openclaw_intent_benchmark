# Building a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline (Episode 14)

How does a raw recording turn into a polished episode with clean audio, a readable transcript, chapter markers, social clips, and metadata that actually helps listeners find what they need? In this episode, we unpack the systems behind a reliable podcast production pipeline.

Host sits down with production systems advisor **Maya Chen** to break down how small teams can design a simple, dependable workflowwithout needing a big media operation.

## In this episode

- Why your production pipeline is part of the product, not just back-office ops.
- The five core stages of a podcast pipeline: capture, post-production, content extraction, review & approval, and distribution.
- How to avoid fragile workflows built on memory, heroics, and "final_v2_real_final.wav" file names.
- Why maintaining **both** raw and cleaned transcripts makes every downstream task easier.
- Where automation shines (transforms, drafts, packaging) and where you still need human editorial judgment.
- How to treat titles as a contract with your listeners rather than an afterthought.
- The review checks that catch the highest-impact mistakes before publish.

## Practical takeaways

By the end of the episode, youll have a concrete checklist for improving your own pipeline:

1. Define a standard folder structure and naming convention for every episode.
2. Create a publish checklist that one person owns end-to-end.
3. Keep both raw and cleaned transcripts, and be explicit about which one downstream tools should use.
4. Standardize your output bundle (final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, and publish log).
5. Maintain a lightweight episode log so you can see where delays and repeated errors are happening.

## Notable ideas

- A transcript is a **rich input**, not the final source of editorial truth.
- Automation works best when it proposes options; humans should decide what goes public.
- Titles do more than summarizethey set expectations and frame the value of the episode.
