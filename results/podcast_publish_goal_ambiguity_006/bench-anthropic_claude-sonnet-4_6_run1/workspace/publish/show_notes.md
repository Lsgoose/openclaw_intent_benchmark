# Show Notes — Ep. 14: The Podcast Production Pipeline (with Maya Chen)

## [00:00:00] Introduction
Host introduces the episode topic: how a podcast goes from raw recording to a polished, distributed episode. Guest Maya Chen, who advises media teams and creator businesses on production systems, joins the conversation.

## [00:00:47] The Pipeline Is the Product
Maya frames the core insight: the production pipeline isn't just operational overhead — it shapes the entire listener experience. Vague titles, sloppy transcripts, misaligned chapter markers, and inconsistent checklists all compound into a degraded product.

## [00:01:20] The Five Stages of a Podcast Production Pipeline
Maya breaks down the five stages:
1. **Capture** — recording plus companion materials (guest bios, sponsor copy, links, references)
2. **Post-production** — audio cleanup, trimming, editorial decisions
3. **Content extraction** — transcripts, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, clips
4. **Review and approval** — fact-checking, sponsor language, names, links, editorial/legal review
5. **Distribution** — hosting platform, newsletter, social channels, archives

The software build pipeline analogy: source material → transformations → validation → packaging → deployment.

## [00:02:29] Where Small Teams Struggle First
Three common pain points:
- **Handoffs** — no single source of truth when multiple people own different stages
- **Inconsistent naming and asset storage** — "final_v2_real_final.wav" syndrome
- **Transcript-driven tasks** — raw transcripts are not publish-ready copy

## [00:03:03] Transcripts Are Rich Inputs, Not Final Truth
A raw transcript can contain filler, false starts, jokes that don't read well, corrected statements, and unverified references. Treating it as clean structured truth leads to low-quality or misleading derivative content.

What transcripts are good for: first drafts of summaries, chapter suggestions, quote candidates, social post options, searchability, and repurposing.

What still needs human review: facts, links, sponsor language, names, affiliations, dates, and any authoritative claims.

> "The transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth." — Maya Chen

## [00:04:15] A Lightweight Pipeline for Small Teams
Practical structure for a two- or three-person team:
- One folder or workspace per episode
- Clear naming convention
- Checklist with defined handoff points
- Fixed **output bundle** for every episode: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, publish log

## [00:04:54] Raw vs. Clean Transcript
Keep both:
- **Raw transcript** — for traceability and internal reference
- **Clean transcript** — disfluencies removed, speaker labels fixed, formatted for readability; preferred source for summarization and repurposing

Separating them prevents downstream tools and teammates from inheriting source noise.

## [00:05:49] Automation: Where Teams Overestimate It
Two areas where automation is commonly over-trusted:
1. **Editorial judgment** — agents can propose titles but don't know audience positioning unless it's been encoded
2. **Exception handling** — guest name corrections, legal review concerns, sensitive anecdotes, sponsor placement constraints; automated systems fail on edge cases, and media teams live on edge cases

Healthy mindset: **use automation to accelerate, not to abdicate**. Automate repetitive transforms (transcription cleanup, first-draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, asset packaging, metadata prefill). Keep human ownership over what goes live.

## [00:06:52] Titles: A Contract with the Listener

Frequency is not strategy — the most repeated phrase in a conversation is rarely the most compelling framing.

## [00:08:13] Pre-Publish Review: The Five Highest-Value Checks
1. **Names and affiliations** — guest name, title, company
2. **Links and references** — verify actual destinations for anything promised in the show
3. **Sponsor compliance** — required language present in the right places
4. **Sensitive edits** — confirm removed content is actually gone
5. **Metadata consistency** — title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy all describe the same episode

## [00:09:13] Practical Takeaways for Small Shows
Steps to improve your pipeline this month:
1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. Create a publish checklist with a single owner
3. Separate raw transcript from clean transcript
4. Define the required output bundle for every episode
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to surface delays, confusion, and repeated errors

Host addition: if experimenting with automation, **automate drafts, not approvals**.

> "Make the machine produce options. Make the human approve what becomes public." — Maya Chen
