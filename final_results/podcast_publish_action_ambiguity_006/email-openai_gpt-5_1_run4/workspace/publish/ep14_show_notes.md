# Episode 14 – How Small Teams Build a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline

**Guest:** Maya Chen, production systems advisor for media teams and creator businesses  
**Sponsor:** SoundLayer – collaboration for small media teams to organize transcripts, versions, and reviews

## Episode Summary

This episode explores how to design a podcast production pipeline that small teams can actually trust.

Host and guest **Maya Chen** walk through the five core stages of a production pipeline—capture, post‑production, content extraction, review/approval, and distribution—and explain why "the pipeline is the product." A strong system reduces errors, keeps your metadata consistent, and makes every episode easier to ship.

- Why listeners experience the *pipeline*, not just the conversation
- The five key stages of a podcast production pipeline
- Common failure points: handoffs, version chaos, and over‑reliance on raw transcripts
- Why a transcript is a rich input, not a final source of editorial truth
- The value of keeping both raw and cleaned transcripts
- How to define a clear output bundle for every episode
- Using automation for acceleration instead of abdication
- Title strategy: framing episodes around audience intent
- High‑impact checks before you hit publish

### Simple Pipeline Blueprint (for 2–3 person teams)

1. **Standardize the episode folder**  
   One workspace per episode, with predictable names for audio, transcripts, and notes.

2. **Define clear handoffs**  
   Decide who owns recording, editing, notes, and final approval—write it down as a checklist.

3. **Separate transcripts**  
   Keep both a raw transcript (for traceability) and a cleaned transcript (for anything public or repurposed).

4. **Standardize your output bundle**  
   Every episode should end up with: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail (if used), and a publish log.

5. **Automate drafts, not approvals**  
   Use tools and agents to generate first‑draft summaries, chapter suggestions, and social copy—but keep humans in the loop for correctness and edge cases.

6. **Log what happened**  
   Keep a simple episode log so you can see where delays and repeated errors occur.
