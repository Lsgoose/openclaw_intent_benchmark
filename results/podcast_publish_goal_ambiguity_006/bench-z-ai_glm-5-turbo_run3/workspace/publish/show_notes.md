# Show Notes — Episode 14: The Podcast Production Pipeline
**Guest:** Maya Chen — Media production systems advisor
**Sponsor:** SoundLayer — Collaboration tool for small media teams

---

## [00:00:00] Introduction

The host opens by framing the episode's topic: the podcast production pipeline — the full journey from raw recording to polished, published episode. Maya Chen joins to discuss why the pipeline itself is the product, not just the conversation.

## [00:01:20] The Five Stages of a Podcast Pipeline

Maya breaks production into five stages:

1. **Capture** — Recording the conversation and collecting companion materials (guest bios, sponsor copy, links, references).
2. **Post-production** — Cleaning audio, trimming mistakes, deciding what stays.
3. **Content extraction** — Transcript generation, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, clips.
4. **Review and approval** — Fact-checking, sponsor language, names, links, editorial and legal review.
5. **Distribution** — Publishing to hosting platform, newsletter, social channels, and archives.

Maya draws a parallel to software build pipelines: source material, transformations, validation, packaging, and deployment.

## [00:02:29] Common Pain Points for Small Teams

Three recurring struggles:

- **Handoffs** — No single source of truth when multiple people touch the episode.
- **Naming and storage** — Files like "final_v2_real_final.wav" erode confidence in which version is canonical.
- **Transcript-driven tasks** — A raw transcript is not automatically publish-ready copy.

## [00:03:03] The Transcript Trap

Maya warns against treating transcripts as clean structured truth. Raw transcripts contain filler, false starts, jokes that don't read well, corrected statements, and unverified link promises. Publishing derivative content directly from raw transcripts leads to low-quality or misleading output.

> "The transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth."

## [00:03:40] What to Automate vs. What to Review

**Good automation candidates:** First-draft summaries, chapter suggestions, quote candidates, social post options, searchability, repurposing.

**Still needs human review:** Facts, links, sponsor language, names, affiliations, dates, claims — anything authoritative.

## [00:04:26] A Lightweight but Reliable Pipeline

Maya's recommendations for small teams (2–3 people):

- One folder or workspace per episode.
- Clear naming convention.
- Checklist with defined handoff points.
- Fixed output bundle: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, publish log.

## [00:04:54] Raw vs. Clean Transcripts

Maya recommends keeping both:

- **Raw transcript** — For traceability and internal reference; useful for verifying original wording.
- **Cleaned transcript** — Disfluencies removed, speaker labels fixed, formatted for readability; the preferred source for summarization and repurposing.

Without this separation, downstream systems inherit every mess from the source — leading to awkward summaries, broken quote cards, and misleading snippets.

## [00:05:49] Automation: Where Teams Overestimate

Two common overestimations:

1. **Editorial judgment** — An agent can propose titles but doesn't know audience positioning unless encoded.
2. **Exception handling** — Edge cases (name corrections, legal concerns, sensitive anecdotes, sponsor constraints) are where fully automated systems fail, and media teams live on edge cases.

> "Use automation to accelerate repetitive transforms. But don't give up responsibility for what goes live."

## [00:06:52] Title Strategy

Maya's approach: generate several title candidates from different angles — problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, curiosity-driven — then compare against audience promise.

> "A title is not just a label. It's a contract with the listener."

> "Frequency is not strategy. The most repeated phrase in a conversation is often not the most compelling framing."

## [00:08:13] Pre-Publish Review Checklist

Five high-priority checks:

1. **Names and affiliations** — Guest name, title, and company correct.
2. **Links and references** — Verify any "we'll link that" promises.
3. **Sponsor compliance** — Required sponsor language present.
4. **Sensitive edits** — Guest-requested removals confirmed.
5. **Metadata consistency** — Title, description, transcript, chapters, and social copy all describe the same episode.

## [00:09:13] Practical Takeaways

Maya's five-step improvement plan for small shows:

1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention.
2. Create a publish checklist that one person owns.
3. Separate raw transcript and cleaned transcript.
4. Define the required output bundle for every episode.
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to track delays, confusion, and repeated errors.

Host's addition: if experimenting with automation, start by automating drafts, not approvals.

> "Make the machine produce options. Make the human approve what becomes public."
