## Timestamps

### [00:00:00] Introduction
The pipeline is the product — how a raw recording becomes a polished, findable episode with clean audio, transcripts, chapter markers, social clips, and metadata.

### [00:01:20] The Five Stages of a Podcast Production Pipeline
1. **Capture** — recording plus companion materials (guest bios, sponsor copy, links, references)
2. **Post-production** — audio cleanup, trimming, editorial decisions
3. **Content extraction** — transcripts, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, clips
4. **Review and approval** — fact-checking, sponsor language, names, links, editorial/legal review
5. **Distribution** — hosting platform, newsletter, social channels, archives

### [00:02:07] The Software Build Analogy
A podcast pipeline mirrors a software build: source material, transformations, validation, packaging, and deployment. Pipelines become fragile when they depend on memory and heroics.

### [00:02:29] Where Small Teams Struggle
- **Handoffs** — no single source of truth across roles
- **Naming and storage** — files like "final_v2_real_final.wav" erode confidence
- **Transcript-driven tasks** — raw transcripts are not publish-ready copy

### [00:03:03] Transcript ≠ Content
A raw transcript contains filler, false starts, jokes that don't read, corrected statements, and unverified link promises. Treating it as clean structured truth produces low-quality or misleading derivative content.

> "The transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth." — Maya Chen

### [00:03:40] What to Automate vs. What to Review
**Good automation inputs:** first-draft summaries, chapter suggestions, quote candidates, social post options, searchability.
**Still needs human review:** facts, links, sponsor language, names, affiliations, dates, claims.

### [00:04:26] A Lightweight but Reliable Pipeline
- One folder or workspace per episode
- Clear naming convention
- Checklist with defined handoff points
- Fixed output bundle: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, publish log

### [00:04:54] Raw vs. Cleaned Transcripts
Keep both. The raw transcript preserves traceability; the cleaned version removes disfluencies, fixes speaker labels, and becomes the preferred source for summarization and repurposing.

### [00:05:49] Where Teams Overestimate Automation
1. **Editorial judgment** — agents don't know audience positioning unless encoded
2. **Exception handling** — name corrections, legal concerns, sensitive anecdotes, sponsor constraints

> "Use automation to accelerate repetitive transforms. But don't give up responsibility for what goes live." — Maya Chen

### [00:06:52] Title Strategy
Generate candidates from multiple angles: problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, curiosity-driven. Compare against audience promise — a title is a contract with the listener.

> "Frequency is not strategy. The most repeated phrase is often not the most compelling framing." — Maya Chen

### [00:08:13] Five Pre-Publish Checks
1. Names and affiliations
2. Links and references
3. Sponsor compliance
4. Sensitive edits (guest removal requests)
5. Metadata consistency across title, description, transcript, chapters, and social copy

### [00:09:13] Practical Takeaways — Improve Your Pipeline This Month
1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. Create a publish checklist that one person owns
3. Separate raw transcript and cleaned transcript
4. Define the required output bundle for every episode
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to spot delays and repeated errors

> "Automate drafts, not approvals. Make the machine produce options. Make the human approve what becomes public." — Maya Chen
