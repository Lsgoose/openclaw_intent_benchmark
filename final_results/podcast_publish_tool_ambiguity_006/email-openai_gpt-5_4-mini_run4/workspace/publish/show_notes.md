# Episode 14 Show Notes

## 00:00 — Why the pipeline is the product
The episode opens by framing the podcast production pipeline as more than operations: it shapes the listener experience from raw recording to polished release.

## 00:01:27 — Five stages of production
Maya breaks the pipeline into five stages: capture, post-production, content extraction, review and approval, and distribution.

## 00:02:29 — Common failure points in small teams
Small teams struggle with handoffs, inconsistent naming and storage, and overtrusting transcripts as if they were already publish-ready.

## 00:03:40 — What transcripts can and cannot do
Transcripts are strong inputs for summaries, chapter ideas, quotes, and searchability, but facts, links, sponsor language, names, dates, and claims still need human review.

## 00:04:26 — A lightweight but reliable setup
A simple episode folder, clear naming, a defined checklist, and a fixed output bundle make downstream work easier and more consistent.

## 00:04:59 — Raw transcript versus cleaned transcript
Keep both versions: raw for traceability, cleaned for readable summarization and repurposing.

## 00:05:57 — Where automation helps and where it fails
Automation is useful for repetitive transforms, but editorial judgment and exception handling still need a human owner.

## 00:06:57 — Titles as a contract
Good title candidates should be explored from different angles because the title is a promise to the listener, not just a label.

## 00:08:20 — Highest-value review checks
Before publish, verify names and affiliations, links and references, sponsor compliance, sensitive edits, and metadata consistency across all surfaces.

## 00:09:23 — Practical next steps for small shows
Standardize folder structure, assign checklist ownership, separate raw and cleaned transcripts, define the output bundle, and keep a lightweight episode log.

## 00:10:05 — Sponsor and close
The episode closes with the sponsor mention for SoundLayer and a reminder to share the episode with the teammate who owns editing, publishing, or content ops.
