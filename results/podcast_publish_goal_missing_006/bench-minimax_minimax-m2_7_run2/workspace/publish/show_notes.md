# Show Notes — Episode 14: Podcast Production Pipeline

## Timestamped Sections

**[00:00] Introduction — The Pipeline Is the Product**
Guest Maya Chen frames production systems as the invisible infrastructure that makes or breaks the listener experience. The host previews the episode's scope: recording through to polished, published episode.

**[00:27] Guest Introduction**
Maya Chen joins. She advises media teams and creator businesses on production systems and workflow design.

**[01:20] The Five Stages of a Podcast Production Pipeline**
Maya breaks production into five stages: capture → post-production → content extraction → review and approval → distribution. Compares the model to a software build pipeline where source material goes through transformations, validation, packaging, and deployment.

**[02:29] Where Small Teams Struggle First**
Three primary pain points: handoffs without a single source of truth; inconsistent naming and asset storage ("final_v2_real_final.wav"); and treating raw transcript as publish-ready copy.

**[03:03] Transcript Quality: Rich Input, Not Final Truth**
A raw transcript contains filler, false starts, jokes that don't read well, and unverified references. Maya introduces the key principle: transcripts are excellent inputs for first drafts, but anything authoritative — facts, links, sponsor language, names, dates, claims — requires human review before publishing.

**[04:26] A Lightweight Pipeline for Small Teams**
For teams of two to three: one folder per episode, clear naming convention, a defined handoff checklist, and a fixed output bundle (audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, and publish log). Standardized outputs make everything downstream easier.

**[05:49] What Small Teams Overestimate in Automation**
Two main overestimate areas: editorial judgment (agents can propose but don't know audience positioning) and exception handling (guest corrections, legal concerns, sensitive anecdotes, sponsor constraints). Most episodes are routine; edge cases break fully automated systems. Healthy mindset: use automation for acceleration, not abdication.

**[06:52] Titles Do Real Work**
A title is a contract with the listener, not just a label. Maya walks through generating candidates from different angles: problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, and curiosity-driven. Key insight: frequency is not strategy — the most repeated phrase is often not the most compelling framing.

**[08:20] The Five Highest-Value Pre-Publish Checks**
Maya's priority checklist: (1) Names and affiliations, (2) links and references, (3) sponsor compliance language, (4) sensitive edits and guest removal requests, (5) metadata consistency across title, description, transcript, chapters, and social copy.

**[09:13] Practical Takeaway: Where to Start This Month**
Action steps: define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention; create a publish checklist owned by one person; separate raw and cleaned transcripts; define the required output bundle per episode; keep a lightweight episode log to spot recurring delays or errors. Maya's closing principle: make the machine produce options; make the human approve what goes public.

**[10:05] Sponsor & Close**
SoundLayer, collaboration tool for small media teams (organizes transcripts, asset versions, and review workflows). Thanks to guest and audience.
