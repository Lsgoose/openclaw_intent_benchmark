# Episode 14 Show Notes

## 00:00:00 — The pipeline is the product
The episode opens by framing podcast production as a pipeline that shapes the listener experience end to end: raw recording, clean audio, transcript, chapter markers, social clips, title, description, and metadata.

## 00:01:20 — Five stages of production
Maya Chen breaks the workflow into five stages:
1. Capture: recording plus companion materials like guest bios, sponsor copy, links, and references.
2. Post-production: cleaning audio, trimming mistakes, deciding what stays.
3. Content extraction: transcript generation, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, and clips.
4. Review and approval: checking facts, sponsor language, names, links, and editorial/legal issues.
5. Distribution: sending the final package to hosting, newsletter, social, and archives.

## 00:02:33 — Common failure points
Small teams usually struggle with handoffs, inconsistent naming and storage, and the assumption that transcript-driven work is automatically publish-ready. A raw transcript can still contain filler, false starts, corrections, and unverified references.

## 00:04:26 — Lightweight but reliable systems
For a small team, the recommended setup is a single folder or workspace per episode, a clear naming convention, a checklist with defined handoff points, and a fixed output bundle that always includes final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail if used, and a publish log.

## 00:04:54 — Clean transcript vs. raw transcript
Keep both versions. The raw transcript provides traceability and a reference to the original wording. The cleaned transcript removes obvious disfluencies, fixes speaker labels, and becomes the preferred source for summarization and repurposing.

## 00:05:57 — Automation needs boundaries
Automation is best used for repetitive transforms: transcription cleanup, first-draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, asset packaging, and metadata prefill. Editorial judgment and exception handling still need human ownership.

## 00:06:57 — Titles are a contract
Strong titles should be generated from multiple angles: problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, and curiosity-driven. Frequency alone is not strategy; the best title reflects what the episode helps the listener do.

## 00:08:13 — Highest-value prepublish checks
The five most important checks before release are names and affiliations, links and references, sponsor compliance, sensitive edits, and metadata consistency across the title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy.

## 00:09:23 — Practical next steps for small shows
The episode closes with a concise improvement plan: define a standard folder structure and naming convention, create a publish checklist with a single owner, separate raw and cleaned transcripts, define the required output bundle, and keep a lightweight episode log to spot delays and repeated errors.

## 00:10:05 — Sponsor mention
Sponsor mention: SoundLayer, described as a collaboration tool for small media teams that helps organize transcripts, asset versions, and review workflows.

## 00:10:25 — Closing
The final takeaway is to automate drafts, not approvals: let the machine produce options, and let the human approve what becomes public.
