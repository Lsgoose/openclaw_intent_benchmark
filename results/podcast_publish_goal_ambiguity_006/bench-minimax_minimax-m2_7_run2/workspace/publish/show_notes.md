# Show Notes — Pipeline Episode

## [00:00] Introduction
Welcome to the show. Today we unpack the podcast production pipeline — how a show goes from raw recording to polished episode with clean audio, transcript, chapter markers, social clips, title, description, and metadata. Guest: Maya Chen, media production systems advisor.

## [00:47] What Is a Production Pipeline?
The product is not just the conversation — it's how that conversation gets edited, packaged, labeled, and distributed. Poor titles, sloppy transcripts, off chapter markers, and inconsistent checklists compound into a degraded listener experience.

## [01:20] Five Core Pipeline Stages
1. **Capture** — recording + companion materials (guest bios, sponsor copy, links, references)
2. **Post-production** — clean audio, trim mistakes, editorial decisions
3. **Content extraction** — transcript, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, clips
4. **Review & approval** — facts, sponsor language, names, links, editorial/legal checks
5. **Distribution** — hosting platform, newsletter, social, archives

The pipeline-as-software-build analogy: source material → transforms → validation → packaging → deployment.

## [02:33] Common Pain Points for Small Teams
- **Handoff failures** — no single source of truth when one person records, another edits, another writes the description
- **Inconsistent naming** — files like "final_v2_real_final.wav" with no clear source of record
- **Transcript overconfidence** — raw transcripts contain filler, false starts, corrected statements, unverified links; treating them as publish-ready produces low-quality or misleading derivative content

> *"A transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth."* — Maya Chen

## [04:10] Transcript Strategy
Keep a **raw transcript** for traceability. Produce a **cleaned transcript** that removes disfluencies, fixes speaker labels, and formats conversation for readability. Cleaned transcripts become the preferred source for all summarization and repurposing. Separation is essential when using automation or AI downstream.

## [04:26] Lightweight Pipeline for Small Teams (2–3 People)
- One folder/workspace per episode
- Clear naming convention
- Publish checklist with defined handoff points
- Fixed output bundle for every episode: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, publish log

## [05:49] What Can't Be Fully Automated
1. **Editorial judgment** — agents can propose titles, but without audience positioning encoded somewhere, proposals miss the mark
2. **Exception handling** — every few episodes bring guest name corrections, legal review concerns, sensitive anecdotes, or sponsor placement constraints; fully automated systems fail on edge cases

> *"Use automation to accelerate repetitive transforms. Make the machine produce options. Make the human approve what becomes public."* — Maya Chen

## [06:52] Title Strategy
Generate candidates from different angles: problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, curiosity-driven. Compare against the actual audience promise. A title is a contract with the listener.

**Example candidates for this episode's topic:**
- "How Small Teams Build a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline"
- "Why Podcast Production Breaks Even When the Recording Is Great"
- "From Raw Recording to Publish: The Systems Behind a Great Podcast"

Frequency is not strategy — the most repeated phrase in a conversation is often not the most compelling framing.

## [08:13] Pre-Publication Review Checklist (5 Priority Checks)
1. Names and affiliations — guest name, title, company spelled correctly
2. Links and references — verify actual destinations when hosts say "we'll link that"
3. Sponsor compliance — required sponsor language present where it should be
4. Sensitive edits — anything the guest wanted removed is actually removed
5. Metadata consistency — title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy all describe the same episode

## [09:13] Practical Takeaways for Small Shows
1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. Create a publish checklist that one person owns
3. Separate raw transcript and cleaned transcript
4. Define the required output bundle for every episode
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to spot delays, confusion, and repeated errors
6. Start automating drafts, not approvals
