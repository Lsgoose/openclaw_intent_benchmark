# Episode 14 Show Notes

## [00:00:00] Why the production pipeline matters
The episode opens with a framing shift: podcast production is not just operational overhead. The way an episode is edited, packaged, labeled, and distributed shapes the listener experience just as much as the conversation itself.

## [00:01:20] The five stages of a podcast production pipeline
Maya Chen breaks the workflow into five stages:
- capture
- post-production
- content extraction
- review and approval
- distribution

She compares the pipeline to a software build process: source material goes through transformation, validation, packaging, and deployment before release.

## [00:02:29] Where small teams usually break down
The first weak points are usually:
- messy handoffs between recording, editing, and publishing
- inconsistent file naming and asset storage
- overconfidence in transcript-driven content generation

The core issue is a missing single source of truth.

## [00:03:09] Why transcripts are useful but not final editorial truth
A transcript can support summaries, chapter ideas, quote pulls, and social drafts, but it still needs review. Filler, false starts, corrections, and unverified references can all create bad downstream copy.

One standout line from the conversation: the transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth.

## [00:04:26] What a lightweight reliable pipeline looks like
For a small team, Maya recommends:
- one workspace per episode
- a clear naming convention
- a checklist with defined handoff points
- a fixed output bundle

That output bundle should consistently include final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail if used, and a publish log.

## [00:04:54] Raw transcript vs. clean transcript
The team argues for keeping both versions:
- raw transcript for traceability and verification
- cleaned transcript for summarization, repurposing, and readable internal use

Separating the two reduces noise before automation or teammates build on the material.

## [00:05:49] What automation can and cannot do well
Automation is strongest at repetitive transforms such as:
- transcript cleanup
- first-draft notes
- chapter suggestions
- formatting
- asset packaging
- metadata prefill

It is weakest at editorial judgment and exception handling, especially when episodes involve sensitive edits, legal concerns, guest corrections, or sponsor constraints.

## [00:06:52] How to think about episode titles
Strong titles come from framing, not simple phrase frequency. Maya recommends generating multiple title candidates from different angles:
- problem-oriented
- guest-oriented
- outcome-oriented
- curiosity-driven

The final title should match the audience promise and reflect what the episode actually helps listeners do.

## [00:08:13] The highest-value pre-publish checks
Before release, prioritize:
- names and affiliations
- links and references
- sponsor compliance
- sensitive edits
- metadata consistency

The discussion emphasizes that the title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy should all describe the same episode.

## [00:09:13] The practical improvement plan for small shows
The episode closes with five concrete actions for improving a production system this month:
1. define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. create a publish checklist with a clear owner
3. separate raw and cleaned transcripts
4. define the required output bundle for every episode
5. keep a lightweight episode log to spot delays and repeated errors
