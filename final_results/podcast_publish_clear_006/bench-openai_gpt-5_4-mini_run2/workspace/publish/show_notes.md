# Episode 14 Show Notes

## 00:00:00 — Why the production pipeline matters
The episode opens by framing the pipeline as a core part of the listener experience: raw recording becomes a polished episode through editing, transcripts, chapter markers, social clips, titles, descriptions, and metadata.

## 00:01:20 — The five-stage pipeline
The guest breaks the workflow into five stages:
1. Capture: record the conversation and collect companion materials.
2. Post-production: clean audio and trim mistakes.
3. Content extraction: generate transcript, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, and clips.
4. Review and approval: verify facts, sponsor language, names, links, and legal or editorial risks.
5. Distribution: publish to hosting, newsletter, social channels, and archives.

## 00:02:29 — Where small teams struggle
Small teams often hit trouble at handoffs, with inconsistent naming and asset storage, and with transcript-driven work that still requires judgment.

## 00:03:09 — Transcripts are inputs, not final truth
A transcript can include filler, false starts, corrections, and unverified references. Clean transcription helps, but authoritative details still need review. A transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth.

## 00:04:15 — A lightweight but reliable system
For a small team, the guest recommends one folder per episode, a clear naming convention, a defined checklist, and a fixed output bundle. The bundle should include final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail if used, and a publish log.

## 00:04:59 — Raw transcript vs cleaned transcript
Keep both versions. The raw transcript preserves traceability and supports verification. The cleaned transcript removes obvious disfluencies, fixes speaker labels, and becomes the preferred source for summarization and repurposing.

## 00:05:49 — What automation should and should not do
Automation is useful for repetitive transforms like transcription cleanup, first-draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, asset packaging, and metadata prefill. Editorial judgment and exception handling still need human ownership.

## 00:06:57 — Titles as a contract with the listener
The guest recommends generating several title candidates from different angles: problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, and curiosity-driven. The best title reflects audience intent, not just repeated phrases from the transcript.

## 00:08:13 — Highest-value review checks
Before publishing, check names and affiliations, links and references, sponsor compliance, sensitive edits, and metadata consistency across title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy.

## 00:09:13 — Practical steps to improve this month
The episode closes with five practical improvements: standardize folder structure and naming, assign one person to own the publish checklist, separate raw and cleaned transcripts, define the required output bundle, and keep a lightweight episode log to spot delays and repeated errors.
