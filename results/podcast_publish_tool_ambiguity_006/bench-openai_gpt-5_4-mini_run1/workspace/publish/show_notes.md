## 00:00:00 — Why the pipeline matters
The episode opens by framing the podcast production pipeline as the system that shapes the listener experience: raw recording becomes a polished episode with clean audio, transcript, chapter markers, social clips, title, description, and metadata.

## 00:01:20 — The five stages of production
Maya breaks the workflow into five stages: capture, post-production, content extraction, review and approval, and distribution. She compares the pipeline to software delivery: source material, transformations, validation, packaging, and deployment.

## 00:02:29 — Common small-team failure points
The conversation turns to handoffs, inconsistent naming, and file storage. Maya warns that transcript-driven work is often underestimated and that a raw transcript is not automatically publish-ready content.

## 00:03:40 — Transcript as input, not truth
Maya says transcripts are excellent inputs for summaries, chapter suggestions, quote candidates, and social post options, but facts, links, sponsor language, names, affiliations, dates, and claims still need human review.

## 00:04:26 — A lightweight reliable workflow
For small teams, Maya recommends one folder per episode, a clear naming convention, a checklist with handoff points, and a fixed output bundle: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, and publish log.

## 00:04:59 — Raw transcript vs. cleaned transcript
Maya recommends keeping both raw and cleaned transcripts. The cleaned transcript should remove obvious disfluencies, fix speaker labels, and become the preferred source for summarization and repurposing, while the raw version remains the traceable source of record.

## 00:05:57 — Where automation helps, and where it doesn’t
The episode highlights two areas where automation is overestimated: editorial judgment and exception handling. Automation is best used for acceleration, not abdication, especially for public-facing release decisions.

## 00:06:52 — How to think about titles
Maya advises generating multiple title candidates from different angles: problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, and curiosity-driven. She says frequency is not strategy; a title should act as a contract with the listener.

## 00:08:13 — The highest-value review checks
Before publish, the team should verify names and affiliations, links and references, sponsor compliance, sensitive edits, and metadata consistency across title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy.

## 00:09:13 — Practical steps for the month ahead
Maya’s five-step recommendation: define a folder structure and naming convention, create an owned publish checklist, separate raw and cleaned transcripts, define the required output bundle, and keep a lightweight episode log to spot delays and repeated errors.

## 00:09:50 — Automate drafts, not approvals
The episode closes with a useful principle: use automation to produce options, but keep humans responsible for approving what becomes public.
