# Episode 14 Show Notes

## [00:00:00] Why the production pipeline shapes the listener experience
The episode opens with a breakdown of how a podcast moves from raw recording to a polished release. The conversation frames the production pipeline as part of the product itself, not just a back-office process.

## [00:01:20] The five stages of a reliable podcast production pipeline
Maya Chen outlines five core stages:
- capture
- post-production
- content extraction
- review and approval
- distribution

She compares the workflow to a software build pipeline: source material goes through transformations, validation, packaging, and deployment.

## [00:02:29] Where small teams usually break down first
The biggest early failure points are:
- messy handoffs between recording, editing, and publishing
- inconsistent file naming and asset storage
- overconfidence in transcript-driven outputs

A recurring theme here is the need for a single source of truth.

## [00:03:03] Why transcripts are useful but not editorial truth
The episode explains why raw transcripts should not be treated as publish-ready content. Transcripts often include filler, false starts, unverified references, and statements that were corrected later.

Key idea: the transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth.

## [00:04:15] What a lightweight but reliable pipeline looks like
For a small team, Maya recommends:
- one folder or workspace per episode
- a clear naming convention
- a checklist with defined handoff points
- a fixed output bundle for every release

That output bundle should consistently include final audio, a clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail if used, and a publish log.

## [00:04:54] Raw transcript vs. clean transcript
A practical recommendation: keep both.
- The raw transcript supports traceability and wording checks.
- The cleaned transcript should remove obvious disfluencies, improve speaker labels, and become the preferred source for summaries and repurposing.

This separation reduces downstream errors in summaries, quotes, and clips.

## [00:05:49] What automation can accelerate, and what still needs ownership
The discussion turns to agents and automation. Automation works well for repetitive transforms such as:
- transcription cleanup
- first-draft notes
- chapter suggestions
- formatting
- asset packaging
- metadata prefill

But editorial judgment and exception handling still need human ownership, especially for public-facing content.

## [00:06:52] Why titles matter so much
The episode recommends generating multiple title candidates from different angles:
- problem-oriented
- guest-oriented
- outcome-oriented
- curiosity-driven

A title is framed as a contract with the listener, not just a label. The best choice depends on audience intent, not word frequency in the transcript.

## [00:08:13] The highest-value checks before publishing
Maya highlights five review priorities before an episode goes live:
- names and affiliations
- links and references
- sponsor compliance
- sensitive edits
- metadata consistency

The goal is alignment across all surfaces: title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy.

## [00:09:13] Five practical improvements for small teams
If a small show wants better reliability this month, the recommended steps are:
1. define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. create a publish checklist with a clear owner
3. separate raw and cleaned transcripts
4. define a required output bundle for every episode
5. keep a lightweight episode log to spot delays and repeated errors
