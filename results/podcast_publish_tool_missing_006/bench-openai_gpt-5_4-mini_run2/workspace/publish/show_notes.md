# Episode 14 Show Notes

## [00:00:00] Opening: Why the pipeline shapes the listener experience
The episode opens by framing podcast production as more than editing audio. The pipeline determines how a raw recording becomes a polished episode with transcripts, chapter markers, social clips, metadata, and discoverability.

## [00:00:27] Guest intro: production systems as product
Maya Chen joins to discuss why teams often only notice the pipeline when something breaks. The core idea: the pipeline is the product.

## [00:00:59] Why discipline matters across packaging and distribution
A show’s title, transcript quality, chapter markers, and publishing checklist all affect whether people click, navigate, and trust the episode.

## [00:01:20] Five stages of a production pipeline
The conversation breaks the workflow into five stages:
- Capture: recording and gathering companion materials
- Post-production: cleaning audio and trimming mistakes
- Content extraction: transcript, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, clips
- Review and approval: checking facts, sponsor language, names, links, and legal/editorial risks
- Distribution: sending the final package to hosting, newsletter, social, and archives

## [00:02:29] Where small teams struggle
The first failure points are handoffs, inconsistent naming, and asset storage. Transcript-driven work is also harder than it looks because a raw transcript is not publish-ready copy.

## [00:03:03] Transcript as input, not truth
Raw transcripts contain filler, corrections, jokes, and unverified references. They’re useful for drafts, but not authoritative enough to publish without review.

## [00:03:40] What can be extracted safely
Transcripts are strong inputs for summaries, chapter ideas, quote candidates, and social drafts. Facts, links, sponsor language, names, affiliations, dates, and claims still need human review.

## [00:04:26] A lightweight but reliable team setup
For small teams, Maya recommends a single episode workspace, clear naming conventions, a checklist with handoff points, and a fixed output bundle including final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, and publish log.

## [00:04:54] Raw transcript vs cleaned transcript
Keeping both versions preserves traceability. The cleaned transcript becomes the preferred source for summarization and repurposing, while the raw transcript remains the reference for exact wording and original flow.

## [00:05:57] What automation gets wrong
Automation is best at repetitive transforms, but not editorial judgment or exception handling. Teams still need humans to own correctness, tone, and release quality.

## [00:06:57] Titles are contracts
Good teams generate multiple title angles: problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, and curiosity-driven. The best title matches what the episode helps the listener do.

## [00:08:13] Review checks before publish
The highest-value checks are names and affiliations, links and references, sponsor compliance, sensitive edits, and metadata consistency across title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy.

## [00:09:23] Practical takeaways for the month
The suggested improvements are simple: standardize folder structure and naming, assign one publish checklist owner, separate raw and cleaned transcripts, define the required output bundle, and keep a lightweight episode log. Consistency beats complexity.

## [00:09:50] Automate drafts, not approvals
The episode closes with a useful operating principle: let machines produce options, but keep human approval for what becomes public.
