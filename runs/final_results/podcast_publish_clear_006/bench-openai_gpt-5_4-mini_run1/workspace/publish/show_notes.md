# Episode 14 Show Notes

## 00:00 — Why the pipeline matters
The episode opens by framing the production pipeline as part of the listener experience: a raw recording becomes a polished episode with clean audio, a transcript, chapter markers, social clips, title, description, and metadata.

## 00:01:20 — The five stages
Maya Chen breaks the pipeline into five stages: capture, post-production, content extraction, review and approval, and distribution. The conversation compares the workflow to a software build pipeline.

## 00:02:29 — Where small teams struggle
Common failure points include handoffs, inconsistent naming and asset storage, and the mistaken idea that a transcript is already publish-ready content.

## 00:03:34 — Transcript as input, not truth
Transcripts are useful for summaries, chapter suggestions, quotes, and social drafts, but facts, links, sponsor language, names, dates, and claims still need human review.

## 00:04:26 — A lightweight reliable setup
For small teams, Maya recommends one folder per episode, a clear naming convention, a checklist with defined handoff points, and a fixed output bundle: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, and publish log.

## 00:04:54 — Raw vs. cleaned transcript
Keep both the raw transcript and a cleaned transcript. The cleaned version should remove obvious disfluencies, fix speaker labels, and become the preferred source for repurposing.

## 00:05:49 — Automation boundaries
Automation is best for repetitive transforms such as transcription cleanup, first-draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, asset packaging, and metadata prefill. It should not replace human ownership of correctness, tone, and release quality.

## 00:06:52 — Title strategy
Generate multiple title candidates from different angles: problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, and curiosity-driven. Frequency alone is not strategy.

## 00:08:13 — Pre-publish review
High-value checks include names and affiliations, links and references, sponsor compliance, sensitive edits, and metadata consistency across the title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy.

## 00:09:13 — Practical takeaway
The recommended first improvements are a standard folder structure, a publish checklist owned by one person, separate raw and cleaned transcripts, a required output bundle, and a lightweight episode log.
