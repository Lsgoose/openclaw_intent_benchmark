# Show Notes — Ep. 14: The Podcast Production Pipeline (with Maya Chen)

## [00:00:00] Introduction
Host introduces the episode topic: how a podcast goes from raw recording to a polished, distributed episode. Guest Maya Chen, who advises media teams and creator businesses on production systems, joins the conversation.

## [00:00:47] The Pipeline Is the Product
Maya frames the core insight: the production pipeline isn't just operational overhead — it shapes the entire listener experience. Vague titles, sloppy transcripts, misaligned chapter markers, and inconsistent checklists all compound into a degraded product.

## [00:01:20] The Five Stages of a Podcast Production Pipeline
Maya breaks down the five stages:
1. **Capture** — recording plus companion materials (guest bios, sponsor copy, links, references)
2. **Post-production** — audio cleanup, trimming, editorial decisions
3. **Content extraction** — transcripts, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, clips
4. **Review and approval** — fact-checking, sponsor language, names, links, editorial/legal review
5. **Distribution** — hosting platform, newsletter, social channels, archives

## [00:02:29] Where Small Teams Struggle First
Three common pain points:
- **Handoffs** — no single source of truth when multiple people own different stages
- **Inconsistent naming and asset storage** — "final_v2_real_final.wav" syndrome
- **Transcript-driven tasks** — raw transcripts are not publish-ready copy

## [00:03:03] Transcripts Are Rich Inputs, Not Final Truth
Maya explains why treating a raw transcript as clean structured content leads to problems: filler, false starts, uncorrected statements, and unverified links all make it into derivative content. Key quote: *"The transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth."*

Good uses for transcripts: first drafts of summaries, chapter suggestions, quote candidates, social post options, searchability, repurposing.

Still requires human review: facts, links, sponsor language, names, affiliations, dates, claims.

## [00:04:15] A Lightweight Pipeline for Small Teams
For a two- or three-person team, Maya recommends:
- One folder or workspace per episode
- A clear naming convention
- A checklist with defined handoff points
- A fixed **output bundle** for every episode: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, publish log

## [00:04:54] Raw vs. Clean Transcripts
Keep both:
- **Raw transcript** — for traceability and internal reference
- **Clean transcript** — disfluencies removed, speaker labels fixed, formatted for readability; this becomes the preferred source for summarization and repurposing

## [00:05:49] Automation: Where Teams Overestimate It
Two areas where automation falls short:
1. **Editorial judgment** — agents can propose titles but don't know audience positioning unless it's been encoded
2. **Exception handling** — guest name corrections, legal review concerns, sensitive anecdotes, sponsor placement constraints; automated systems fail on edge cases

Healthy mindset: *"Use automation to accelerate repetitive transforms… But don't give up responsibility for what goes live."*

## [00:06:52] Titles: A Contract with the Listener
Maya's approach: generate several title candidates from different angles — problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, curiosity-driven — then compare against the actual audience promise. Frequency of a phrase in the transcript is not a strategy for picking a title.

Example candidates for this episode:
- "How Small Teams Build a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline"
- "Why Podcast Production Breaks Even When the Recording Is Great"
- "From Raw Recording to Publish: The Systems Behind a Great Podcast"

## [00:08:13] The Five Highest-Value Pre-Publish Checks
1. Names and affiliations — get the guest's name, title, and company right
2. Links and references — verify actual destinations for anything mentioned
3. Sponsor compliance — confirm required language is present
4. Sensitive edits — confirm anything the guest wanted removed is actually removed
5. Metadata consistency — title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy must all describe the same episode

## [00:09:13] Practical Takeaways for Small Shows
Steps to improve your pipeline this month:
1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. Create a publish checklist that one person owns
3. Separate raw transcript and cleaned transcript
4. Define the required output bundle for every episode
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to surface delays and repeated errors
6. *(Host addition)* If experimenting with automation, start by automating drafts — not approvals
