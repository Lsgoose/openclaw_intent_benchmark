# Show Notes — Ep. 14: The Podcast Production Pipeline (with Maya Chen)

Maya Chen advises media teams and creator businesses on production systems. In this episode she walks through the full lifecycle of a podcast episode — from raw recording to published release — and explains where small teams gain the most from better process.

---

## [00:00:00] Introduction — The Pipeline Is the Product

The host frames the episode: everything that happens *after* a recording — editing, transcription, chapter markers, metadata, distribution — shapes the listener experience just as much as the conversation itself. Maya's opening observation sets the tone: most people don't notice the pipeline until something breaks.

---

## [00:01:20] The Five Stages of a Podcast Production Pipeline

Maya breaks the pipeline into five stages:

1. **Capture** — recording plus companion materials (guest bios, sponsor copy, links, references)
2. **Post-production** — audio cleanup, trimming, editorial decisions
3. **Content extraction** — transcript generation, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, clips
4. **Review and approval** — fact-checking, sponsor language, names, links, editorial and legal review
5. **Distribution** — hosting platform, newsletter, social channels, archives

The software build pipeline analogy: source material → transformations → validation → packaging → deployment.

---

## [00:02:29] Where Small Teams Struggle First

Three common pain points:

- **Handoffs without a single source of truth** — one person records, another edits, someone else writes the description, and nothing is coordinated
- **Inconsistent naming and asset storage** — files named `final_v2_real_final.wav` with no clear source of record
- **Transcript-driven tasks require more judgment than they appear** — a raw transcript is not publish-ready copy

---

## [00:03:03] Transcripts Are Rich Inputs, Not Final Truth

Raw transcripts contain filler, false starts, jokes that don't read well, statements corrected later, and unverified references ("we'll put the link in the show notes"). Treating a transcript as clean structured truth leads to low-quality or misleading derivative content.

**What transcripts are good for:** first drafts of summaries, chapter suggestions, quote candidates, social post options, searchability, repurposing.

**What still needs human review:** facts, links, sponsor language, names, affiliations, dates, and any authoritative-sounding claims.

> "The transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth." — Maya Chen

---

## [00:04:15] Lightweight Pipeline for Small Teams

For a two- or three-person team, Maya recommends:

- One folder or workspace per episode
- A clear naming convention
- A checklist with defined handoff points
- A **fixed output bundle** for every episode: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail (if used), and a publish log

**On transcripts:** keep both raw and clean versions. The raw transcript preserves traceability; the cleaned transcript (disfluencies removed, speaker labels fixed, readable formatting) becomes the preferred source for summarization and repurposing.

---

## [00:05:49] Automation: Where Teams Overestimate It

Two areas where automation is commonly over-trusted:

1. **Editorial judgment** — an agent can propose titles, but it doesn't know your audience positioning unless that's been encoded somewhere
2. **Exception handling** — guest name corrections, legal review concerns, sensitive anecdotes, sponsor placement constraints; automated systems tend to fail on edge cases, and media teams live on edge cases

The healthy mindset: **use automation to accelerate repetitive transforms** (transcription cleanup, first-draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, asset packaging, metadata prefill) — but don't give up responsibility for what goes live.

> "Make the machine produce options. Make the human approve what becomes public." — Maya Chen

---

## [00:06:52] Titles: A Contract with the Listener

Titles do a lot of work. Maya's approach: generate several candidates from different angles — problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, curiosity-driven — then compare against your actual audience promise.

Example candidates for this episode:
- *How Small Teams Build a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline*
- *Why Podcast Production Breaks Even When the Recording Is Great*
- *From Raw Recording to Publish: The Systems Behind a Great Podcast*

Key insight: **frequency is not strategy**. The most repeated phrase in a conversation is often not the most compelling framing.

Recommended workflow: three title candidates, one short description, one long description, and a final approved version logged before publish.

---

## [00:08:13] Pre-Publish Review: The Five Highest-Value Checks

1. **Names and affiliations** — guest name, title, and company
2. **Links and references** — verify every destination that was promised in the episode
3. **Sponsor compliance** — required language present in the right places
4. **Sensitive edits** — confirm anything the guest wanted removed is actually gone
5. **Metadata consistency** — title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy all describe the same episode

---

## [00:09:13] Practical Takeaways

If you run a small show and want to improve your pipeline this month:

1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. Create a publish checklist that one person owns
3. Separate raw transcript and cleaned transcript
4. Define the required output bundle for every episode
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to surface where delays and repeated errors occur

Host addition: **if you're experimenting with automation, start by automating drafts, not approvals.**
