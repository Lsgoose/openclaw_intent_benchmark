# Episode 14: Building a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline

**Guest:** Maya Chen — media production systems advisor

---

## [00:00:00] Introduction

Host introduces the episode's focus: how a show moves from raw recording to a polished, distributed episode. The argument is that the production pipeline shapes the listener experience just as much as the conversation itself.

> "The pipeline is the product." — Maya Chen

---

## [00:01:20] The Five Stages of a Podcast Production Pipeline

Maya breaks down the full pipeline into five stages:

1. **Capture** — Recording the conversation and collecting companion materials (guest bios, sponsor copy, links, references)
2. **Post-production** — Audio cleanup, trimming, and editorial decisions
3. **Content extraction** — Transcript generation, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, and clips
4. **Review and approval** — Fact-checking, sponsor language, names, links, and editorial/legal review
5. **Distribution** — Publishing to hosting platform, newsletter, social channels, and archives

The pipeline is compared to a software build system: source material → transformations → validation → packaging → deployment.

---

## [00:02:29] Where Small Teams Struggle

Three common pain points for small teams:

- **Handoffs without a single source of truth** — multiple people own different stages with no shared reference
- **Inconsistent naming and asset storage** — files like `final_v2_real_final.wav` create version confusion
- **Transcript ≠ publish-ready content** — raw transcripts contain filler, false starts, unverified links, and statements later corrected

---

## [00:03:34] What to Extract from Transcripts — and What Still Needs Human Review

Transcripts are strong inputs for:
- Episode summaries (first drafts)
- Chapter suggestions
- Quote candidates
- Social post options
- Searchability and repurposing

Human review is still required for:
- Facts, links, and references
- Sponsor language
- Names and affiliations
- Dates and claims

> "The transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth." — Maya Chen

---

## [00:04:15] Lightweight Pipeline Design for Small Teams

Recommended structure for a two- or three-person team:

- One folder or workspace per episode
- Clear naming convention
- Checklist with defined handoff points
- **Standard output bundle** for every episode:
  - Final audio
  - Clean transcript
  - Show notes
  - Title
  - Description
  - Chapter list
  - Thumbnail (if applicable)
  - Publish log

**Raw vs. clean transcript:** Keep both. The raw transcript preserves traceability; the cleaned transcript (disfluencies removed, speaker labels fixed) becomes the preferred source for summarization and repurposing.

---

## [00:05:49] Automation: What Teams Overestimate

Two areas where automation is commonly over-relied upon:

1. **Editorial judgment** — Agents can propose titles, but audience positioning must be encoded explicitly
2. **Exception handling** — Edge cases (name corrections, legal concerns, sensitive anecdotes, sponsor constraints) require human judgment

Healthy mindset: **Use automation to accelerate, not to abdicate.**

Automation works well for: transcription cleanup, first-draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, asset packaging, metadata prefill.

---

## [00:06:52] Titles: Strategy Over Frequency

Titles do significant work — they are a contract with the listener, not just a label.

Recommended approach: generate candidates from multiple angles:
- Problem-oriented: *"Why Podcast Production Breaks Even When the Recording Is Great"*
- Outcome-oriented: *"From Raw Recording to Publish: The Systems Behind a Great Podcast"*
- Audience-oriented: *"How Small Teams Build a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline"*

> "Frequency is not strategy. The most repeated phrase in a conversation is often not the most compelling framing."

Workflow note: aim for three title candidates, one short description, one long description, and a final approved version logged before publish.

---

## [00:08:13] Pre-Publish Review Checklist

Five highest-value checks before an episode goes live:

1. **Names and affiliations** — Verify guest name, title, and company
2. **Links and references** — Confirm every promised link has a verified destination
3. **Sponsor compliance** — Required sponsor language is present and correctly placed
4. **Sensitive edits** — Anything the guest requested removed is actually removed
5. **Metadata consistency** — Title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy all describe the same episode

---

## [00:09:13] Practical Takeaways

Steps to improve your pipeline this month:

1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. Create a publish checklist with a single owner
3. Separate raw transcript from cleaned transcript
4. Define the required output bundle for every episode
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to surface delays and repeated errors
6. *(Host addition)* When experimenting with automation, automate drafts — not approvals
