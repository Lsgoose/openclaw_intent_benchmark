# Episode 14 Show Notes: Building a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline

## 00:00 — Why the pipeline matters
The episode opens by framing the production pipeline as the thing that shapes the listener experience from raw recording to polished release. A reliable pipeline covers audio, transcript, chapter markers, social clips, title, description, and metadata.

## 00:01:20 — The five stages of production
The discussion breaks the workflow into five stages: capture, post-production, content extraction, review and approval, and distribution. The core idea is that podcast production is a system of transformations, validation, packaging, and deployment.

## 00:02:29 — Where small teams struggle
The episode identifies common failures: unclear handoffs, inconsistent naming and storage, and assuming transcripts are ready-made publishable content. A raw transcript can contain filler, corrections, and unverified references.

## 00:03:40 — What transcripts are for
Transcripts are useful inputs for summaries, chapter suggestions, quote candidates, and social post options, but facts, links, sponsor language, names, affiliations, dates, and claims still need human review. A transcript is a rich input, not final editorial truth.

## 00:04:26 — Building a lightweight reliable pipeline
For small teams, the recommended foundation is one folder per episode, a clear naming convention, a checklist with explicit handoff points, and a fixed output bundle: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, and publish log.

## 00:04:59 — Keep raw and cleaned transcripts separate
The episode recommends keeping both raw and cleaned transcripts. The cleaned version removes obvious disfluencies, fixes speaker labels, and becomes the preferred source for summarization and repurposing. The raw transcript remains available for traceability.

## 00:05:57 — Automation helps, but edge cases matter
Automation is useful for transcription cleanup, first-draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, asset packaging, and metadata prefill. It is not a substitute for editorial judgment or exception handling, especially when name corrections, legal concerns, sensitive anecdotes, or sponsor constraints appear.

## 00:06:57 — Titles are contracts
The episode recommends generating several title angles: problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, and curiosity-driven. A title should reflect the episode’s promise to the listener, not simply repeat the most common phrase.

## 00:08:20 — Highest-value review checks
Before release, the most important checks are names and affiliations, links and references, sponsor compliance, sensitive edits, and metadata consistency across title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy.

## 00:09:13 — Practical first steps
The closing advice is to standardize folder structure and naming, assign one owner to the publish checklist, separate raw and cleaned transcripts, define the required output bundle, and keep a lightweight episode log to spot repeated issues.
