# Show Notes

## [00:00:00] Why the production pipeline is the product
- The host and Maya Chen introduce the idea that the podcast production pipeline shapes the entire listener experience, not just the raw conversation.
- Maya outlines common failure modes: vague titles, sloppy transcripts, inaccurate chapter markers, and inconsistent publishing checklists.

## [00:01:20] The five stages of a podcast production pipeline
- Maya breaks the pipeline into five stages: capture, post-production, content extraction, review/approval, and distribution.
- The team compares this to a software build pipeline, highlighting how overreliance on memory and heroics makes systems fragile.

## [00:02:29] Common pain points for small teams
- Where small teams struggle: messy handoffs, poor naming conventions, and unclear sources of truth.
- Why transcript-driven tasks are deceptively complex, and why a raw transcript is not automatically publish-ready copy.

## [00:03:34] Transcripts as rich inputs, not final truth
- Maya explains how transcripts should drive summaries, chapter suggestions, quote candidates, and social options.
- Anything authoritative—facts, links, sponsor language, names, dates, or claims—still needs human review.
- Key idea: treat the transcript as a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth.

## [00:04:26] Designing a lightweight pipeline for a small team
- Recommendations for a simple but reliable setup: one workspace per episode, clear naming conventions, a checklist with defined handoffs, and a fixed output bundle.
- Standard outputs include final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, and a publish log.

## [00:04:54] Raw vs. cleaned transcripts
- Why it’s worth maintaining both a raw transcript and a cleaned transcript.
- The cleaned transcript removes disfluencies, fixes speaker labels, and improves readability; the raw transcript remains the traceable source.

## [00:05:49] Where automation gets overestimated
- Maya describes two common overestimates: editorial judgment and exception handling.
- Fully automated systems often fail on edge cases like name corrections, sensitive content, or sponsor constraints.

## [00:06:52] Titles as contracts with the listener
- How to generate multiple title candidates from different angles: problem-oriented, guest-oriented, and outcome-oriented.
- Why frequency in the transcript is not a strategy for titles, and how good titles reflect the true promise of the episode.

## [00:08:13] High-value review checks before publishing
- Five priority checks: names and affiliations, links and references, sponsor compliance, sensitive edits, and metadata consistency.
- The team emphasizes that title, description, transcript, chapters, and social copy should all describe the same episode.

## [00:09:13] Practical steps to improve your pipeline this month
- Maya’s five actions for small teams: standardize folder structure, create a publish checklist, separate raw vs. cleaned transcripts, define the required output bundle, and keep an episode log.
- The host adds one more: automate drafts, not approvals—let machines propose options while humans own what goes live.
