# Episode 14 — Show Notes

## Timestamped Sections

**[00:00:00] Introduction**
Welcome to the show. Today's topic: the podcast production pipeline — how a show goes from raw recording to polished episode with audio, transcript, chapter markers, social clips, metadata, and more. Guest: Maya Chen, media production systems advisor.

**[00:01:20] The Five Stages of a Podcast Pipeline**
Maya breaks the pipeline into five stages: (1) Capture — recording and collecting companion materials; (2) Post-production — audio cleanup and trimming; (3) Content extraction — transcripts, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, and clips; (4) Review and approval — fact-checks, sponsor language, names, links; (5) Distribution — to hosting platform, newsletter, social channels, and archives. Draws an analogy to a software build pipeline: source material, transformations, validation, packaging, and deployment.

**[00:02:29] Where Small Teams Struggle First**
Three common pain points: (1) Handoffs with no single source of truth; (2) Inconsistent naming — files like "final_v2_real_final.wav"; (3) Treating raw transcripts as publish-ready copy.

**[00:03:03] Transcript Quality: Rich Input, Not Final Truth**
A raw transcript contains filler, false starts, jokes that don't read well, self-corrected statements, and unverified verbal references ("we'll put the link in the show notes"). Maya: "A transcript is an excellent input for first drafts, not the final source of editorial truth."

**[00:04:26] A Lightweight Pipeline for Small Teams**
For teams of two to three: one folder per episode, clear naming convention, a checklist with defined handoff points, and a fixed output bundle. Required bundle: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, and publish log. Always keep raw and cleaned transcripts separate — the cleaned version becomes the preferred source for summarization and repurposing.

**[00:05:49] Automation: Where Teams Overestimate**
Two areas of overestimate: (1) Editorial judgment — an agent can propose titles, but can't know audience positioning unless it's been encoded; (2) Exception handling — every few episodes brings a guest name correction, legal concern, or sponsor constraint. Maya's mindset: "Use automation to accelerate repetitive transforms. Don't give up responsibility for what goes live."

**[00:06:52] Titles Do a Lot of Work**
Generate candidates from different angles: problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, curiosity-driven. Compare against actual audience promise. "A title is not just a label. It's a contract with the listener." Frequency is not strategy — the most repeated phrase is often not the most compelling framing.

**[00:08:20] Five Highest-Value Pre-Publish Checks**
(1) Names and affiliations — get guest name, title, and company right; (2) Links and references — verify actual destinations; (3) Sponsor compliance — required language present where needed; (4) Sensitive edits — guest-requested removals actually removed; (5) Metadata consistency — title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy all describe the same episode.

**[00:09:13] Practical Takeaways**
Maya's five-step plan for small shows: (1) Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention; (2) Create a publish checklist owned by one person; (3) Separate raw and cleaned transcripts; (4) Define the required output bundle for every episode; (5) Keep a lightweight episode log to spot recurring delays and errors. Host adds: "If you're experimenting with automation, start by automating drafts, not approvals." Maya: "Make the machine produce options. Make the human approve what becomes public."
