# Episode 14 — Building a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline

## Episode Summary

In this episode, host and guest Maya Chen break down what it actually takes to build a podcast production pipeline that holds up under real-world conditions. Maya advises media teams and creator businesses on production systems, and she brings a clear-eyed view of where small teams struggle and what a lightweight but reliable process looks like.

The conversation covers the five stages of a production pipeline — capture, post-production, content extraction, review and approval, and distribution — and why each stage matters for the final listener experience. Maya explains why treating a raw transcript as publish-ready content is a common and costly mistake, how to think about automation without giving up editorial responsibility, and what the highest-value review checks are before an episode goes live.

Key themes include the importance of separating raw and cleaned transcripts, defining a standard output bundle for every episode, and using automation to accelerate drafts rather than replace approvals.

## Key Takeaways

1. **The pipeline is the product.** Listeners feel production quality even when they can't name it. Vague titles, sloppy transcripts, and inconsistent metadata all erode trust.

2. **Five stages of a podcast pipeline:** Capture → Post-production → Content extraction → Review & approval → Distribution.

3. **Raw transcript ≠ publish-ready content.** Transcripts are a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth. Filler, false starts, and unverified links all need human review.

4. **Separate raw and cleaned transcripts.** Keep the raw for traceability; use the cleaned version as the source for summaries, show notes, and repurposing.

5. **Define a standard output bundle.** Every episode should end with the same core assets: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, and a publish log.

6. **Automate drafts, not approvals.** Use automation to accelerate repetitive transforms. Keep a human responsible for what goes live.

7. **Titles are a contract with the listener.** Generate candidates from multiple angles — problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented — and choose based on audience intent, not keyword frequency.

8. **Five pre-publish review checks:** Names and affiliations, links and references, sponsor compliance, sensitive edits, metadata consistency.
