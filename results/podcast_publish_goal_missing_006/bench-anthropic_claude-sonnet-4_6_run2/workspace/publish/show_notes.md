# Episode 14 – The Podcast Production Pipeline
## Show Notes

**Guest:** Maya Chen – media systems advisor for creator businesses and media teams

---

### [00:00:00] Introduction – The Pipeline Is the Product

The host frames the episode: a podcast production pipeline shapes the entire listener experience, from raw recording to polished episode with clean audio, transcript, chapter markers, social clips, title, description, and metadata.

> "The pipeline is the product." — Maya Chen

---

### [00:01:20] The Five Stages of a Podcast Production Pipeline

Maya breaks down the five core stages:

1. **Capture** – Record the conversation; collect guest bios, sponsor copy, links, and references.
2. **Post-production** – Clean audio, trim mistakes, decide what stays.
3. **Content extraction** – Generate transcript, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, and clips.
4. **Review and approval** – Check facts, sponsor language, names, links, and editorial/legal issues.
5. **Distribution** – Publish to hosting platform, newsletter, social channels, and archives.

---

### [00:02:29] Where Small Teams Struggle First

Three common pain points:

- **Handoffs** – No single source of truth when multiple people own different stages.
- **Inconsistent naming and asset storage** – Files like `final_v2_real_final.wav` create version confusion.
- **Transcript-driven tasks** – Raw transcripts are not publish-ready copy; they require editorial judgment.

---

### [00:03:03] Transcripts: Rich Input, Not Final Truth

A raw transcript can contain filler, false starts, jokes that don't read well, corrected statements, and unverified links. Treating it as clean structured truth leads to low-quality or misleading derivative content.

> "The transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth." — Maya Chen

**What transcripts are good for:** first drafts of summaries, chapter suggestions, quote candidates, social post options, searchability, and repurposing.

**What still needs human review:** facts, links, sponsor language, names, affiliations, dates, and claims.

---

### [00:04:26] Lightweight Pipeline for Small Teams

Recommended structure for a two- or three-person team:

- One folder or workspace per episode
- Clear naming convention
- Checklist with defined handoff points
- **Standard output bundle** for every episode: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail (if used), and a publish log

**On transcripts:** Keep both raw and cleaned versions. The raw transcript supports traceability; the cleaned transcript becomes the preferred source for summarization and repurposing.

---

### [00:05:49] Automation: Acceleration, Not Abdication

Where teams overestimate automation:

- **Editorial judgment** – An agent can propose titles but doesn't know audience positioning unless it's been encoded.
- **Exception handling** – Guest name corrections, legal review concerns, sensitive anecdotes, and sponsor placement constraints require human oversight.

> "Use automation to accelerate repetitive transforms… But don't give up responsibility for what goes live." — Maya Chen

Automate: transcription cleanup, first-draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, asset packaging, metadata prefill.
Keep human: correctness, tone, and release quality.

---

### [00:06:52] Titles: A Contract with the Listener

Generate several title candidates from different angles:

- Problem-oriented: *"Why Podcast Production Breaks Even When the Recording Is Great"*
- Guest-oriented or outcome-oriented framing
- Curiosity-driven: *"From Raw Recording to Publish: The Systems Behind a Great Podcast"*

> "A title is not just a label. It's a contract with the listener." — Maya Chen

Frequency of a phrase in the transcript is not a strategy. Good titles come from understanding what the episode is helping the listener do.

**Recommended workflow output before publish:** three title candidates, one short description, one long description, and a final approved version logged.

---

### [00:08:13] Pre-Publish Review Checklist

Five highest-value checks before an episode goes live:

1. **Names and affiliations** – Verify guest name, title, and company.
2. **Links and references** – Confirm every promised link resolves to the correct destination.
3. **Sponsor compliance** – Confirm required sponsor language is present.
4. **Sensitive edits** – Verify that anything the guest wanted removed is actually removed.
5. **Metadata consistency** – Title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy must all describe the same episode.

---

### [00:09:13] Practical Takeaways

Steps to improve your pipeline this month:

1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention.
2. Create a publish checklist that one person owns.
3. Separate raw transcript and cleaned transcript.
4. Define the required output bundle for every episode.
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to surface delays, confusion, and repeated errors.
6. *(Host addition)* If experimenting with automation, start by automating drafts — not approvals.
