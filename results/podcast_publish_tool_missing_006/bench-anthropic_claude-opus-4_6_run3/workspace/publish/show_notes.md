# Episode 14: Building a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline

## Show Notes

**Guest:** Maya Chen – production systems advisor for media teams and creator businesses
[Guest profile](https://creators.example.com/guests/maya-chen)

---

### [00:00:00] Introduction & Why the Pipeline Is the Product

The host introduces the topic: how a raw recording becomes a polished, discoverable episode. Maya frames the core idea — the production pipeline isn't just operational overhead, it shapes the listener experience.

### [00:01:20] The Five Stages of a Podcast Production Pipeline

Maya breaks the pipeline into five stages:
1. **Capture** – recording + companion materials (bios, links, sponsor copy)
2. **Post-production** – audio cleanup and editorial trimming
3. **Content extraction** – transcripts, summaries, show notes, chapters, clips
4. **Review & approval** – fact-checking, sponsor compliance, link verification
5. **Distribution** – publishing to hosting, newsletter, social, and archives

### [00:02:29] Where Small Teams Struggle First

Common pain points: unclear handoffs, inconsistent file naming, and treating raw transcripts as publish-ready content.

### [00:03:03] Transcripts: Rich Input, Not Final Truth

Why raw transcripts contain filler, false starts, and unverified claims. What can be safely extracted (draft summaries, chapter suggestions, quote candidates) versus what still needs human review (facts, links, names, dates).

### [00:04:15] Designing a Lightweight Pipeline for Small Teams

Practical structure: one workspace per episode, clear naming conventions, defined handoff points, and a fixed output bundle (final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapters, thumbnail, publish log).

### [00:04:54] Raw vs. Clean Transcripts

Why keeping both matters — the raw version for traceability, the cleaned version as the preferred source for downstream content generation.

### [00:05:49] Where Automation Gets Overestimated

Two common traps: editorial judgment (agents can't infer audience positioning without explicit context) and exception handling (guest corrections, legal concerns, sensitive content).

### [00:06:26] Automation for Acceleration, Not Abdication

Use automation for repetitive transforms — transcription cleanup, first-draft notes, formatting, metadata prefill. Keep humans responsible for what goes live.

### [00:06:52] The Art of Episode Titles

Generate multiple title candidates from different angles (problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, curiosity-driven). Frequency is not strategy — the best title comes from understanding what the episode helps the listener do.

### [00:08:13] The Five Highest-Value Pre-Publish Checks

1. Names and affiliations
2. Links and references
3. Sponsor compliance
4. Sensitive edits confirmed removed
5. Metadata consistency across all surfaces

### [00:09:13] Practical Takeaways

- Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention
- Create a publish checklist with clear ownership
- Separate raw and cleaned transcripts
- Define a required output bundle for every episode
- Keep a lightweight episode log to spot recurring issues
- If experimenting with automation: automate drafts, not approvals
