# Episode 14: Building a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline

## Show Notes

**[00:00:00] Introduction to podcast production pipelines**
Host introduces the topic of podcast production pipelines and how they shape the entire listener experience. Joined by guest Maya Chen, who advises media teams and creator businesses on production systems.

**[00:00:47] The pipeline is the product**
Maya explains that people often think the product is just the conversation, but in practice the way that conversation gets edited, packaged, labeled, and distributed matters a lot. Small mistakes can stack up without production discipline.

**[00:01:20] The five stages of podcast production**
Maya breaks down the pipeline into five stages:
1. **Capture** - Recording and collecting companion materials
2. **Post-production** - Audio cleanup and editing decisions
3. **Content extraction** - Transcripts, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapters, titles, clips
4. **Review and approval** - Fact checking, sponsor language, names, links
5. **Distribution** - Final package to hosting platform, newsletter, social channels, archives

**[00:02:29] Common pain points for small teams**
Three main struggles:
1. **Handoffs** - No single source of truth between team members
2. **Inconsistent naming and asset storage** - Files like "final_v2_real_final.wav"
3. **Transcript-driven tasks** - Raw transcripts aren't automatically publish-ready copy

**[00:03:34] Transcripts: rich input, not final truth**
Transcripts are excellent for first drafts of summaries, chapter suggestions, quotes, and social posts. But anything authoritative (facts, links, sponsor language, names, claims) needs human review. The transcript is "a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth."

**[00:04:26] Lightweight pipeline for small teams**
Recommendations for teams of 2-3 people:
- One folder or workspace per episode
- Clear naming conventions
- Checklist with defined handoff points
- Fixed output bundle (final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, publish log)

**[00:04:54] Raw vs. clean transcripts**
Keep both: raw transcript for traceability and internal reference, cleaned transcript for readability and as preferred source for summarization and repurposing. This separation prevents downstream systems from inheriting source messes.

**[00:05:49] Automation pitfalls**
Teams often overestimate automation in two areas:
1. **Editorial judgment** - Agents can propose titles but don't know audience positioning
2. **Exception handling** - Automated systems fail on edge cases (guest name corrections, legal concerns, sensitive anecdotes, sponsor constraints)

**[00:06:52] Title strategy**
Titles do a lot of work - they're "a contract with the listener." Generate multiple candidates from different angles (problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, curiosity-driven) and compare against audience promise. Frequency is not strategy.

**[00:08:13] High-value pre-publish checks**
Five priority checks:
1. **Names and affiliations** - Guest details correct
2. **Links and references** - Verify actual destinations
3. **Sponsor compliance** - Required language present
4. **Sensitive edits** - Requested removals actually removed
5. **Metadata consistency** - Title, description, transcript, chapters, social copy all describe same episode

**[00:09:13] Practical takeaways for improving pipelines**
1. Define standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. Create a publish checklist with clear ownership
3. Separate raw and cleaned transcripts
4. Define required output bundle for every episode
5. Keep lightweight episode log to track delays, confusion, errors

**[00:09:58] Automation philosophy**
"Make the machine produce options. Make the human approve what becomes public." Start by automating drafts, not approvals.
