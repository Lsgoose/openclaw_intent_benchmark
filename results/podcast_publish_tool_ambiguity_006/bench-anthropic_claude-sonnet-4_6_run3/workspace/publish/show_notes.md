# Episode 14 – "Building a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline"

**Guest:** Maya Chen, media production systems advisor

---

## Timestamps

### [00:00:00] Introduction
Welcome and framing: why the production pipeline shapes the entire listener experience, not just the recording itself.

### [00:00:36] The Pipeline Is the Product
Maya's core thesis: production discipline is invisible until something breaks — and then everyone realizes the pipeline *is* the product.

### [00:01:20] The Five Stages of a Podcast Production Pipeline
Maya breaks down the full workflow:
1. **Capture** — recording plus companion materials (guest bios, sponsor copy, links, references)
2. **Post-production** — audio cleanup, trimming, editorial decisions
3. **Content extraction** — transcripts, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, clips
4. **Review and approval** — fact-checking, sponsor language, names, links, editorial/legal review
5. **Distribution** — hosting platform, newsletter, social channels, archives

### [00:02:07] The Software Build Pipeline Analogy
Source material → transformations → validation → packaging → deployment. Pipelines become fragile when they depend on memory and heroics.

### [00:02:29] Where Small Teams Struggle First
Three common pain points:
- **Handoffs** — no single source of truth across roles
- **Inconsistent naming and asset storage** — "final_v2_real_final.wav" syndrome
- **Transcript-driven tasks** — raw transcripts are not publish-ready copy

### [00:03:03] Transcripts Are Rich Inputs, Not Final Truth
Why treating a raw transcript as clean structured content leads to low-quality or misleading derivative work. Filler, false starts, unverified links, and corrected statements all live in the raw file.

### [00:03:40] What to Extract vs. What Needs Human Review
- **Good for automation:** first-draft summaries, chapter suggestions, quote candidates, social post options, searchability
- **Requires human review:** facts, links, sponsor language, names, affiliations, dates, claims

### [00:04:15] Lightweight Pipeline for Small Teams (2–3 People)
Maya's recommended structure:
- One folder per episode
- Clear naming convention
- Checklist with defined handoff points
- Fixed output bundle: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, publish log

### [00:04:54] Raw Transcript vs. Clean Transcript
Keep both. Raw = traceability and internal reference. Clean = preferred source for summarization and repurposing. Separating them prevents downstream noise.

### [00:05:49] Where Teams Overestimate Automation
1. **Editorial judgment** — agents can propose titles but don't know your audience positioning unless it's encoded
2. **Exception handling** — edge cases (name corrections, legal concerns, sensitive anecdotes, sponsor constraints) break fully automated systems

### [00:06:26] Automation for Acceleration, Not Abdication
Use automation for: transcription cleanup, first-draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, asset packaging, metadata prefill. Human ownership remains essential for correctness, tone, and release quality.

### [00:06:52] Titles: A Contract with the Listener
Generate multiple candidates from different angles — problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, curiosity-driven — then match against your actual audience promise. Frequency in the transcript ≠ best framing.

### [00:08:03] Pre-Publish Review: Five High-Value Checks
1. Names and affiliations
2. Links and references (verify actual destinations)
3. Sponsor compliance
4. Sensitive edits confirmed removed
5. Metadata consistency across title, description, transcript, chapters, and social copy

### [00:09:13] Practical Takeaways for Small Shows
Steps to improve your pipeline this month:
1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. Create a publish checklist with a single owner
3. Separate raw and clean transcripts
4. Define the required output bundle for every episode
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to surface delays and repeated errors
6. *(Host addition)* Automate drafts, not approvals — make the machine produce options, make the human approve what goes public
