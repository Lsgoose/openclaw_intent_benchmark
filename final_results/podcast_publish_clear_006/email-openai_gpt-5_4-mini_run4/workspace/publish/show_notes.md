# Episode 14 Show Notes: Building a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline

## 00:00:00 — What a production pipeline really is
The episode opens by framing the production pipeline as the system that shapes the listener experience from raw recording to polished release. The conversation focuses on the steps that turn a conversation into a usable episode package.

## 00:01:20 — The five major stages
Maya Chen breaks the workflow into five stages:
1. Capture: record the conversation and collect companion materials.
2. Post-production: clean audio, trim mistakes, and decide what stays.
3. Content extraction: generate transcript, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, and clips.
4. Review and approval: check facts, sponsor language, names, links, and legal or editorial issues.
5. Distribution: publish to hosting, newsletter, social, and archives.

## 00:02:29 — Where small teams get stuck
The biggest failure points are handoffs, inconsistent file naming, and assuming transcript-driven content is automatically publish-ready. The episode stresses that unclear ownership and messy storage create avoidable mistakes.

## 00:03:09 — Why transcripts need cleanup
A transcript can contain filler, corrections, and unverified references. The episode emphasizes that transcripts are rich inputs for drafts, but not final editorial truth. Anything authoritative still needs human review.

## 00:04:26 — A lightweight reliable workflow
For small teams, Maya recommends one folder per episode, clear naming, a checklist with handoff points, and a fixed output bundle. The standard bundle includes final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail if used, and a publish log.

## 00:04:59 — Raw transcript vs. cleaned transcript
The discussion strongly recommends keeping both. The raw transcript stays as traceable source material, while the cleaned transcript becomes the preferred source for summarization and repurposing.

## 00:05:57 — What automation can and cannot do
Automation is useful for repetitive transforms like cleanup, drafts, chapter suggestions, formatting, and asset packaging. It is not a replacement for editorial judgment, exception handling, or release responsibility.

## 00:06:57 — Titles as a contract with the listener
The episode recommends generating multiple title candidates: problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, and curiosity-driven. A title should reflect the audience promise, not just the most repeated phrase in the transcript.

## 00:08:20 — Highest-value review checks
Before publish, check names and affiliations, links and references, sponsor compliance, sensitive edits, and metadata consistency across the title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy.

## 00:09:23 — Practical improvements to make this month
The episode closes with a simple improvement plan: define a standard folder structure, create a publish checklist, separate raw and cleaned transcripts, define the required output bundle, and keep a lightweight episode log to spot recurring issues.
