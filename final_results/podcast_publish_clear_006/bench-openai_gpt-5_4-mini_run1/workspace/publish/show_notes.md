[00:00:00] Introduction
The episode opens with the core idea that the podcast production pipeline shapes the listener experience, not just the recording itself. The team frames the workflow from raw conversation to polished episode assets.

[00:01:27] Major stages of the pipeline
The guest breaks the pipeline into five stages: capture, post-production, content extraction, review and approval, and distribution. The discussion emphasizes that each stage depends on the quality of the one before it.

[00:02:29] Handoffs, naming, and transcript judgment
Small teams often struggle with unclear handoffs, inconsistent file naming, and the mistaken assumption that a transcript is already publish-ready content. The transcript is described as a rich input, not final editorial truth.

[00:03:40] What transcripts are good for
Transcripts work well as inputs for summaries, chapter suggestions, quote candidates, and social post ideas. Facts, links, sponsor language, names, affiliations, dates, and claims still need human review.

[00:04:26] Lightweight but reliable workflow
For small teams, the recommended setup is a per-episode workspace, clear naming conventions, a defined checklist, and a standard output bundle. That bundle should include final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail if used, and a publish log.

[00:04:59] Raw transcript versus cleaned transcript
The guest strongly recommends keeping both raw and cleaned transcripts. The cleaned transcript removes obvious disfluencies and becomes the preferred source for summarization and repurposing, while the raw transcript remains available for traceability.

[00:05:57] Automation limits
Automation is useful for repetitive transforms like cleanup, formatting, and metadata prefill, but it should not replace editorial judgment or exception handling. The human remains responsible for what goes live.

[00:06:57] Title strategy
The episode recommends generating multiple title candidates from different angles: problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, and curiosity-driven. A title is treated as a contract with the listener, not just a label.

[00:08:20] Highest-value review checks
Before publish, the team should verify names and affiliations, links and references, sponsor compliance, sensitive edits, and metadata consistency across title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy.

[00:09:23] Practical takeaways
The closing advice is to define a standard folder structure, assign ownership for the publish checklist, separate raw and cleaned transcripts, standardize the output bundle, and keep a lightweight episode log. Automation should draft, not approve.
