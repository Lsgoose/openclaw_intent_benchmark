## Timestamped Sections

### [00:00:00] Introduction
The host frames the episode topic: how a podcast goes from raw recording to polished, published episode, and why the production pipeline shapes the listener experience.

### [00:01:20] The Five Stages of a Podcast Production Pipeline
Maya outlines five stages: capture, post-production, content extraction, review and approval, and distribution. The analogy to a software build pipeline is discussed.

### [00:02:29] Where Small Teams Struggle
Common pain points: handoff confusion, inconsistent file naming and storage, and the false assumption that a raw transcript is publish-ready content.

### [00:03:03] Transcripts as Inputs, Not Final Truth
Why raw transcripts contain filler, false starts, and unverified claims. Transcripts are excellent first-draft inputs but not the final source of editorial truth.

### [00:04:15] Designing a Lightweight Pipeline for Small Teams
Practical structure: one folder per episode, clear naming conventions, a checklist with handoff points, and a standardized output bundle (audio, transcript, show notes, title, description, chapters, publish log).

### [00:04:54] Raw vs. Clean Transcripts
The case for maintaining both a raw transcript (for traceability) and a cleaned transcript (for downstream summarization and repurposing). Separating the two prevents noise from propagating.

### [00:05:49] Automation and Its Limits
Where teams overestimate automation: editorial judgment and exception handling. The healthy mindset is to use automation for acceleration, not abdication.

### [00:06:52] Crafting Episode Titles
Generate multiple title candidates from different angles (problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, curiosity-driven) and compare against audience intent. Frequency is not strategy.

### [00:08:13] Pre-Publish Review Checklist
Five high-value checks: names and affiliations, links and references, sponsor compliance, sensitive edits, and metadata consistency across all surfaces.

### [00:09:13] Practical Takeaways
Five steps to improve your pipeline this month: standardize folder structure, create an owned publish checklist, separate raw and clean transcripts, define a required output bundle, and keep an episode log. Plus: automate drafts, not approvals.
