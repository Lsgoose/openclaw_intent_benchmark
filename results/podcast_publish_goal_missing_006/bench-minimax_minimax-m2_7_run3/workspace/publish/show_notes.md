# Show Notes — Episode 14: The Podcast Production Pipeline

**Guest:** Maya Chen | **Episode:** Pipeline Fundamentals

---

## [00:00] Introduction

Host welcomes Maya Chen, media production systems advisor. Frames the episode around how a show goes from raw recording to polished, published episode.

---

## [00:01:20] The Five Major Pipeline Stages

Maya breaks production into five stages:
1. **Capture** — recording + companion materials (guest bios, sponsor copy, links, references)
2. **Post-production** — clean audio, trim mistakes, editorial decisions
3. **Content extraction** — transcript, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, clips
4. **Review and approval** — fact-check, sponsor language, names, links, editorial/legal review
5. **Distribution** — hosting platform, newsletter, social channels, archives

Analogous to a software build pipeline: source material → transformations → validation → packaging → deployment.

---

## [00:02:29] Where Small Teams Struggle First

Three most common pain points:
- **Broken handoffs** — one person records, another edits, another writes the description, no single source of truth
- **Inconsistent naming** — files like `final_v2_real_final.wav` create confusion about the source of record
- **Transcript-driven tasks** — raw transcripts are not publish-ready; require judgment to extract clean, authoritative content

---

## [00:03:03] Transcript as Input, Not Editorial Truth

A raw transcript contains filler, false starts, jokes that don't read well, self-corrected statements, and unverified links. Treating it as structured truth produces low-quality or misleading derivative content.

**Key quote:** *"The transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth."*

Recommended workflow: keep a raw transcript for traceability; produce a cleaned transcript for summarization and repurposing.

---

## [00:04:26] A Lightweight but Reliable Pipeline

For a team of two to three:
- One folder per episode with a clear naming convention
- A defined checklist with clear handoff points
- A **fixed output bundle**: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, and publish log
- Separate raw and cleaned transcripts at the source level

Standardized outputs make everything downstream easier.

---

## [00:05:49] Automation: Acceleration vs. Abdication

Where small teams and automation experimenters overestimate:
1. **Editorial judgment** — agents can propose titles, but can't know your audience positioning without encoded context
2. **Exception handling** — every few episodes bring guest name corrections, legal concerns, sensitive anecdotes, sponsor constraints; automated systems fail on edge cases

Healthy mindset: use automation to accelerate repetitive transforms (transcription cleanup, first-draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, asset packaging). Make the machine produce options; make the human approve what goes live.

---

## [00:06:52] Title Strategy

Titles do real work — not just labels but a contract with the listener. Generate candidates from different angles:
- Problem-oriented
- Guest-oriented
- Outcome-oriented
- Curiosity-driven

Compare each against the actual audience promise. Frequency is not strategy; the most repeated phrase is often not the most compelling framing.

Pre-publish workflow: three title candidates, one short description, one long description, final approved version logged.

---

## [00:08:13] Pre-Release Review Checklist

Five highest-value checks before an episode goes live:
1. **Names and affiliations** — guest name, title, company correct
2. **Links and references** — if the host says "we'll link that," verify the actual destination
3. **Sponsor compliance** — required sponsor language present and positioned correctly
4. **Sensitive edits** — anything the guest asked removed is actually removed
5. **Metadata consistency** — title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy all describe the same episode

Metadata consistency is often underrated; misalignment between title and summary makes the production feel unreliable.

---

## [00:09:13] Practical Takeaways

What a small show should do first this month:
1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. Create a publish checklist owned by one person
3. Separate raw transcript and cleaned transcript
4. Define the required output bundle for every episode
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to surface delays, confusion, and repeated errors

*"You don't need an elaborate system to get meaningful gains. You need consistency."*
