# Show Notes

## 00:00 — Opening: the pipeline is the product
The episode opens with the idea that podcast production is more than recording. The real listener experience is shaped by the full pipeline: audio cleanup, transcript quality, chapter markers, social clips, titles, descriptions, and metadata.

## 00:01 — The five stages of a podcast pipeline
Maya Chen breaks the pipeline into five parts:
- capture: recording the conversation and collecting companion materials
- post-production: cleaning audio and trimming mistakes
- content extraction: transcript, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, and clips
- review and approval: checking facts, sponsor language, names, links, and legal or editorial risks
- distribution: sending the final package to hosting, newsletter, social, and archives

## 00:02 — Handoffs, naming, and source-of-truth problems
Small teams often struggle with handoffs, inconsistent file naming, and unclear source materials. Maya notes that a transcript is not automatically publish-ready, and that sloppy process creates downstream errors.

## 00:03 — Transcripts as inputs, not final truth
The transcript is useful for drafts of summaries, chapter ideas, quote pulls, and social options, but it still needs human review for facts, links, sponsor language, names, affiliations, dates, and claims. A key line from the episode: the transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth.

## 00:04 — Lightweight pipeline for small teams
For a small team, the recommendation is to keep one folder per episode, use a clear naming convention, define a checklist with handoff points, and standardize the output bundle. The bundle should include final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail if used, and a publish log.

## 00:05 — Raw transcript vs clean transcript
Maya recommends keeping both raw and cleaned transcripts. The raw version preserves traceability and wording checks, while the cleaned version removes disfluencies, fixes speaker labels, and becomes the preferred source for repurposing.

## 00:06 — Automation should accelerate, not replace judgment
Automation works well for transcription cleanup, first-draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, asset packaging, and metadata prefill. It should not replace responsibility for correctness, tone, or release quality, especially when exceptions appear.

## 00:07 — Titles are a contract with the listener
The discussion covers title strategy: generate multiple angles, including problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, and curiosity-driven candidates. The best title is not the most repeated phrase; it is the one that matches listener intent and the episode promise.

## 00:08 — Review checks before publish
The highest-value checks are names and affiliations, links and references, sponsor compliance, sensitive edits, and metadata consistency. The title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy should all describe the same episode.

## 00:09 — Practical takeaways for small shows
The final advice is to define a folder structure and naming convention, create a publish checklist owned by one person, separate raw and cleaned transcripts, define the required output bundle, and keep a lightweight episode log to spot delays and recurring errors.

## 00:10 — Closing: automate drafts, not approvals
The episode closes with a simple rule for teams using automation: let the machine generate options, but keep a human responsible for what becomes public.
