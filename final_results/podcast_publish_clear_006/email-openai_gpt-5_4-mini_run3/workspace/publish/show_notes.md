# Episode 14 Show Notes

## [00:00:00] Introduction: the pipeline is the product
The episode opens by framing podcast production as more than recording and editing. The production pipeline shapes the listener experience because it determines how raw audio becomes a polished, discoverable episode.

## [00:01:20] The five stages of a reliable pipeline
Maya Chen outlines five stages:
1. Capture — recording the conversation and collecting companion materials.
2. Post-production — cleaning audio, trimming mistakes, and deciding what stays.
3. Content extraction — transcript generation, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, and clips.
4. Review and approval — checking facts, sponsor language, names, links, and legal or editorial issues.
5. Distribution — publishing to the hosting platform, newsletter, social channels, and archives.

## [00:02:29] Where small teams struggle
The first breakdowns usually come from handoffs, inconsistent naming, and unstable asset storage. Transcript-driven work also needs judgment, because a raw transcript is not automatically publish-ready content.

## [00:03:34] Transcript as input, not source of truth
Transcripts are useful for first drafts of summaries, chapter ideas, quote candidates, and social copy. But facts, links, sponsor language, names, affiliations, dates, and claims still need human review.

## [00:04:26] A lightweight output bundle
For small teams, reliability comes from a single folder per episode, clear naming, a defined checklist, and a fixed output bundle: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail if used, and a publish log.

## [00:04:59] Clean transcript versus raw transcript
Keep both. The raw transcript preserves traceability and the original flow. The cleaned transcript removes obvious disfluencies, fixes labels, and becomes the preferred source for summarization and repurposing.

## [00:05:57] What automation can and cannot do
Automation is strongest at repetitive transforms such as cleanup, first drafts, chapter suggestions, formatting, packaging, and metadata prefill. It is weakest at editorial judgment and exception handling.

## [00:06:52] Titles as a contract with the listener
Maya recommends generating multiple title candidates from different angles: problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, and curiosity-driven. The best title reflects the audience promise, not just repeated phrasing in the transcript.

## [00:08:13] The highest-value review checks
Before publishing, prioritize five checks: names and affiliations, links and references, sponsor compliance, sensitive edits, and metadata consistency across title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy.

## [00:09:13] Practical first steps for this month
The recommended improvements are: define folder structure and naming conventions, create an owned publish checklist, separate raw and cleaned transcripts, define the output bundle, and keep a lightweight episode log.
