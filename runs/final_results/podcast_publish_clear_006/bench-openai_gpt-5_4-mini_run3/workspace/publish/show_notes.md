# Episode 14 Show Notes: Building a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline

[00:00:00] Opening: the production pipeline shapes the listener experience from raw recording to polished release.
- The episode frames the pipeline as the product, not just the conversation.
- Goal: make the path from recording to distribution reliable, repeatable, and reviewable.

[00:01:20] The five major stages of a podcast pipeline.
- Capture: record the conversation and gather guest bios, sponsor copy, links, and references.
- Post-production: clean audio, trim mistakes, and decide what stays.
- Content extraction: create transcript, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, and clips.
- Review and approval: check facts, sponsor language, names, links, and editorial/legal issues.
- Distribution: send the final package to hosting, newsletter, social channels, and archives.

[00:02:29] Where small teams struggle first.
- Handoffs break down when no one owns the source of truth.
- File naming and asset storage become chaotic without conventions.
- Transcript-driven tasks still need judgment; raw transcript is not publish-ready copy.

[00:03:34] What transcripts are good for, and what still needs review.
- Great inputs for first drafts of summaries, chapter suggestions, quote candidates, and social options.
- Still needs human review for facts, links, sponsor language, names, affiliations, dates, and claims.
- Key idea: a transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth.

[00:04:26] What a lightweight but reliable pipeline looks like.
- Use one folder or workspace per episode.
- Keep a clear naming convention and a checklist with defined handoff points.
- Standardize an output bundle: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail if used, and publish log.

[00:04:54] Raw transcript versus cleaned transcript.
- Keep both for traceability.
- Cleaned transcript becomes the preferred source for summarization and repurposing.
- Raw transcript stays useful for wording checks and reconstructing the original flow.

[00:05:49] Where automation is overestimated.
- Editorial judgment cannot be fully automated without audience context.
- Exception handling is where systems break: name corrections, legal issues, sensitive anecdotes, sponsor constraints.
- Use automation for repetitive transforms, not for abdication of responsibility.

[00:06:57] Titles as a strategic contract with the listener.
- Generate multiple angles: problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, curiosity-driven.
- Choose based on audience intent, not just repeated phrases.
- Frequency is not strategy.

[00:08:13] Highest-value review checks before publishing.
- Names and affiliations.
- Links and references.
- Sponsor compliance.
- Sensitive edits.
- Metadata consistency across title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy.

[00:09:13] Practical takeaways for improving a pipeline this month.
- Define episode folder structure and naming conventions.
- Create a publish checklist with clear ownership.
- Separate raw and cleaned transcripts.
- Define the required output bundle.
- Maintain a lightweight episode log to spot delays and repeated errors.
- Automate drafts first; keep human approval for what becomes public.
