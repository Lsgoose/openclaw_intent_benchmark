## Core editorial takeaways
- The production pipeline shapes the listener experience, not just the recording itself.
- Small teams benefit from treating the workflow like a build pipeline: source material, transforms, validation, packaging, and deployment.
- Transcripts are useful as rich inputs for summaries, titles, chapters, and repurposing, but they are not final editorial truth.
- Reliable production comes from standardized outputs, explicit handoffs, verified links, and consistent review.
- Automation works best for draft generation and repetitive transforms, while humans remain accountable for public release quality.

## Short description
How small podcast teams can build a dependable production workflow, from clean transcripts and verified links to final packaging, review, and release.

## Long description
What turns a strong recording into a reliable episode release? In this conversation, Maya Chen breaks down the full podcast production pipeline: capture, post-production, content extraction, review, and distribution.

The episode covers where small teams usually break down first, why clean transcripts should be separated from raw transcripts, how standardized output bundles reduce errors, and why metadata consistency matters across every surface listeners see. It also explores where automation helps most in podcast operations and where human review still matters.

If you run a podcast with a lean team, this episode offers a practical framework for creating a repeatable, lower-friction production process that improves both quality and consistency.
