## 00:00:00 — Opening: why the pipeline matters
The episode opens by framing podcast production as more than a set of chores. The pipeline shapes the listener experience by turning raw recording into a polished episode with clean audio, transcript, chapters, social clips, title, description, and metadata.

## 00:00:27 — Introducing Maya Chen
The host welcomes Maya Chen, who advises media teams and creator businesses on production systems.

## 00:00:36 — The pipeline is the product
Maya says the pipeline becomes visible when something breaks, because the workflow itself is part of the product.

## 00:00:47 — Packaging, labeling, and distribution
The conversation shifts to how editing, packaging, labeling, and distribution affect whether people actually discover and trust the show.

## 00:01:20 — Major stages of a podcast production pipeline
Maya breaks the pipeline into five stages: capture, post-production, content extraction, review and approval, and distribution.

## 00:02:13 — Why the software analogy works
The host compares the process to a software build pipeline. Maya agrees: source material, transformations, validation, packaging, and deployment all fit.

## 00:02:33 — Where small teams struggle
The first pain point is handoffs. The second is inconsistent naming and storage. The third is assuming transcript-driven work is automatically ready to publish.

## 00:03:09 — A transcript is not the final truth
Maya explains that transcripts contain filler, false starts, corrections, and unverified references. Treating them as final truth leads to low-quality or misleading derivative content.

## 00:03:40 — What transcripts are good for
Transcripts are strong inputs for summaries, chapter ideas, quotes, social options, searchability, and repurposing, but facts, links, sponsor language, names, affiliations, dates, and claims still need human review.

## 00:04:15 — Small-team output bundle
For a lightweight but reliable workflow, Maya recommends one episode workspace, clear naming, a defined checklist, and a standard output bundle: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, and publish log.

## 00:04:59 — Keep raw and cleaned transcripts separate
Maya strongly recommends keeping both raw and cleaned transcripts. The cleaned transcript becomes the preferred source for summarization and repurposing, while the raw version remains useful for traceability.

## 00:05:28 — Why separation matters for automation
Without separation, downstream tools inherit the mess from the source, which creates awkward summaries, broken quote cards, and misleading snippets.

## 00:05:57 — What automation cannot own
Maya says teams often overestimate automation in editorial judgment and exception handling. Most episodes are routine, but edge cases require human oversight.

## 00:06:26 — Automation should accelerate, not replace judgment
The healthy mindset is to use automation for repetitive transforms like transcript cleanup, first-draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, asset packaging, and metadata prefill, while humans retain responsibility for correctness and tone.

## 00:06:57 — Titles as a contract
Maya argues that titles do a lot of work and should be generated from multiple angles: problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, and curiosity-driven.

## 00:07:19 — Example title approaches
She gives examples of different title framings for the same episode about production workflows, showing how audience intent changes the best choice.

## 00:07:50 — Frequency is not strategy
The most repeated phrase in a conversation is not necessarily the most compelling title. Good titles come from understanding what the episode helps the listener do.

## 00:08:13 — Highest-value review checks
Before publication, Maya prioritizes five checks: names and affiliations, links and references, sponsor compliance, sensitive edits, and metadata consistency.

## 00:09:02 — Consistency across every surface
The title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy should all describe the same episode. When they drift, the production feels unreliable.

## 00:09:23 — Practical improvements for small shows
Maya recommends defining folder structure and naming, assigning one owner to the publish checklist, separating raw and cleaned transcripts, standardizing the output bundle, and keeping a lightweight episode log.

## 00:09:58 — Automate drafts, not approvals
The host adds a key takeaway: let the machine produce options, but make the human approve what becomes public.
