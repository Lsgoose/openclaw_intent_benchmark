# Episode 14 Show Notes

## 00:00 — Why the production pipeline shapes the listener experience
The episode opens with a practical definition of the podcast production pipeline: the full path from raw recording to a polished release, including audio cleanup, transcripts, chapter markers, social clips, metadata, titles, and descriptions. The central idea is that the pipeline is not just back-office process—it directly affects how the audience experiences the show.

## 00:01 — The five stages of a reliable podcast production pipeline
Maya Chen breaks the workflow into five stages:
- Capture
- Post-production
- Content extraction
- Review and approval
- Distribution

This framing treats the episode workflow like a media build pipeline, where source material is transformed, validated, packaged, and published.

## 00:02 — Where small teams usually struggle first
The conversation focuses on common failure points for lean teams:
- weak handoffs between people
- no single source of truth
- inconsistent naming and file storage
- overconfidence in transcript-driven publishing

A key warning here: a transcript may be useful input, but it is not automatically publish-ready copy.

## 00:03 — What transcripts are good for, and what still needs human review
The cleaned transcript is presented as a strong source for:
- episode summary drafts
- chapter suggestions
- quote candidates
- social post options

But anything factual or authoritative still needs review, especially:
- names
- affiliations
- dates
- claims
- sponsor language
- links

Quote pull from the episode: “The transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth.”

## 00:04 — A lightweight pipeline for a two- or three-person team
For small teams, Maya recommends:
- one workspace or folder per episode
- a clear naming convention
- a checklist with explicit handoffs
- a fixed output bundle

That output bundle should consistently include final audio, a clean transcript, show notes, a title, a description, chapter markers, artwork if needed, and a publish log.

## 00:04 — Raw transcript vs. cleaned transcript
The discussion then narrows to transcript hygiene. The recommendation is to keep both versions:
- a raw transcript for traceability and internal verification
- a cleaned transcript for summarization and repurposing

This separation reduces downstream noise and improves the quality of anything built from the transcript later.

## 00:05 — What automation can accelerate, and where it still breaks down
The episode turns to automation and agent-assisted workflows. The takeaway is that automation is valuable for repetitive transformations such as:
- transcription cleanup
- first-draft notes
- chapter suggestions
- formatting
- metadata prefilling

But teams should not automate away responsibility. Editorial judgment, audience positioning, and exception handling still require human ownership.

## 00:06 — Titles as audience contracts, not just labels
Maya explains why titles deserve deliberate iteration. Instead of mechanically choosing the most repeated phrase from a transcript, teams should generate multiple framing options, such as:
- problem-oriented
- guest-oriented
- outcome-oriented
- curiosity-driven

The larger point: a title should match what the episode actually helps the listener do.

## 00:08 — The highest-value review checks before publish
The final review checklist emphasizes five checks:
- names and affiliations
- links and references
- sponsor compliance
- sensitive edits
- metadata consistency

This section reinforces that listeners notice when the title, description, transcript, and social copy do not align.

## 00:09 — A practical upgrade plan for small podcast teams
To improve a production workflow this month, the recommended starting points are:
1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention.
2. Create a publish checklist with one clear owner.
3. Separate raw and cleaned transcripts.
4. Define the required output bundle for every episode.
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to spot delays and repeated mistakes.
