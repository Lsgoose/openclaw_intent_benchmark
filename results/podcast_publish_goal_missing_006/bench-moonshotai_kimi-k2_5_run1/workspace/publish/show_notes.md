# Episode 14: The Podcast Production Pipeline

## Show Notes

---

### [00:00:00] Introduction: What Is a Podcast Production Pipeline?

The episode opens with an overview of how a podcast goes from raw recording to polished episode—covering clean audio, transcripts, chapter markers, social clips, titles, descriptions, and metadata that helps listeners find the show.

**Guest:** Maya Chen, who advises media teams and creator businesses on production systems.

---

### [00:01:27] The Five Stages of Production

Maya breaks down the pipeline into five distinct stages:

1. **Capture** – Recording the conversation plus companion materials (guest bios, sponsor copy, links, references)
2. **Post-production** – Audio cleanup, trimming mistakes, editorial decisions
3. **Content extraction** – Transcript generation, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, clips
4. **Review and approval** – Fact-checking, sponsor language verification, names, links, legal review
5. **Distribution** – Publishing to hosting platform, newsletter, social channels, archives

---

### [00:02:33] Common Pain Points for Small Teams

Three areas where small teams typically struggle:

- **Handoffs** – No single source of truth when multiple people touch the episode
- **Inconsistent naming** – Files like "final_v2_real_final.wav" create version confusion
- **Transcript assumptions** – Raw transcripts are not automatically publish-ready copy

---

### [00:03:40] What to Extract from Transcripts vs. What Needs Human Review

**Good for automation:** First drafts of summaries, chapter suggestions, quote candidates, social post options, searchability

**Requires human review:** Facts, links, sponsor language, names, affiliations, dates, claims

Key principle: *"The transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth."*

---

### [00:04:26] Building a Lightweight Pipeline (Small Teams)

Maya's recommendations for teams of 2–3 people:

- One folder/workspace per episode
- Clear naming conventions
- Checklist with defined handoff points
- Fixed output bundle for every episode: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, publish log

---

### [00:04:54] Clean vs. Raw Transcripts

**Raw transcript:** Kept for traceability and internal reference
**Clean transcript:** Removes disfluencies, fixes speaker labels, formats for readability—this becomes the preferred source for summarization and repurposing

---

### [00:05:57] Automation: Where Teams Overestimate

Two common overestimations:

1. **Editorial judgment** – Agents can propose titles but don't know your audience positioning
2. **Exception handling** – Automated systems fail on edge cases (name corrections, legal reviews, sensitive edits, sponsor constraints)

Mindset: Use automation for acceleration, not abdication.

---

### [00:06:52] Crafting Episode Titles

Generate multiple candidates from different angles:

- Problem-oriented
- Guest-oriented  
- Outcome-oriented
- Curiosity-driven

Compare against your audience promise. A title is a contract with the listener—not just a label.

Example variations for this episode:
- "How Small Teams Build a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline"
- "Why Podcast Production Breaks Even When the Recording Is Great"
- "From Raw Recording to Publish: The Systems Behind a Great Podcast"

---

### [00:08:20] The Five Highest-Value Pre-Release Checks

1. Names and affiliations (guest name, title, company)
2. Links and references (verify every "we'll link that" actually has a destination)
3. Sponsor compliance (required language present where it should be)
4. Sensitive edits (confirm requested removals are actually removed)
5. Metadata consistency (title, description, transcript, chapters, social copy all describe the same episode)

---

### [00:09:23] Practical Takeaways: Improve Your Pipeline This Month

Five steps for small shows:

1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. Create a publish checklist that one person owns
3. Separate raw transcript from cleaned transcript
4. Define the required output bundle for every episode
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to track delays and repeated errors

Bonus: Start automation by automating drafts, not approvals. Make the machine produce options. Make the human approve what becomes public.
