# Episode 14: Podcast Production Pipeline

## Timestamps

**[00:00:00]** Introduction to podcast production pipelines and why they shape the entire listener experience.

**[00:00:27]** Guest introduction: Maya Chen, media systems advisor for creator businesses.

**[00:00:36]** Maya's opening insight: "The pipeline is the product" - people don't notice it until something breaks.

**[00:00:47]** Discussion on how the conversation gets packaged, labeled, and distributed matters as much as the content itself.

**[00:01:20]** The five major stages of podcast production:
1. Capture (recording + companion materials)
2. Post-production (audio cleanup, editing)
3. Content extraction (transcripts, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapters, titles, clips)
4. Review and approval (fact-checking, sponsor compliance, sensitive edits)
5. Distribution (hosting platform, newsletter, social channels, archives)

**[00:02:13]** Analogy to software build pipelines: source material, transformations, validation, packaging, deployment.

**[00:02:29]** Three common pain points for small teams:
1. Handoffs between team members without single source of truth
2. Inconsistent naming and asset storage
3. Transcript-driven tasks requiring more judgment than expected

**[00:03:03]** The transcript fallacy: raw transcripts contain filler, false starts, unverified references, and shouldn't be treated as clean structured truth.

**[00:03:40]** What to extract from transcripts vs. what needs human review:
- Good for: first drafts of summaries, chapter suggestions, quote candidates, social post options
- Needs review: facts, links, sponsor language, names, affiliations, dates, claims

**[00:04:10]** Producer note: "rich input, not final truth" identified as key quote.

**[00:04:26]** Lightweight pipeline for small teams (2-3 people):
- One folder/workspace per episode
- Clear naming conventions
- Checklist with defined handoff points
- Fixed output bundle (final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, publish log)

**[00:04:54]** Transcript strategy: maintain both raw (for traceability) and cleaned (for readability and repurposing) versions.

**[00:05:28]** Importance of reducing noise before asking tools or teammates to build on results.

**[00:05:49]** Where teams overestimate automation:
1. Editorial judgment (titles, positioning)
2. Exception handling (guest corrections, legal concerns, sensitive content)

**[00:06:31]** Healthy mindset: "Use automation for acceleration, not abdication."

**[00:06:52]** Title strategy: generate multiple candidates from different angles (problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, curiosity-driven).

**[00:07:17]** Example title variations for production workflow episode.

**[00:07:44]** Key insight: "Frequency is not strategy" - most repeated phrases aren't necessarily the best framing.

**[00:08:03]** Workflow recommendation: three title candidates, short description, long description, final approved version logged before publish.

**[00:08:20]** Five high-value pre-publish checks:
1. Names and affiliations
2. Links and references
3. Sponsor compliance
4. Sensitive edits
5. Metadata consistency (title, description, transcript, chapters, social copy alignment)

**[00:09:02]** Importance of metadata consistency across all surfaces.

**[00:09:13]** Practical takeaways for small shows:
1. Define standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. Create publish checklist with single owner
3. Separate raw and cleaned transcripts
4. Define required output bundle for every episode
5. Keep lightweight episode log to track delays, confusion, errors

**[00:09:50]** Final automation advice: "Start by automating drafts, not approvals."

**[00:10:05]** Sponsor mention: SoundLayer collaboration tool for small media teams.

**[00:10:20]** Closing remarks and thanks.
