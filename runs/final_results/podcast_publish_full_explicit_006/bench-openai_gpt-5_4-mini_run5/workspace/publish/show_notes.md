# Episode 14 Show Notes

[00:00] Opening: Why the production pipeline matters
The episode frames the pipeline as the thing that shapes the listener experience from raw recording through polished release.

[00:01:20] Major stages of a podcast pipeline
Maya Chen breaks the process into capture, post-production, content extraction, review and approval, and distribution.

[00:02:29] Common failure points for small teams
The discussion covers handoff gaps, inconsistent file naming, and the trap of treating a transcript as publish-ready content.

[00:03:34] What transcripts are good for
Transcripts are strong inputs for summaries, chapter suggestions, quote candidates, and social posts, but facts, names, links, sponsor language, and claims still need human review.

[00:04:26] Building a lightweight reliable workflow
The guest recommends one workspace per episode, clear naming, a defined checklist, and a fixed output bundle that includes final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, and publish log.

[00:04:59] Raw transcript vs. cleaned transcript
Keep both. The raw transcript preserves traceability; the cleaned transcript becomes the preferred source for repurposing and summarization.

[00:05:49] Where automation helps and where it breaks
Automation is useful for repetitive transforms, but editorial judgment and exception handling still need a human owner.

[00:06:57] Titles are contracts with listeners
The episode recommends generating several title angles: problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, and curiosity-driven, then choosing the one that best matches audience intent.

[00:08:13] Highest-value pre-publish checks
Before launch, verify names and affiliations, links and references, sponsor compliance, sensitive edits, and metadata consistency across title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy.

[00:09:13] Practical takeaways for small shows
Define folder structure and naming conventions, assign one publish checklist owner, separate raw and cleaned transcripts, standardize the output bundle, and keep a lightweight episode log.

[00:10:05] Sponsor and close
The episode closes with sponsor thanks, a guest sign-off, and a reminder to share the episode with teammates who handle editing, publishing, or content operations.
