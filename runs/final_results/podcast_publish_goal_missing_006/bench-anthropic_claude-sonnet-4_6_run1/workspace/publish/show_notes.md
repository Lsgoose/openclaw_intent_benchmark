# Episode 14 – Show Notes
## The Podcast Production Pipeline

*Guest: Maya Chen — media systems advisor for creator businesses and media teams*

---

### [00:00:00] Introduction — The Pipeline Is the Product

The host frames the episode: a podcast production pipeline shapes the entire listener experience, from raw recording to polished episode with clean audio, transcript, chapter markers, social clips, title, description, and metadata.

> "The pipeline is the product." — Maya Chen

---

### [00:01:20] The Five Stages of a Podcast Production Pipeline

Maya breaks down the full pipeline:

1. **Capture** — Record the conversation; collect guest bios, sponsor copy, links, and references.
2. **Post-production** — Clean audio, trim mistakes, decide what stays.
3. **Content extraction** — Transcript generation, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, and clips.
4. **Review and approval** — Fact-check names, links, sponsor language, and anything with editorial or legal risk.
5. **Distribution** — Publish to hosting platform, newsletter, social channels, and archives.

---

### [00:02:29] Where Small Teams Break Down

Three common pain points:

- **Handoffs without a single source of truth** — recording, editing, and writing owned by different people with no shared system.
- **Inconsistent naming and asset storage** — files like `final_v2_real_final.wav` erode confidence in which version is canonical.
- **Treating the raw transcript as publish-ready copy** — transcripts contain filler, false starts, unverified links, and statements later corrected.

---

### [00:03:34] What to Extract from a Transcript — and What Still Needs Human Review

Transcripts are strong inputs for:
- Episode summaries (first drafts)
- Chapter suggestions
- Quote candidates
- Social post options
- Search and repurposing

Anything authoritative still requires human review: facts, links, sponsor language, names, affiliations, dates, and claims.

> "The transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth." — Maya Chen

---

### [00:04:15] Building a Lightweight, Reliable Pipeline for Small Teams

Recommended structure for a two- or three-person team:

- One folder or workspace per episode
- A clear naming convention
- A checklist with defined handoff points
- A **fixed output bundle** for every episode: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail (if used), and a publish log

**On transcripts:** Keep both raw and cleaned versions. The raw transcript preserves traceability; the cleaned transcript (disfluencies removed, speaker labels fixed, readable formatting) becomes the preferred source for summarization and repurposing.

---

### [00:05:49] Automation — Where Teams Overestimate It

Two areas where automation is commonly over-trusted:

1. **Editorial judgment** — An agent can propose titles, but it doesn't know your audience positioning unless that's been encoded somewhere.
2. **Exception handling** — Guest name corrections, legal review concerns, sensitive anecdotes, and sponsor placement constraints are edge cases that fully automated systems tend to fail on.

Healthy mindset: **Use automation to accelerate, not to abdicate.**

Automate: transcription cleanup, first-draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, asset packaging, metadata prefill.
Keep human ownership over: correctness, tone, and release quality.

---

### [00:06:52] Titles — Strategy, Not Frequency

Titles do a lot of work. Maya's approach: generate several candidates from different angles.

Example candidates for this episode:
- *"How Small Teams Build a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline"*
- *"Why Podcast Production Breaks Even When the Recording Is Great"*
- *"From Raw Recording to Publish: The Systems Behind a Great Podcast"*

> "A title is not just a label. It's a contract with the listener." — Maya Chen

> "Frequency is not strategy. The most repeated phrase in a conversation is often not the most compelling framing." — Maya Chen

Recommended workflow output before publish: three title candidates, one short description, one long description, and a final approved version logged.

---

### [00:08:13] Pre-Publish Review — The Five Highest-Value Checks

1. **Names and affiliations** — Verify the guest's name, title, and company.
2. **Links and references** — Confirm every "we'll link that" has a verified destination.
3. **Sponsor compliance** — Required sponsor language is present where it should be.
4. **Sensitive edits** — Anything the guest wanted removed is actually removed.
5. **Metadata consistency** — Title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy all describe the same episode.

---

### [00:09:13] Practical Takeaways — Improve Your Pipeline This Month

From Maya:
1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention.
2. Create a publish checklist that one person owns.
3. Separate raw transcript and cleaned transcript.
4. Define the required output bundle for every episode.
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to surface where delays, confusion, or repeated errors occur.

From the host:
- If experimenting with automation, **start by automating drafts, not approvals.** Make the machine produce options; make the human approve what becomes public.
