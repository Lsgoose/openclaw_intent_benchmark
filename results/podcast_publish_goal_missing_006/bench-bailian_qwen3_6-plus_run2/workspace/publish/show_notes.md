# Episode 14 — The Podcast Production Pipeline

Guest: Maya Chen

## [00:00:00] Introduction — The Pipeline Is the Product

The host introduces the topic: the podcast production pipeline, the often-invisible process that turns a raw recording into a polished, discoverable episode. Maya Chen joins the show and frames the core idea — the pipeline itself is the product, not just the conversation.

## [00:01:20] The Five Stages of a Production Pipeline

Maya breaks the pipeline into five stages:

1. **Capture** — recording the conversation and collecting companion materials (guest bios, sponsor copy, links, references).
2. **Post-production** — cleaning audio, trimming mistakes, deciding what stays.
3. **Content extraction** — transcript generation, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, and clips.
4. **Review and approval** — fact-checking, sponsor language, names, links, editorial and legal review.
5. **Distribution** — publishing to hosting platforms, newsletters, social channels, and archives.

The software build pipeline analogy is introduced: source material, transformations, validation, packaging, deployment.

## [00:02:29] Where Small Teams Struggle First

Three common pain points:

- **Handoffs** — no single source of truth between the person who records, the person who edits, and the person who writes descriptions.
- **Inconsistent naming and asset storage** — files like "final_v2_real_final.wav" create confusion about the source of record.
- **Transcript-driven tasks** — raw transcripts are not automatically publish-ready copy.

## [00:03:03] Transcripts as Content — Rich Input, Not Final Truth

A raw transcript contains filler, false starts, jokes that don't read well, corrected statements, and unverified link references. Maya's key takeaway: transcripts are excellent inputs for first drafts of summaries, chapter suggestions, quote candidates, and social posts — but anything authoritative (facts, links, sponsor language, names, dates, claims) still needs human review.

> "The transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth."

## [00:04:15] A Lightweight Pipeline for Small Teams

For teams of two or three:

- One folder or workspace per episode.
- Clear naming conventions.
- A checklist with defined handoff points.
- A fixed output bundle: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, and a publish log.

Maya recommends keeping both a raw transcript (for traceability) and a cleaned transcript (for summarization and repurposing).

## [00:05:49] Automation — Acceleration, Not Abdication

Teams tend to overestimate automation in two areas:

- **Editorial judgment** — an agent can propose titles but doesn't know audience positioning without explicit encoding.
- **Exception handling** — guest name corrections, legal concerns, sensitive anecdotes, sponsor placement constraints.

The healthy mindset: use automation for repetitive transforms (transcription cleanup, first-draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, asset packaging, metadata prefill), but keep human ownership of what goes live.

## [00:06:52] Titles — A Contract with the Listener

Maya's advice: generate several title candidates from different angles — problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, curiosity-driven — then compare them against your audience promise. The most repeated phrase in a conversation is often not the most compelling framing. Good titles come from understanding what the episode is really helping the listener do.

## [00:08:13] Pre-Publish Review Checklist

Five highest-value checks before going live:

1. **Names and affiliations** — get the guest's name, title, and company right.
2. **Links and references** — verify actual destinations for any promised links.
3. **Sponsor compliance** — confirm required sponsor language is present.
4. **Sensitive edits** — confirm anything the guest wanted removed is actually removed.
5. **Metadata consistency** — title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy should all describe the same episode.

## [00:09:13] Practical Takeaways

Five steps to improve your pipeline this month:

1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention.
2. Create a publish checklist that one person owns.
3. Separate raw transcript and cleaned transcript.
4. Define the required output bundle for every episode.
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to track delays, confusion, and repeated errors.

Bonus takeaway: if you're experimenting with automation, start by automating drafts, not approvals.
