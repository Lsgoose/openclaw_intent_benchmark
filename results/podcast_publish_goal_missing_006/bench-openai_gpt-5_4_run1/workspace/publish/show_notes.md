# Show Notes

## [00:00:00] Why title tests fail
The episode opens with a core problem: teams misread title tests when they confuse click-through rate with audience fit. The conversation frames title testing as a question of audience promise, not just surface-level performance.

## [00:00:18] Guest introduction
Jordan Kim joins to explain how podcast networks approach title experiments and packaging reviews.
- Jordan Kim: https://creators.example.com/guests/jordan-kim
- Packaging review: https://creators.example.com/resources/packaging-review

## [00:00:34] Build a title test matrix around audience promise
A useful title test matrix only works when each option maps to a real audience promise. Without that, results mostly measure novelty instead of resonance.
- Title test matrix: https://creators.example.com/resources/title-test-matrix

## [00:01:04] Stop overvaluing CTR and novelty
The discussion shifts to a common failure mode: teams focus too heavily on thumbnail packaging, click-through rate, and novelty while neglecting listener intent.
- Packaging review: https://creators.example.com/resources/packaging-review

## [00:01:39] What to track in a test log
Rather than relying on raw winners alone, the recommendation is to keep a test log with the hypothesis, audience segment, winning frame, and the reason it won.
- Title test matrix: https://creators.example.com/resources/title-test-matrix

## [00:02:09] What a healthy title workflow looks like
A practical workflow includes one shared title board, a short review loop, and a final approved headline before distribution. The goal is not endless testing; it is learning which promise best matches the episode.

## [00:02:41] The mistake that breaks reuse
A major topic shift focuses on context loss: teams run tests without recording the surrounding conditions, then reuse a winner in the wrong setting and see weaker performance.

## [00:03:10] What small teams should do now
The episode closes with a simple action plan: start an experiment log, write the audience promise for each title option, and stop treating click-through rate as the whole strategy.
