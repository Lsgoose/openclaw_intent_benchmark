# Episode 14 · Building a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline

## [00:00:00] Why the pipeline is part of the product
- The production pipeline shapes the entire listener experience, not just the recording.
- Titles, transcripts, chapter markers, and metadata all influence discoverability and navigation.
- Small process mistakes compound into a noticeably worse episode.

## [00:01:20] The five stages of a podcast production pipeline
- Capture: recording the conversation and collecting guest bios, sponsor copy, and reference links.
- Post-production: cleaning audio, trimming mistakes, and deciding what stays.
- Content extraction: transcripts, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapters, titles, and clips.
- Review and approval: checking facts, names, links, and sponsor language for editorial and legal risk.
- Distribution: publishing to the host platform, newsletter, social channels, and archives.

## [00:02:29] Common failure modes for small teams
- Painful handoffs when no one has a single source of truth for an episode.
- Confusing file names like "final_v2_real_final.wav" that obscure the source of record.
- Treating raw transcripts as publish-ready copy instead of noisy source material.

## [00:03:34] Using transcripts as rich inputs, not final truth
- Transcripts are great for first drafts of summaries, chapters, quotes, and social copy.
- Anything that sounds authoritative—facts, links, sponsor language, names, affiliations, dates, or claims—still needs human review.
- Key idea: the transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth.

## [00:04:26] Designing a lightweight pipeline for small teams
- Use one folder or workspace per episode with a clear naming convention.
- Maintain a fixed output bundle: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, and publish log.
- Standardizing outputs makes downstream repurposing and distribution much easier.

## [00:04:54] Raw vs. cleaned transcripts
- Keep the raw transcript for traceability and internal reference.
- Create a cleaned transcript that removes disfluencies, fixes speaker labels, and improves readability.
- Use the cleaned transcript as the default source for summaries and repurposed content.

## [00:05:49] Where automation and agents help—and where they don’t
- Automation works best on repetitive transforms: transcript cleanup, first-draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, and metadata prefill.
- Editorial judgment and exception handling still require humans, especially on edge cases.
- Treat automation as acceleration, not abdication; someone still owns what goes live.

## [00:06:52] Crafting titles that act like contracts
- Generate multiple title candidates from different angles: problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, curiosity-driven.
- Don’t pick titles purely by repeated phrases in the transcript; frequency is not strategy.
- A good title reflects the true promise of the episode for the listener.

## [00:08:13] High-value checks before you hit publish
- Verify guest names, titles, and affiliations.
- Confirm all promised links and references, including sponsor URLs.
- Ensure sponsor copy meets required language and placement constraints.
- Double-check that requested sensitive edits are actually applied.
- Align title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy so they all describe the same episode.

## [00:09:13] A five-step improvement plan for small shows
- Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention.
- Create a publish checklist with a clearly responsible owner.
- Separate raw and cleaned transcripts for every episode.
- Define the required output bundle and stick to it.
- Keep a lightweight episode log to spot delays, confusion, and recurring errors.
