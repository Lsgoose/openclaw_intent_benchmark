# Episode 14 – Building a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline for Small Teams

**Guest:**
- **Maya Chen** – Consultant to media teams and creator businesses on production systems and operations.
  - Profile: https://creators.example.com/guests/maya-chen

## Episode overview

In this conversation, we break down what it actually takes to ship a reliable podcast, week after week. Consultant **Maya Chen** walks through a five‑stage production pipeline and shows how even small teams can reduce chaos, improve quality, and make better use of their transcripts.

> “A transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth.” – Maya Chen

We cover why the pipeline *is* part of the product, how to avoid version sprawl (no more `final_v2_real_final.wav`), and where automation helps vs. where you still need human judgment before something goes live.

## What we cover

**1. The five stages of a podcast production pipeline**
- Capture: recording the conversation and collecting guest bios, sponsor copy, links, and references.
- Post‑production: cleaning audio, trimming mistakes, and deciding what stays in the episode.
- Content extraction: transcript generation, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, and clips.
- Review & approval: checking facts, sponsor language, names, links, and any potential editorial or legal issues.
- Distribution: shipping the final package to your host, newsletter, social channels, and archives.

**2. Why transcripts are powerful but not enough**
- The difference between raw transcripts (for traceability) and cleaned transcripts (for readability and downstream use).
- How unedited transcripts can carry over filler, false starts, unverified links, and corrected statements.
- Why anything authoritative—facts, names, links, dates, claims, sponsor language—still needs human review.

**3. Designing a lightweight but reliable pipeline for small teams**
- One workspace per episode with a clear naming convention.
- A checklist with defined handoffs and ownership.
- A standard output bundle: final audio, raw + cleaned transcripts, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, and a simple publish log.

**4. Using automation and agents responsibly**
- Where automation shines: first‑draft summaries, chapter suggestions, quote candidates, formatting, asset packaging, and metadata prefill.
- The limits of automation: audience positioning, sensitive edits, sponsor constraints, and edge cases.
- The mindset shift: use automation for acceleration, not abdication.

**5. Titles, metadata, and the listener contract**
- Generating multiple title options: problem‑oriented, guest‑oriented, outcome‑oriented, curiosity‑driven.
- Why the best title aligns with your audience promise, not just repeated phrases in the transcript.
- Keeping metadata consistent across title, description, transcript, chapters, and social copy.

## Practical takeaways

By the end of the episode, you’ll have a simple blueprint to improve your pipeline this month:

1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention.
2. Create a publish checklist that one person owns.
3. Maintain both raw and cleaned transcripts, and know when to use each.
4. Decide on a required output bundle for every episode.
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to spot delays, confusion, or repeated errors.
6. If you’re experimenting with automation, start by automating drafts—not approvals.
