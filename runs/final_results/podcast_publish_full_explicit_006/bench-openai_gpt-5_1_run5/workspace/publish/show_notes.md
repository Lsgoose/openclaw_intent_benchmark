# Episode 14 – Building a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline

## [00:00:00] Why your production pipeline *is* the product
- How a show goes from raw recording to polished episode: audio, transcript, chapter markers, social clips, title, description, and metadata.
- Maya Chen joins as a guest advisor on production systems.
- The idea that listeners experience the whole pipeline, not just the conversation.

## [00:01:20] The five stages of a podcast production pipeline
- Capture: recording plus companion materials (guest bios, sponsor copy, links, references).
- Post-production: cleaning audio, trimming mistakes, deciding what stays.
- Content extraction: transcripts, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, clips.
- Review & approval: fact checks, sponsor language, names, links, editorial/legal issues.
- Distribution: publishing to hosting platforms, newsletter, social channels, and archives.

## [00:02:29] Common pain points for small teams
- Fragile workflows that rely on memory and “heroics.”
- Handoffs without a single source of truth; confusion over who owns what.
- Inconsistent naming and storage (e.g., “final_v2_real_final.wav”).
- Over-trusting raw transcripts as publish-ready content.

## [00:03:03] Transcripts as rich input, not final truth
- Raw transcripts contain filler, false starts, jokes that don’t read well, and statements corrected later.
- Unverified promises like “we’ll put the link in the show notes” can leak into downstream content.
- Transcripts are excellent inputs for drafts (summaries, chapters, quotes, social options).
- Anything authoritative—facts, links, sponsor language, names, affiliations, dates, claims—still needs human review.

## [00:04:15] Designing a lightweight but reliable pipeline for small teams
- One folder or workspace per episode with clear naming conventions.
- A simple checklist with defined handoff points between capture, editing, copy, and publish.
- A standard output bundle for every episode: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, and publish log.
- How standardizing outputs makes downstream reuse and automation easier.

## [00:04:54] Raw vs. clean transcripts
- Why you should keep both raw and cleaned transcripts.
- Raw transcript: retained for traceability and internal reference.
- Clean transcript: removes disfluencies, fixes speaker labels, and improves readability.
- Cleaned transcripts become the preferred source for summarization and repurposing.
- Separating raw and cleaned materials reduces noise for tools and teammates.

## [00:05:49] Where automation and agents actually help
- Automation is best for repetitive transforms: transcript cleanup, first-draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, metadata prefill, and asset packaging.
- Automation is weaker at editorial judgment and exception handling (name corrections, legal concerns, sensitive anecdotes, sponsor constraints).
- A healthy mindset: use automation for acceleration, not abdication; a human still owns what goes live.

## [00:06:52] Crafting titles that act as a contract with the listener
- Why teams obsess over titles and why that’s justified.
- Generating multiple title candidates from different angles:
  - Problem-oriented (e.g., production pipelines).
  - Guest-oriented.
  - Outcome-oriented or curiosity-driven.
- Titles shouldn’t be chosen purely by repeated phrases in the transcript.
- A good title reflects what the episode helps the listener do.

## [00:08:13] High-value final checks before publish
- Names and affiliations: getting the guest’s name, title, and company right.
- Links and references: verifying every destination that gets “we’ll link that” treatment.
- Sponsor compliance: confirming required language and placement.
- Sensitive edits: ensuring any promised removals are actually made.
- Metadata consistency: aligning title, description, transcript, chapters, and social copy.

## [00:09:13] Quick wins to improve your pipeline this month
- Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention.
- Create a publish checklist that one person clearly owns.
- Explicitly separate raw and cleaned transcripts.
- Define a required output bundle for every episode.
- Maintain a lightweight episode log to spot delays, confusion, or repeated errors.
- If you use automation, start by automating drafts, not approvals.
