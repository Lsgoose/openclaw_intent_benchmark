# Pipeline Episode with Maya Chen

## Introduction: What Is a Podcast Production Pipeline?
- **(00:00 - 00:27)** Host introduces the topic: how a show goes from raw recording to polished episode with clean audio, transcript, chapter markers, social clips, title, description, and discovery metadata.

## The Pipeline as Product
- **(00:27 - 01:27)** Maya Chen joins and explains that the production pipeline shapes the entire listener experience. She notes that people often think the product is just the conversation, but the way it gets edited, packaged, labeled, and distributed matters significantly. Poor titles reduce clicks, sloppy transcripts hurt downstream content, incorrect chapter markers break navigation, and inconsistent checklists lead to compounding mistakes.

## The Five Stages of Production
- **(01:27 - 02:07)** Maya breaks the pipeline into five stages:
  1. **Capture** – recording and collecting companion materials (bios, sponsor copy, links)
  2. **Post-production** – cleaning audio, trimming mistakes
  3. **Content extraction** – transcripts, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapters, titles, clips
  4. **Review and approval** – fact-checking, sponsor language, names, links
  5. **Distribution** – publishing to hosting platform, newsletter, social channels, archives

## Common Pain Points for Small Teams
- **(02:07 - 03:03)** Three primary struggles: (1) handoffs between team members without a single source of truth, (2) inconsistent naming and asset storage (e.g., "final_v2_real_final.wav"), and (3) transcript-driven tasks that look easy but require significant editorial judgment.

## Transcripts: Rich Input, Not Final Truth
- **(03:03 - 04:15)** Discussion of why raw transcripts are not publish-ready. They contain filler, false starts, jokes that don't read well, corrected statements, and unverified link mentions. Transcripts are excellent for first drafts of summaries, chapter suggestions, quotes, and social posts—but facts, links, sponsor language, names, affiliations, dates, and claims require human review.

## Lightweight Pipeline for Small Teams
- **(04:15 - 04:54)** For teams of 2-3 people: one folder per episode, clear naming conventions, a checklist with defined handoff points, and a standardized output bundle (final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapters, thumbnail, publish log).

## Raw vs. Clean Transcripts
- **(04:54 - 05:49)** Maya recommends keeping both: the raw transcript for traceability and internal reference, and a cleaned version with disfluencies removed, speaker labels fixed, and readable formatting. This separation prevents downstream systems from inheriting source mess.

## Automation: Acceleration, Not Abdication
- **(05:49 - 06:52)** Common overestimation of automation occurs in editorial judgment (agents can propose titles but don't know audience positioning unless encoded) and exception handling (edge cases like name corrections, legal reviews, sensitive anecdotes, or sponsor constraints). Automation should accelerate repetitive transforms—cleanup, first drafts, suggestions, formatting, packaging—but humans must own correctness, tone, and release quality.

## Titling Strategy
- **(06:52 - 08:13)** Generate several candidates from different angles: problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, curiosity-driven. Compare against audience promise. A title is a contract with the listener, not just a label. Frequency is not strategy—the most repeated phrase is often not the most compelling framing.

## Pre-Publish Review Checklist
- **(08:13 - 09:12)** Five highest-value checks: (1) names and affiliations, (2) links and references, (3) sponsor compliance, (4) sensitive edits, and (5) metadata consistency across all surfaces.

## Practical Takeaways for This Month
- **(09:12 - 10:05)** Five steps to improve your pipeline now: (1) define standard folder structure and naming, (2) create a publish checklist with one owner, (3) separate raw and cleaned transcripts, (4) define required output bundle for every episode, and (5) keep a lightweight episode log to track delays and errors.
