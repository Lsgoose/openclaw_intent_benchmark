# Episode 14 – Building a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline

**Guest:** Maya Chen – media production systems advisor

---

## [00:00:00] Introduction

Host introduces the episode's focus: how a show travels from raw recording to polished, distributed episode. Maya Chen joins to discuss the systems behind that journey.

> "The pipeline is the product." — Maya Chen

---

## [00:01:20] The Five Stages of a Podcast Production Pipeline

Maya breaks down the full pipeline into five stages:

1. **Capture** – Recording the conversation plus companion materials (guest bios, sponsor copy, links, references)
2. **Post-production** – Audio cleanup, trimming, and editorial decisions
3. **Content extraction** – Transcript generation, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, and clips
4. **Review and approval** – Fact-checking, sponsor language, names, links, and editorial/legal review
5. **Distribution** – Publishing to hosting platform, newsletter, social channels, and archives

The pipeline is analogous to a software build: source material → transformations → validation → packaging → deployment.

---

## [00:02:29] Where Small Teams Struggle

Three common pain points for small teams:

- **Handoffs without a single source of truth** – multiple people own different steps with no shared reference
- **Inconsistent naming and asset storage** – files like `final_v2_real_final.wav` create version confusion
- **Treating raw transcripts as publish-ready copy** – transcripts contain filler, false starts, unverified links, and statements later corrected

> "The transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth." — Maya Chen

---

## [00:04:15] Lightweight Pipeline for Small Teams

Practical structure for a two-to-three person team:

- One folder per episode with a clear naming convention
- A checklist with defined handoff points
- A **standard output bundle** for every episode: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, and a publish log

**Raw vs. cleaned transcript:** Keep both. The raw transcript preserves traceability; the cleaned transcript (disfluencies removed, speaker labels fixed) becomes the preferred source for summarization and repurposing.

---

## [00:05:49] Automation: What It Can and Can't Do

Where teams overestimate automation:

- **Editorial judgment** – agents can propose titles but don't know audience positioning unless it's encoded
- **Exception handling** – guest name corrections, legal concerns, sensitive anecdotes, sponsor constraints

Healthy mindset: *use automation to accelerate, not abdicate*. Automate repetitive transforms (transcription cleanup, first-draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, metadata prefill). Keep human ownership over what goes live.

---

## [00:06:52] Titles: Strategy Over Frequency

Titles do significant work — they're a contract with the listener, not just a label. Recommended approach:

- Generate several candidates from different angles: problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, curiosity-driven
- Compare against your actual audience promise
- Frequency of a phrase in the transcript ≠ best framing

Example title candidates for a production-pipeline episode:
- *"How Small Teams Build a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline"*
- *"Why Podcast Production Breaks Even When the Recording Is Great"*
- *"From Raw Recording to Publish: The Systems Behind a Great Podcast"*

Workflow standard: three title candidates, one short description, one long description, and a final approved version logged before publish.

---

## [00:08:13] Pre-Publish Review Checklist

Five highest-value checks before an episode goes live:

1. **Names and affiliations** – guest name, title, and company verified
2. **Links and references** – every promised link confirmed at its actual destination
3. **Sponsor compliance** – required sponsor language present in the right places
4. **Sensitive edits** – anything the guest requested removed is actually removed
5. **Metadata consistency** – title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy all describe the same episode

---

## [00:09:13] Practical Takeaways

Steps to improve your pipeline this month:

1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. Create a publish checklist with a single owner
3. Separate raw transcript from cleaned transcript
4. Define the required output bundle for every episode
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to surface delays, confusion, and repeated errors

**Bonus:** If experimenting with automation, start by automating *drafts*, not *approvals*. Make the machine produce options; make the human approve what becomes public.
