# Episode 14: Building a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline

## Introduction [00:00:00 - 00:01:20]
- Welcome and episode topic: podcast production pipelines
- Introduction of guest Maya Chen, who advises media teams on production systems
- The pipeline shapes the entire listener experience
- Production discipline matters even for small teams

## The Five Stages of a Production Pipeline [00:01:20 - 00:02:29]
1. **Capture** – Recording the conversation and collecting companion materials (guest bios, sponsor copy, links, references)
2. **Post-production** – Cleaning audio, trimming mistakes, deciding what stays in the episode
3. **Content extraction** – Transcript generation, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, clips
4. **Review and approval** – Fact-checking, sponsor language verification, names, links, editorial and legal review
5. **Distribution** – Publishing to hosting platform, newsletter, social channels, and archives

## Common Pain Points for Small Teams [00:02:29 - 00:03:08]
- **Handoffs** – No single source of truth between record/edit/description roles
- **Inconsistent naming** – Files with names like "final_v2_real_final.wav" create confusion
- **Transcript-driven tasks** – Raw transcripts require human judgment; they are not automatically publish-ready

## Raw vs. Clean Transcripts [00:03:08 - 00:05:48]
- Raw transcripts contain filler, false starts, jokes that don't read well, and unverified statements
- Transcripts are excellent inputs for first drafts (summaries, chapters, quotes, social posts)
- Content requiring review: facts, links, sponsor language, names, affiliations, dates, claims
- **Key principle:** "The transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth." [00:04:08]
- Recommendation: Keep both raw transcript (for traceability) and cleaned transcript (for repurposing)
- Clean transcripts should remove disfluencies, fix speaker labels, and format for readability
- Separating raw from cleaned material prevents downstream systems from inheriting source mess

## Lightweight Pipeline for Small Teams [00:05:48 - 00:08:12]
**Core practices:**
- One folder or workspace per episode
- Clear naming convention
- Checklist with defined handoff points
- Fixed output bundle for every episode: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, publish log

**Title creation:**
- Generate candidates from multiple angles: problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, curiosity-driven
- Compare against audience promise
- A title is a contract with the listener
- Frequency is not strategy—most repeated phrase is not necessarily the most compelling framing

**Workflow outputs:** Three title candidates, one short description, one long description, final approved version logged before publish [00:08:04]

## Pre-Publish Review Checklist [00:08:12 - 00:09:13]
Five high-value checks before going live:
1. Names and affiliations – Verify guest name, title, company
2. Links and references – Confirm "we'll link that" promises
3. Sponsor compliance – Required sponsor language present
4. Sensitive edits – Confirm requested removals are complete
5. Metadata consistency – Title, description, transcript, chapters, and social copy all describe the same episode

## Automation: What to Automate vs. Human Review [00:09:13 - 00:10:04]
**Automation excels at:**
- Repetitive transforms: transcription cleanup, first-draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, asset packaging, metadata prefill

**Human judgment required for:**
- Editorial decisions (audience positioning)
- Exception handling (guest corrections, legal reviews, sensitive anecdotes, sponsor constraints)
- Final approval of what goes live

**Key mindset:** Use automation for acceleration, not abdication. "Make the machine produce options. Make the human approve what becomes public." [00:10:01]

## Actionable Takeaways [00:10:04 - 00:10:24]
To improve your pipeline this month:
1. Define standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. Create a publish checklist with a single owner
3. Separate raw and cleaned transcripts
4. Define required output bundle for every episode
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to track delays and repeated errors
