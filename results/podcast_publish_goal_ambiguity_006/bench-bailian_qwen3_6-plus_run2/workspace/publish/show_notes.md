# Episode 14 — The Podcast Production Pipeline with Maya Chen

## [00:00:00] Introduction: The Pipeline Is the Product
The host introduces the episode's topic — the podcast production pipeline — and welcomes guest Maya Chen, who advises media teams and creator businesses on production systems. Maya frames the core idea: the pipeline itself is the product, not just the conversation.

## [00:01:20] The Five Stages of a Podcast Production Pipeline
Maya breaks the pipeline into five stages:
1. **Capture** — recording and collecting companion materials (bios, sponsor copy, links, references).
2. **Post-production** — cleaning audio, trimming mistakes, deciding what stays.
3. **Content extraction** — transcript generation, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, clips.
4. **Review and approval** — fact-checking, sponsor language, names, links, editorial and legal review.
5. **Distribution** — publishing to hosting platforms, newsletters, social channels, and archives.

## [00:02:29] Where Small Teams Struggle First
Three common pain points:
- **Handoffs** — no single source of truth between recording, editing, and description writing.
- **Inconsistent naming and asset storage** — files like "final_v2_real_final.wav" create confusion.
- **Transcript-driven tasks** — raw transcripts are not automatically publish-ready copy.

## [00:03:03] Transcript as Content: Rich Input, Not Final Truth
Transcripts contain filler, false starts, jokes that don't read well, and unverified references. Maya recommends using transcripts as excellent inputs for first drafts of summaries, chapters, quotes, and social posts — but anything authoritative (facts, links, sponsor language, names, dates, claims) still needs human review.

## [00:04:15] A Lightweight but Reliable Pipeline for Small Teams
For teams of two or three: one folder per episode, clear naming conventions, a checklist with defined handoff points, and a fixed output bundle (final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, publish log).

## [00:04:54] Raw Transcript vs. Clean Transcript
Maya strongly recommends keeping both. The raw transcript provides traceability; the cleaned transcript (disfluencies removed, speaker labels fixed, readable formatting) becomes the preferred source for summarization and repurposing.

## [00:05:49] Automation and Agents: Where Teams Overestimate
Teams overestimate automation in two areas:
- **Editorial judgment** — agents can propose titles but don't know audience positioning without explicit encoding.
- **Exception handling** — edge cases (guest name corrections, legal concerns, sensitive anecdotes, sponsor constraints) trip up fully automated systems.

The healthy mindset: use automation for acceleration, not abdication.

## [00:06:52] Titles: Strategy Over Frequency
Generate title candidates from different angles (problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, curiosity-driven) and compare against audience promise. "Frequency is not strategy" — the most repeated phrase in a conversation is rarely the most compelling framing. A title is a contract with the listener.

## [00:08:13] Pre-Publish Review Checklist
Five highest-value checks:
1. Names and affiliations
2. Links and references
3. Sponsor compliance
4. Sensitive edits (confirm removals)
5. Metadata consistency across all surfaces

## [00:09:13] Practical Takeaways: Improve Your Pipeline This Month
1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention.
2. Create a publish checklist that one person owns.
3. Separate raw transcript and cleaned transcript.
4. Define the required output bundle for every episode.
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to track delays and repeated errors.

Bonus: if experimenting with automation, start by automating drafts, not approvals.
