# Episode 14 Show Notes

## [00:00:00] Introduction: why the pipeline shapes the listener experience
The episode opens by framing the production pipeline as more than operations: it directly shapes the final listener experience, from raw recording to polished release.

## [00:01:20] The five major stages of a podcast production pipeline
The conversation breaks the pipeline into five stages:
1. Capture — record the conversation and gather companion materials.
2. Post-production — clean audio, trim mistakes, and decide what stays.
3. Content extraction — generate transcript, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, and clips.
4. Review and approval — verify facts, sponsor language, names, links, and legal/editorial risks.
5. Distribution — publish to hosting, newsletter, social, and archives.

## [00:02:29] Common breakdowns in small-team workflows
Small teams often struggle with handoffs, inconsistent naming, and unclear source-of-truth files. Transcript-driven work also needs judgment, because a raw transcript is not automatically publish-ready content.

## [00:03:09] Transcripts as inputs, not final truth
The discussion highlights transcript pitfalls: filler, false starts, corrected statements, and unverified promises about links. Transcripts are excellent for first drafts, searchability, and repurposing, but facts, links, sponsor language, names, affiliations, dates, and claims still need human review.

## [00:04:15] Building a lightweight but reliable pipeline
For small teams, the recommended structure is one folder per episode, a clear naming convention, a checklist with handoff points, and a fixed output bundle that includes final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, and a publish log.

## [00:04:54] Raw transcript vs. cleaned transcript
The episode recommends keeping both versions. The raw transcript preserves traceability and original wording, while the cleaned transcript removes disfluencies, fixes speaker labels, and becomes the preferred input for summarization and repurposing.

## [00:05:49] What automation should and should not do
Automation works well for repetitive transforms such as transcription cleanup, first-draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, asset packaging, and metadata prefill. But editorial judgment, edge cases, and release responsibility still belong to humans.

## [00:06:57] Title strategy and audience promise
The episode argues that titles should be generated from different angles — problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, and curiosity-driven — then checked against the audience promise. Frequency alone is not strategy.

## [00:08:13] Review checks before publish
Five high-value checks are emphasized: names and affiliations, links and references, sponsor compliance, sensitive edits, and metadata consistency across title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy.

## [00:09:13] Practical steps to improve this month
The closing takeaway is to standardize folder structure and naming, assign one owner for the publish checklist, separate raw and cleaned transcripts, define a required output bundle, and keep a lightweight episode log to track delays and repeated errors.

## [00:09:50] Automate drafts, not approvals
The episode ends with a clear principle: use automation to produce options, but keep humans responsible for approving what becomes public.
