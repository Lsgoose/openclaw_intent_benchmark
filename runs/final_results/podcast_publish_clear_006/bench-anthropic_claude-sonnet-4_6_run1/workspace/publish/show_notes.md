# Episode 14: Building a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline

**Guest:** Maya Chen — media production systems advisor

---

## [00:00:00] Introduction

Host introduces the episode's focus: how a show moves from raw recording to a polished, distributed episode. Guest Maya Chen joins to discuss production systems and why the pipeline itself shapes the listener experience.

---

## [00:01:20] The Five Stages of a Podcast Production Pipeline

Maya breaks down the full pipeline into five stages:

1. **Capture** — Recording the conversation and collecting companion materials (guest bios, sponsor copy, links, references)
2. **Post-production** — Audio cleanup, trimming, and editorial decisions
3. **Content extraction** — Transcript generation, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, and clips
4. **Review and approval** — Fact-checking, sponsor language, names, links, and editorial/legal review
5. **Distribution** — Publishing to hosting platform, newsletter, social channels, and archives

---

## [00:02:29] Where Small Teams Struggle: Handoffs, Naming, and Transcripts

Three common pain points for small teams:

- **Handoffs** — No single source of truth when multiple people own different stages
- **Inconsistent naming and asset storage** — Version confusion ("final_v2_real_final.wav")
- **Transcript overconfidence** — Raw transcripts are not publish-ready copy; they contain filler, false starts, unverified links, and statements later corrected

---

## [00:03:34] What to Extract from Transcripts — and What Still Needs Human Review

Transcripts are strong inputs for:
- Episode summaries (first drafts)
- Chapter suggestions
- Quote candidates
- Social post options
- Searchability and repurposing

Human review is still required for:
- Facts, links, and references
- Sponsor language
- Names and affiliations
- Dates and claims

> "The transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth." — Maya Chen

---

## [00:04:15] Lightweight Pipeline Design for Small Teams

Recommended structure for a two- or three-person team:

- One folder or workspace per episode
- Clear naming convention
- Checklist with defined handoff points
- **Standard output bundle** for every episode: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, publish log

**On transcripts:** Keep both raw and cleaned versions. The raw transcript preserves traceability; the cleaned transcript becomes the preferred source for summarization and repurposing.

---

## [00:05:49] Automation: Where Teams Overestimate It

Two areas where automation is commonly overestimated:

1. **Editorial judgment** — Agents can propose titles, but audience positioning must be encoded explicitly
2. **Exception handling** — Edge cases (name corrections, legal concerns, sensitive anecdotes, sponsor constraints) require human attention

Healthy mindset: *Use automation to accelerate repetitive transforms — not to replace approval.*

Automate: transcription cleanup, first-draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, asset packaging, metadata prefill.
Keep human: correctness, tone, and release quality sign-off.

---

## [00:06:52] Titles: Generating Candidates from Multiple Angles

Recommended approach — generate title candidates from different frames:

- **Problem-oriented:** "Why Podcast Production Breaks Even When the Recording Is Great"
- **Guest-oriented:** "How Small Teams Build a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline"
- **Outcome-oriented:** "From Raw Recording to Publish: The Systems Behind a Great Podcast"

> "A title is not just a label. It's a contract with the listener." — Maya Chen

Frequency of a phrase in the transcript is not a reliable signal for title quality. Choose based on audience intent.

---

## [00:08:13] Pre-Publish Review: The Five Highest-Value Checks

Before any episode goes live:

1. **Names and affiliations** — Verify guest name, title, and company
2. **Links and references** — Confirm every promised link resolves correctly
3. **Sponsor compliance** — Required sponsor language is present and correctly placed
4. **Sensitive edits** — Confirm requested removals are actually removed
5. **Metadata consistency** — Title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy all describe the same episode

---

## [00:09:13] Practical Takeaways: Improving Your Pipeline This Month

Steps to take immediately:

1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. Create a publish checklist with a single owner
3. Separate raw transcript from cleaned transcript
4. Define the required output bundle for every episode
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to surface delays and repeated errors

Host addition: *If experimenting with automation, start by automating drafts — not approvals.*
