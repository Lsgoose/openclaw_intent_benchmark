# Episode 14 – Building a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline

## [00:00:00] Why your production pipeline *is* the product
- Defining the podcast production pipeline and why listeners feel its quality even if they never see it.
- The five high-level stages: capture, post-production, content extraction, review & approval, and distribution.
- Why titles, transcripts, chapter markers, and metadata shape discovery and listener trust.

## [00:01:27] The five stages of a podcast production pipeline
- Deep dive into each stage and how they mirror a software build pipeline.
- Common failure modes: handoffs, inconsistent naming, and messy asset storage.
- Why over-reliance on memory and “heroics” makes pipelines fragile.

## [00:02:33] Where small teams struggle first
- Typical pain points in multi-person workflows and unclear ownership.
- The danger of “final_v2_real_final.wav” file naming and missing sources of truth.
- Why transcript-driven tasks seem easy but actually require judgment.

## [00:03:03] Transcripts as inputs, not final truth
- How raw transcripts contain filler, false starts, and unverified promises of links.
- Using transcripts to generate summaries, chapters, quotes, and social drafts—without treating them as authoritative.
- The principle: treat the transcript as a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth.

## [00:04:15] Designing a lightweight pipeline for a 2–3 person team
- One folder or workspace per episode, with clear naming conventions.
- Using a checklist with defined handoff points to stabilize the process.
- Standardizing an output bundle: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, and publish log.

## [00:04:54] Raw vs. cleaned transcripts
- Why you should keep both a raw transcript and a cleaned, readable version.
- How a cleaned transcript improves summaries, quote cards, and repurposed content.
- Reducing “noise” before handing work to tools or teammates.

## [00:05:49] Automation, agents, and healthy expectations
- Where teams typically overestimate automation: editorial judgment and exception handling.
- Using automation to accelerate transforms (cleanup, drafts, formatting) rather than to replace human approval.
- The “acceleration, not abdication” mindset for public-facing content.

## [00:06:52] Titles as contracts with the listener
- Why title obsession is justified: titles carry discovery and expectation-setting.
- Generating multiple title candidates from different angles (problem, guest, outcome, curiosity).
- Avoiding frequency-based titling: the most repeated phrase is rarely the best framing.

## [00:08:13] High-value pre-publish checks
- Five priority checks: names/affiliations, links and references, sponsor compliance, sensitive edits, and metadata consistency.
- How mismatched titles, descriptions, and show notes erode listener trust.
- Aligning all surfaces so the same episode is described consistently everywhere.

## [00:09:13] Practical playbook: upgrading your pipeline this month
- Step-by-step changes for small shows:
  - Standardize episode folder structure and naming.
  - Create and assign ownership for a publish checklist.
  - Separate raw and cleaned transcripts.
  - Define a required output bundle for every episode.
  - Keep a lightweight episode log to spot delays and recurring errors.
- Starting with automation on drafts, not approvals—machines propose options, humans decide what goes live.
