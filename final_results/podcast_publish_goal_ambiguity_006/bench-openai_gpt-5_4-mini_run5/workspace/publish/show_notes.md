[00:00:00] Introduction
- The episode frames the podcast production pipeline as the thing that shapes the listener experience, from raw recording to polished episode assets.
- Maya Chen joins to discuss production systems for media teams and creator businesses.

[00:01:20] Pipeline stages
- Maya outlines five stages: capture, post-production, content extraction, review and approval, and distribution.
- The conversation compares podcast production to a software build pipeline with source material, transforms, validation, packaging, and deployment.

[00:02:29] Common failure points
- Small teams often struggle with handoffs, inconsistent naming and asset storage, and overconfidence in transcript-driven tasks.
- A raw transcript is not automatically publish-ready copy.

[00:03:34] Transcript use and human review
- Transcripts are strong inputs for summaries, chapter suggestions, quote candidates, and social posts.
- Facts, links, sponsor language, names, affiliations, dates, and claims still need human review.

[00:04:26] Lightweight pipeline basics
- Maya recommends one folder per episode, clear naming, a checklist with handoff points, and a fixed output bundle.
- The output bundle should include final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, and publish log.

[00:04:54] Raw vs. cleaned transcripts
- Keep the raw transcript for traceability and verification.
- Use the cleaned transcript as the preferred source for summarization and repurposing.

[00:05:49] Automation boundaries
- Agents can help with repetitive transforms, but editorial judgment and exception handling remain human responsibilities.
- Automation should accelerate work, not replace accountability for what goes live.

[00:06:52] Titles as listener contracts
- Generate multiple title candidates from different angles: problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, and curiosity-driven.
- Frequency in the transcript is not a strategy for title selection.

[00:08:13] Review checklist
- High-value checks include names and affiliations, links and references, sponsor compliance, sensitive edits, and metadata consistency.
- The title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy should all describe the same episode.

[00:09:13] Practical takeaways
- Start with standard folder structure and naming, a publish checklist, separate raw and cleaned transcripts, a required output bundle, and a lightweight episode log.
- Automate drafts, not approvals.
- The human should approve what becomes public.
