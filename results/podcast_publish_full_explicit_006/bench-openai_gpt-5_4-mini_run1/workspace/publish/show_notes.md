# Episode 14 Show Notes: Building a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline

## [00:00:00] Why the pipeline matters
The episode opens by framing the production pipeline as the thing that shapes the listener experience: raw recording, clean audio, transcript, chapter markers, social clips, title, description, and metadata all matter.

## [00:01:20] The five stages of a podcast pipeline
Maya Chen breaks the process into five stages: capture, post-production, content extraction, review and approval, and distribution. She compares it to a software build pipeline with source material, transformations, validation, packaging, and deployment.

## [00:02:29] Where small teams struggle first
The first failure point is handoffs, followed by inconsistent naming and asset storage. Transcript-driven tasks also require judgment; a raw transcript is not automatically publish-ready copy.

## [00:03:34] Transcript as input, not truth
A transcript can include filler, false starts, corrected statements, and unverified references. It is useful for first drafts of summaries, chapter suggestions, quote candidates, and social post options, but facts, links, sponsor language, names, affiliations, dates, and claims still need review.

## [00:04:26] What a lightweight pipeline should standardize
For a small team, Maya recommends one folder per episode, a clear naming convention, a checklist with defined handoff points, and a fixed output bundle: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail if used, and a publish log.

## [00:04:54] Why raw and cleaned transcripts should both exist
Keep the raw transcript for traceability and the cleaned transcript for readability. The cleaned version should be the preferred source for summarization and repurposing, while the raw transcript remains useful for verification.

## [00:05:49] Where automation helps and where it fails
Automation is strongest for repetitive transforms like transcription cleanup, first-draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, asset packaging, and metadata prefill. It is weakest on editorial judgment and exception handling.

## [00:06:57] Title strategy
Maya recommends generating multiple title candidates from different angles: problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, and curiosity-driven. A title is a contract with the listener, not just a label.

## [00:08:13] Highest-value review checks
The most important pre-publish checks are names and affiliations, links and references, sponsor compliance, sensitive edits, and metadata consistency across title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy.

## [00:09:13] First improvements for a small show
The practical takeaway: define a folder structure and naming convention, create a publish checklist, separate raw and cleaned transcripts, define the output bundle, and keep a lightweight episode log. Automate drafts, not approvals.
