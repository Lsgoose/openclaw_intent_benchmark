# Episode 14: The Podcast Production Pipeline with Maya Chen

## Introduction (00:00:00 - 00:00:46)
- Host introduces the topic: podcast production pipelines — how a show goes from raw recording to polished episode with clean audio, transcript, chapter markers, social clips, title, description, and metadata.
- Guest Maya Chen joins; she advises media teams and creator businesses on production systems.

## The Five Stages of a Podcast Production Pipeline (00:01:27 - 00:02:06)
Maya breaks podcast production into five stages:
1. **Capture** — Recording the conversation and collecting companion materials (guest bios, sponsor copy, links, references).
2. **Post-production** — Cleaning audio, trimming mistakes, deciding what stays in the episode.
3. **Content extraction** — Transcript generation, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, and clips.
4. **Review and approval** — Fact-checking, sponsor language verification, names, links, editorial/legal review.
5. **Distribution** — Final package goes to hosting platform, newsletter, social channels, and archives.

> "If the title is vague, people don't click. If the transcript is sloppy, your downstream content generation gets worse. If your chapter markers are off, listeners can't navigate."

## Where Small Teams Struggle (00:02:33 - 00:03:02)
- **Handoffs** — No single source of truth between recording, editing, and description writing.
- **Naming and storage** — Files called "final_v2_real_final.wav" create confusion about which version is authoritative.
- **Transcript-driven tasks** — Raw transcripts are not automatically publish-ready; they require judgment.

## Transcripts: Raw vs. Clean (00:04:54 - 00:05:22)
- Maya recommends keeping both: **raw transcript** for traceability, **cleaned transcript** for summarization and repurposing.
- Clean transcript removes disfluencies, fixes speaker labels, and formats for readability.

## Automation: What Works and What Doesn't (00:05:49 - 00:06:30)
**Automation works well for:**
- Transcription cleanup
- First-draft notes
- Chapter suggestions
- Formatting and asset packaging
- Metadata prefill

**Automation fails on:**
- Editorial judgment (titles matching audience positioning)
- Exception handling (guest name corrections, legal reviews, sensitive edits, sponsor constraints)

> "The transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth."

## Practical Takeaways for Small Teams (00:09:23 - 00:09:50)
Maya's five first steps:
1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention.
2. Create a publish checklist with one owner.
3. Separate raw and cleaned transcripts.
4. Define the required output bundle for every episode (audio, transcript, notes, title, description, chapters, thumbnail, log).
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to track delays, confusion, and repeated errors.
