# Episode 14: Building a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline

## Show Notes

### [00:00] Introduction
Welcome to Episode 14, where we unpack how a raw podcast recording becomes a polished, distributed episode. We cover the full production pipeline, from capture to distribution, and why operational discipline directly shapes listener experience.

Guest: Maya Chen, media production systems advisor for creator teams and media businesses.
[Maya Chen bio](https://creators.example.com/guests/maya-chen)

### [00:01] The 5 core pipeline stages
Maya breaks the production process into 5 repeatable stages:
1. **Capture**: Record conversation + collect companion materials (guest bios, links, sponsor copy)
2. **Post-production**: Clean audio, trim mistakes, finalize episode content
3. **Content extraction**: Generate transcripts, summaries, show notes, chapters, titles, and clips
4. **Review and approval**: Validate facts, links, sponsor language, and editorial choices
5. **Distribution**: Publish final assets to hosting platforms, newsletters, and social channels

*Analogy: Podcast pipelines work like software build pipelines, with source material, transformations, validation, and deployment steps.*

### [00:02] Common small team pain points
The most frequent failures for small teams:
- Poor handoffs between team members with no single source of truth
- Inconsistent file naming and asset storage (e.g. "final_v2_real_final.wav")
- Overreliance on raw transcripts as publish-ready content

### [00:03] Transcript best practices
- Raw transcripts are for internal reference and traceability only
- Always create a cleaned transcript (disfluencies removed, speaker labels fixed) for repurposing
- Transcripts are excellent first-draft inputs, but never the final source of editorial truth
- All claims, facts, names, and links require human review before publishing

### [00:04] Lightweight pipeline for small teams
For teams of 2-3 people, prioritize:
- One dedicated folder/workspace per episode
- Clear, consistent file naming convention
- Defined handoff checklists
- Standardized output bundle (final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapters, publish log)

[Podcast release checklist template](https://creators.example.com/checklists/podcast-release)

### [00:05] Automation do's and don'ts
What teams usually overestimate about automation:
1. Editorial judgment (automation doesn't know your audience positioning)
2. Exception handling (edge cases like name corrections, legal reviews, and sponsor constraints break fully automated flows)

*Healthy rule: Use automation for acceleration, not abdication. Automate repetitive work (transcript cleanup, first-draft notes, formatting) but keep human ownership of final public content.*

### [00:06] Title strategy
Titles are a contract with your listener, not just a label. Generate multiple candidates from different angles:
- Problem-oriented
- Guest-oriented
- Outcome-oriented
- Curiosity-driven

Avoid choosing titles based only on most repeated phrases – frequency is not strategy.

### [00:08] Pre-publish high-value checks
Always validate these 5 items before going live:
1. Correct guest names, titles, and affiliations
2. Verified working links for all referenced resources
3. Sponsor compliance (required language present in correct placement)
4. Sensitive edits confirmed (anything requested to be removed is actually removed)
5. Metadata consistency across title, description, chapters, and social copy

### [00:09] Quick wins for pipeline improvement
If you want to upgrade your pipeline this month:
1. Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. Create a publish checklist owned by a single person
3. Separate raw and cleaned transcript workflows
4. Define required output assets for every episode
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to track repeated errors or delays
