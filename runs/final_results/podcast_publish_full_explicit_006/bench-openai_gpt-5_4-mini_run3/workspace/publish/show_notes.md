# Episode 14 Show Notes

## [00:00:00] Opening: why the pipeline matters
The episode opens by framing the podcast production pipeline as more than operations — it shapes the listener experience from raw recording through polished release.

## [00:01:20] The five stages of production
Maya Chen outlines five stages: capture, post-production, content extraction, review and approval, and distribution. She compares the pipeline to software build systems: source material, transformations, validation, packaging, and deployment.

## [00:02:29] Common failure points in small teams
The conversation turns to handoffs, inconsistent naming, and the hidden judgment involved in transcript-driven tasks. A raw transcript is not automatically publish-ready content.

## [00:03:40] Transcript as input, not final truth
Transcripts are useful for summaries, chapter ideas, quote candidates, and searchability, but facts, links, sponsor language, names, affiliations, dates, and claims still need human review.

## [00:04:26] Lightweight but reliable team workflow
A small team can stay organized with one folder per episode, clear naming conventions, a checklist for handoffs, and a fixed output bundle: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, and publish log.

## [00:04:59] Raw transcript vs. cleaned transcript
The episode recommends keeping both raw and cleaned transcripts. The cleaned version removes disfluencies, fixes speaker labels, and becomes the preferred source for summarization and repurposing.

## [00:05:49] Automation’s limits
Automation helps with repetitive transforms like transcription cleanup, first-draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, asset packaging, and metadata prefill. It should not replace editorial judgment or exception handling.

## [00:06:57] Titles as strategy
Several title angles are suggested: problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, and curiosity-driven. A title is described as a contract with the listener, not just a label.

## [00:08:20] Review checks before publishing
The highest-value checks are names and affiliations, links and references, sponsor compliance, sensitive edits, and metadata consistency across title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy.

## [00:09:13] Practical improvements for this month
The episode closes with a simple improvement plan: define a folder structure, assign checklist ownership, separate raw and cleaned transcripts, standardize the output bundle, and keep a lightweight episode log.
