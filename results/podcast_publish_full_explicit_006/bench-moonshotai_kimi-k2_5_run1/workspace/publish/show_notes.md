## Show Notes

### [00:00:00] Introduction: Why Pipeline Matters
The episode opens with a framing of podcast production pipelines as shaping the entire listener experience—not just the conversation itself, but how it gets edited, packaged, labeled, and distributed.

### [00:00:27] Guest Introduction: Maya Chen
Maya Chen joins the show to discuss how production systems operate as the invisible infrastructure behind every polished episode.

> "This is one of those topics people don't always notice until something breaks, and then suddenly everyone realizes the pipeline is the product."

### [00:01:20] The Five Stages of a Production Pipeline
Maya outlines the five core stages every pipeline needs:

1. **Capture** — Recording the conversation plus companion materials (guest bios, sponsor copy, links, references)
2. **Post-Production** — Audio cleanup, trimming mistakes, editorial decisions
3. **Content Extraction** — Transcripts, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, clips
4. **Review and Approval** — Fact-checking, sponsor language verification, legal/editorial review
5. **Distribution** — Publishing to hosting platforms, newsletters, social channels, and archives

### [00:02:29] Common Pain Points for Small Teams
Three recurring struggles:
- **Handoffs** — No single source of truth when different people handle recording, editing, and description writing
- **Inconsistent naming** — Files like "final_v2_real_final.wav" destroy confidence in the source of record
- **Transcript misuse** — Raw transcripts contain filler, false starts, and unverified claims that shouldn't flow directly to public content

### [00:03:40] Transcripts: Rich Input, Not Final Truth
Maya emphasizes that transcripts are excellent for first drafts but require human review for anything authoritative:

> "A transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth."

**Good for automation:** First drafts of summaries, chapter suggestions, quote candidates, social post options  
**Requires review:** Facts, links, sponsor language, names, affiliations, dates, claims

### [00:04:26] Lightweight Pipeline for Small Teams
Maya's recommendations for 2-3 person teams:
- One folder/workspace per episode
- Clear naming conventions
- Defined handoff checkpoints
- Standardized output bundle (final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapters, thumbnail, publish log)

### [00:04:54] Raw vs. Clean Transcripts
Keep both versions:
- **Raw transcript** — For traceability and internal reference
- **Cleaned transcript** — Remove disfluencies, fix speaker labels, format for readability; becomes the source for summarization and repurposing

### [00:05:49] Automation: Where Teams Overestimate
Two areas where automation often fails:
1. **Editorial judgment** — Agents can propose titles but don't know audience positioning unless explicitly encoded
2. **Exception handling** — Every few episodes brings edge cases (name corrections, legal concerns, sensitive edits, sponsor constraints) that break fully automated systems

> "Use automation to accelerate repetitive transforms... but don't give up responsibility for what goes live."

### [00:06:52] Titles as Audience Contracts
Maya advises generating multiple title candidates from different angles:
- Problem-oriented
- Guest-oriented
- Outcome-oriented
- Curiosity-driven

> "A title is not just a label. It's a contract with the listener."

**Example candidates for this episode:**
- "How Small Teams Build a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline"
- "Why Podcast Production Breaks Even When the Recording Is Great"
- "From Raw Recording to Publish: The Systems Behind a Great Podcast"

### [00:08:13] Pre-Publish Checklist
Five high-value checks before going live:
1. Names and affiliations — Verify guest details
2. Links and references — Confirm every "we'll link that" has a verified destination
3. Sponsor compliance — Required language is present
4. Sensitive edits — Confirm guest-requested removals are complete
5. Metadata consistency — Title, description, transcript, chapters, and social copy all describe the same episode

### [00:09:13] Takeaways for Small Shows
Five improvements to make this month:
1. Define standard episode folder structure and naming convention
2. Create a publish checklist with one clear owner
3. Separate raw and cleaned transcripts
4. Define required output bundle for every episode
5. Keep a lightweight episode log to identify delays and repeated errors

Bonus: Start automation by automating drafts, not approvals.

> "Make the machine produce options. Make the human approve what becomes public."
