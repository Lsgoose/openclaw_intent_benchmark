# Episode 14 Show Notes

[00:00:00] Opening: Why the production pipeline shapes the listener experience
The episode opens by framing the podcast production pipeline as the system that turns a raw recording into a polished release with clean audio, transcript, chapter markers, clips, title, description, and metadata.

[00:01:20] Pipeline stages: capture through distribution
Maya Chen outlines five stages: capture, post-production, content extraction, review and approval, and distribution. The discussion compares the workflow to a software build pipeline with source material, validation, packaging, and deployment.

[00:02:29] Handoffs, naming, and transcript risk
The conversation turns to common small-team failure points: unclear handoffs, messy file naming, and the danger of treating a transcript as ready-to-publish truth. A raw transcript can include filler, corrections, and unverified references.

[00:03:40] What transcripts are good for
Transcripts are positioned as strong inputs for summaries, chapter suggestions, quote candidates, and social drafts, but facts, links, sponsor language, names, dates, affiliations, and claims still need human review.

[00:04:26] Lightweight pipeline design for small teams
A reliable small-team setup includes one folder per episode, clear naming conventions, a defined checklist, and a fixed output bundle: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail if used, and a publish log.

[00:04:59] Raw transcript vs. cleaned transcript
The episode recommends keeping both versions. The raw transcript supports traceability and verification, while the cleaned transcript becomes the preferred source for summarization and repurposing.

[00:05:49] Automation boundaries
Automation is useful for repetitive transforms such as transcription cleanup, first-draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, asset packaging, and metadata prefill. Editorial judgment and exception handling still require a human owner.

[00:06:57] Title strategy
The episode advises generating several title candidates from different angles: problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, and curiosity-driven. The best title should match audience intent rather than simply repeat the most common phrase.

[00:08:13] Review checks before publish
Five key checks are highlighted: names and affiliations, links and references, sponsor compliance, sensitive edits, and metadata consistency across title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy.

[00:09:13] Practical takeaway for the month
The episode closes with a simple improvement plan: define folder structure and naming, assign one person to the checklist, separate raw and cleaned transcripts, define a standard output bundle, and keep a lightweight episode log. The machine can draft options; humans approve what goes public.
