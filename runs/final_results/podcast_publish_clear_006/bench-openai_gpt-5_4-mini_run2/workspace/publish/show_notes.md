# Episode 14 Show Notes

[00:00:00] Opening
The episode frames the podcast production pipeline as the system that shapes the listener experience, from raw recording to polished distribution.

[00:00:27] Guest introduction
Maya Chen joins to discuss why the pipeline becomes visible only when something breaks.

[00:01:20] Major stages of the pipeline
Five stages are outlined: capture, post-production, content extraction, review and approval, and distribution.

[00:02:29] Common small-team pain points
Handoffs, inconsistent file naming, and the mistaken assumption that a transcript is automatically publish-ready.

[00:03:09] Transcript as input, not truth
Raw transcripts can contain filler, false starts, corrected statements, and unverified link references, so they require human review before publication.

[00:04:15] Lightweight pipeline for small teams
A reliable setup uses a per-episode workspace, a clear naming convention, a checklist with handoff points, and a fixed output bundle.

[00:04:59] Raw transcript and cleaned transcript
Keep both. The raw transcript preserves traceability, while the cleaned transcript becomes the preferred source for summaries and repurposing.

[00:05:49] Automation boundaries
Automation can help with drafting, formatting, and packaging, but it should not replace editorial judgment or exception handling.

[00:06:57] Title strategy
Generate multiple title candidates from different angles and choose based on audience promise, not frequency alone.

[00:08:13] Review priorities
The highest-value checks are names and affiliations, links and references, sponsor compliance, sensitive edits, and metadata consistency.

[00:09:13] Practical takeaway
Define folder structure, own the publish checklist, separate raw and cleaned transcripts, standardize the output bundle, and keep a lightweight episode log.

[00:10:05] Sponsor mention
SoundLayer is the episode sponsor, described as a collaboration tool for small media teams that helps organize transcripts, asset versions, and review workflows.
