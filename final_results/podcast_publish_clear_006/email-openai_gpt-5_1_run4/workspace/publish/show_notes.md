# Episode 14  Building a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline

## [00:00:00] Why your production pipeline *is* the product
- Defining the podcast production pipeline and why it shapes the listener experience
- Maya Chen joins to discuss how production systems affect titles, transcripts, chapter markers, and metadata
- The idea that small operational issues (sloppy transcripts, vague titles, inconsistent checklists) compound into listener-facing problems

## [00:01:20] The five core stages of a podcast production pipeline
- Capture: recording plus gathering guest bios, sponsor copy, and reference links
- Post-production: cleaning audio, trimming mistakes, deciding what to keep
- Content extraction: transcripts, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, and clips
- Review and approval: fact checks, link verification, sponsor language, and legal or editorial review
- Distribution: publishing the final package to the host, newsletter, social channels, and archives

## [00:02:29] Common failure points in small-team workflows
- Painful handoffs when multiple people touch an episode without a single source of truth
- Confusing file names (e.g., many versions of "final" audio) eroding confidence in the source of record
- Over-reliance on raw transcripts as if they were publish-ready content

## [00:03:34] Transcripts as rich input, not final truth
- Why raw transcripts contain filler, false starts, jokes that dont read well, and unverified promises like well put the link in the show notes
- Using transcripts as inputs for first drafts of summaries, titles, chapter suggestions, quotes, and social posts
- The need for human review on facts, names, links, sponsor language, dates, and claims
- Quote-worthy idea: treat the transcript as a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth

## [00:04:26] Designing a lightweight, reliable pipeline for small teams
- Standardizing on one folder or workspace per episode with clear naming conventions
- Defining a simple checklist with explicit handoff points
- Establishing a fixed output bundle (final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, publish log)
- How standard outputs make downstream automation and collaboration easier

## [00:04:54] Raw transcript vs. cleaned transcript
- Keeping the raw transcript for traceability and internal reference
- Producing a cleaned transcript that fixes speaker labels, removes obvious disfluencies, and improves readability
- Using the cleaned transcript as the primary input for summaries and repurposed content while preserving the raw version for verification

## [00:05:49] Where automation helps and where it breaks
- Applying automation to repetitive transforms: transcript cleanup, draft notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, and metadata prefill
- Why editorial judgment and exception handling still require humans, especially around sensitive edits and edge cases
- Framing automation as acceleration, not abdication of responsibility

## [00:06:52] Crafting reliable, audience-aligned titles
- Generating multiple title candidates from different angles: problem-oriented, guest-oriented, and outcome-oriented
- Examples of alternative framings for an episode about production workflows
- Why frequency of phrases in a transcript isnt a strategy for picking titles
- Treating the title as a contract with the listener

## [00:08:13] High-value pre-publish review checks
- Verifying guest names, titles, and affiliations
- Confirming all promised links and references, including sponsor destinations
- Ensuring sponsor language meets requirements
- Double-checking that sensitive or requested removals are actually edited out
- Aligning metadata across title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy

## [00:09:13] Practical steps to improve your pipeline this month
- Define an episode folder structure and naming convention
- Create a publish checklist owned by a specific person
- Separate raw and cleaned transcripts as a default practice
- Standardize the required output bundle for every episode
- Maintain a lightweight episode log to track delays and recurring errors
- Start automating drafts and formatting before you automate approvals
