# Show Notes

## [00:00:00] Why title tests fail
The episode opens with a warning against confusing click-through rate with audience fit. The host frames the core problem: teams often interpret title test results as proof of strategy when they may only be measuring novelty.

## [00:00:34] A useful title test matrix needs a real audience promise
Jordan Kim explains that title test options only produce meaningful results when each option maps to a real audience promise. Without that alignment, teams are not learning what resonates with the right listeners.

## [00:01:04] Click-through rate is not the whole strategy
The conversation shifts to a common failure mode: over-indexing on packaging, novelty, and click-through rate while losing sight of listener intent. The point is not to reject testing, but to use it in service of audience fit.

## [00:01:39] What to track in a healthy title testing process
Jordan recommends keeping a test log that records the hypothesis, audience segment, winning frame, and why it won. That creates a learning system instead of a pile of isolated experiments.

## [00:02:09] What a healthy workflow looks like
A workable process includes one shared title board, a short review loop, and a final approved headline before distribution. The goal is not endless optimization but learning which promise actually matches the episode.

- Packaging review resource: https://creators.example.com/resources/packaging-review

## [00:02:41] Context loss is a repeat mistake
A major topic shift lands on a practical warning: when teams fail to record the context around a winning headline, they reuse it in the wrong place and performance drops.

## [00:03:10] What a small team should do this month
The closing advice is concrete: start a simple experiment log, write down the audience promise for each option, and stop treating click-through rate as the whole strategy.
