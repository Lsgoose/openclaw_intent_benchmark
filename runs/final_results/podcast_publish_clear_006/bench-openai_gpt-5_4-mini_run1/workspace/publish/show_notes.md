## 00:00 — Episode setup: why the pipeline matters
The episode opens by framing the podcast production pipeline as more than operations. It shapes the listener experience from raw recording to polished release.

## 00:27 — Guest introduction and core thesis
Maya Chen joins to argue that the pipeline becomes visible only when something breaks. In practice, the pipeline is the product.

## 00:47 — What production discipline changes
A show is not just the conversation itself. Editing, packaging, labeling, and distribution all affect whether the episode lands well and gets discovered.

## 01:20 — The major stages of a podcast pipeline
Maya breaks the process into five stages:
- Capture: record the conversation and collect companion materials
- Post-production: clean audio and trim mistakes
- Content extraction: generate transcript, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, and clips
- Review and approval: verify facts, sponsor language, names, links, and legal or editorial risks
- Distribution: publish to hosting, newsletter, social channels, and archives

## 02:29 — Common failure points for small teams
The first problems tend to be handoffs, inconsistent naming, and transcript-driven work that looks easy but still needs judgment.

## 03:09 — Why transcripts are not final truth
A transcript can include filler, corrections, and placeholder language like “we’ll put the link in the show notes” before the link is actually verified. Transcript text is useful, but not authoritative by default.

## 03:40 — What can be extracted versus what still needs review
Transcripts are strong inputs for first drafts of summaries, chapter suggestions, quote candidates, and social posts. Facts, names, links, sponsor language, affiliations, dates, and claims still need human review.

## 04:15 — A lightweight reliable workflow for small teams
A simple system works best: one folder per episode, clear naming, a defined checklist, and a fixed output bundle that always includes final audio, cleaned transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail if used, and a publish log.

## 04:54 — Raw transcript versus cleaned transcript
Keep both. The raw transcript preserves traceability, while the cleaned transcript becomes the preferred source for summarization and repurposing.

## 05:57 — Where automation is often overestimated
Automation is weak at editorial judgment and exception handling. Agents can propose options, but edge cases still require human ownership.

## 06:31 — Automation should accelerate, not replace responsibility
Use automation for repetitive transforms such as cleanup, notes, chapter suggestions, formatting, and metadata prefill, but keep humans accountable for what goes live.

## 06:57 — Titles as a contract with the listener
Good title generation should produce several angles: problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, and curiosity-driven. The best title depends on audience intent.

## 07:19 — Example title framings
Sample options include: “How Small Teams Build a Reliable Podcast Production Pipeline,” “Why Podcast Production Breaks Even When the Recording Is Great,” and “From Raw Recording to Publish: The Systems Behind a Great Podcast.”

## 08:20 — Highest-value prepublish checks
The episode highlights five key checks before publishing: names and affiliations, links and references, sponsor compliance, sensitive edits, and metadata consistency across title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy.

## 09:23 — Practical first steps for small shows
The recommended starting point is to define folder structure and naming, assign one publish checklist owner, separate raw and cleaned transcripts, define the episode output bundle, and keep a lightweight episode log to spot recurring issues.

## 09:50 — Automation lesson to keep in mind
Automate drafts, not approvals. The machine should produce options; the human should approve what becomes public.
