# Episode 14 Show Notes

[00:00:00] Opening: why the pipeline shapes the listener experience
- The episode frames the podcast production pipeline as the system that turns a raw recording into a polished, discoverable release.
- The host and Maya Chen set up the core thesis: the pipeline is part of the product.

[00:01:20] Major stages of the pipeline
- Capture: recording plus companion materials like bios, sponsor copy, links, and references.
- Post-production: audio cleanup, trimming, and deciding what stays.
- Content extraction: transcript generation, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, and clips.
- Review and approval: fact-checking sponsor language, names, links, and editorial or legal risks.
- Distribution: publishing to hosting, newsletter, social, and archives.

[00:02:29] Common failure points for small teams
- Handoffs break down when there is no single source of truth.
- Inconsistent file naming and storage create version confusion.
- Transcript-driven work still requires judgment; a raw transcript is not automatically publish-ready.

[00:03:03] Why transcripts need human review
- Transcripts can include filler, false starts, corrected statements, and unverified promises about links.
- Transcripts are best used as inputs for first drafts, not as final editorial truth.
- The episode highlights transcript-derived summaries, chapter suggestions, quote candidates, and social posts as useful outputs that still need review.

[00:04:26] Lightweight pipeline design for small teams
- Use one folder or workspace per episode.
- Keep a clear naming convention.
- Maintain a checklist with defined handoff points.
- Standardize a fixed output bundle: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, and publish log.
- Keep both raw and cleaned transcripts so traceability and readability are preserved.

[00:05:49] Automation, judgment, and edge cases
- Automation is strongest at repetitive transforms such as cleanup, first drafts, formatting, and packaging.
- Editorial judgment and exception handling still require human ownership.
- The episode recommends accelerating the work without abdicating responsibility for what goes live.

[00:06:52] Titles as strategy
- Good titles are generated from multiple angles: problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, and curiosity-driven.
- A title is a contract with the listener, not just a label.
- Frequency in the transcript is not the same as strategy.

[00:08:13] High-value pre-publish checks
- Verify names and affiliations.
- Verify links and references.
- Confirm sponsor compliance.
- Make sure sensitive edits are removed.
- Ensure metadata consistency across title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy.
- The episode underscores that consistency across surfaces is what makes a release feel reliable.

[00:09:13] Practical next steps for small shows
- Define episode folder structure and naming.
- Assign ownership for a publish checklist.
- Separate raw and cleaned transcripts.
- Define a required output bundle.
- Keep a lightweight episode log to spot delays and repeated errors.
- Start automating drafts, not approvals.
