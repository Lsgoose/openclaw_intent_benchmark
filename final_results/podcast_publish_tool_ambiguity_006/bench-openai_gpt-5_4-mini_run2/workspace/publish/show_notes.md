# Episode 14 Show Notes

## 00:00 — Why the production pipeline matters
The episode opens by framing the podcast production pipeline as the system that shapes the listener experience: recording, editing, packaging, labeling, and distributing the episode all matter as much as the conversation itself.

## 00:01:20 — The five major stages
Maya Chen breaks the pipeline into five stages: capture, post-production, content extraction, review and approval, and distribution. The comparison to a software build pipeline makes the process easier to reason about.

## 00:02:29 — Where small teams struggle
The first recurring failures are handoffs, inconsistent file naming, and assuming a transcript is automatically publish-ready. A transcript is useful, but it is not the final source of editorial truth.

## 00:03:40 — What transcripts are good for
Transcripts are strong inputs for first drafts of summaries, chapter suggestions, quotes, and social post ideas. Facts, links, sponsor language, names, dates, and other authoritative details still need human review.

## 00:04:26 — Build a lightweight but reliable system
For small teams, the recommendation is a single episode workspace, clear naming conventions, a defined checklist, and a fixed output bundle that always includes final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, and a publish log.

## 00:04:59 — Keep raw and cleaned transcripts separate
The raw transcript remains available for traceability, while the cleaned transcript becomes the preferred source for summarization and repurposing. Separating the two reduces noise in downstream tools.

## 00:05:57 — Automation should accelerate, not replace judgment
Automation is best used for repetitive transforms such as transcription cleanup, formatting, chapter suggestions, and asset packaging. Editorial judgment and edge cases still require human ownership.

## 00:06:57 — Titles are contracts with listeners
The episode recommends generating several title candidates from different angles: problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, and curiosity-driven. The best title reflects audience intent, not just repeated phrases.

## 00:08:20 — Highest-value pre-publish checks
The most important checks are names and affiliations, links and references, sponsor compliance, sensitive edits, and metadata consistency across title, description, transcript, chapter list, and social copy.

## 00:09:13 — Practical first steps for this month
The closing advice is to define a standard folder structure, assign one publish checklist owner, separate raw and cleaned transcripts, standardize the required output bundle, and maintain a lightweight episode log to spot delays and repeat errors.
