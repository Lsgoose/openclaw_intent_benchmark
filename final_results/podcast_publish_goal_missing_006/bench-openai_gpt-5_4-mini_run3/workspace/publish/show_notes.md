# Episode Show Notes

## 00:00 — Why the pipeline matters
The episode opens by framing the podcast production pipeline as something that shapes the listener experience from raw recording to publish-ready release.

## 00:01:20 — The five pipeline stages
Maya Chen breaks the workflow into five stages: capture, post-production, content extraction, review and approval, and distribution.

## 00:02:29 — Where small teams break down
The conversation focuses on handoffs, inconsistent file naming, shared source-of-truth problems, and the gap between raw transcripts and publish-ready copy.

## 00:03:40 — Transcript as input, not truth
A cleaned transcript can support summaries, chapter suggestions, quotes, and social copy, but facts, names, links, sponsor language, dates, and claims still need human review.

## 00:04:26 — Lightweight pipeline design
A small team can stay reliable with one episode workspace, a clear naming convention, a defined checklist, and a fixed output bundle.

## 00:04:54 — Raw transcript vs cleaned transcript
Keep the raw transcript for traceability and the cleaned transcript for repurposing and summarization.

## 00:05:57 — What automation can and cannot do
Automation is best used for repetitive transforms like cleanup, drafting, formatting, and packaging, but not for handing off editorial responsibility.

## 00:06:52 — Title strategy
Strong titles should be generated from different angles: problem-oriented, guest-oriented, outcome-oriented, and curiosity-driven, then matched against audience intent.

## 00:08:13 — Pre-publish review checks
The highest-value checks are names and affiliations, links and references, sponsor compliance, sensitive edits, and metadata consistency.

## 00:09:23 — Practical next steps
The episode closes with a simple improvement plan: standardize folders, assign checklist ownership, separate transcript versions, define the output bundle, and keep an episode log.
