## [00:01:20] The five stages of a podcast production pipeline
- Capture: recording conversations and collecting bios, sponsor copy, links, and references.
- Post-production: cleaning audio, trimming mistakes, deciding what stays in.
- Content extraction: transcripts, summaries, quotes, show notes, chapter markers, titles, clips.
- Review and approval: fact checks, sponsor language, names, links, editorial and legal checks.
- Distribution: pushing the final package to hosting, newsletter, social channels, and archives.

## [00:02:29] Common failure modes in small-team production
- Handoffs without a single source of truth across recording, editing, and copywriting.
- Inconsistent naming and storage leading to files like final_v2_real_final.wav.
- Over-trusting transcripts as publish-ready copy instead of raw material.

---

## [00:03:03] How to treat transcripts as rich input, not final truth
- Why raw transcripts include filler, false starts, corrections, and unverified well link that promises.
- Using transcripts for first drafts of summaries, chapters, quotes, and social copy.
- Keeping human review for facts, links, sponsor language, names, affiliations, dates, and claims.
- Key idea: the transcript is a rich input, not the final source of editorial truth.

---

## [00:04:15] A lightweight pipeline for 23 person teams
- One folder or workspace per episode with a clear naming convention.
- A checklist with defined handoff points across recording, editing, copy, and publish.
- A fixed output bundle: final audio, clean transcript, show notes, title, description, chapter list, thumbnail, and publish log.
- How standardizing outputs makes downstream repurposing and automation easier.

## [00:04:54] Raw vs cleaned transcripts
- Why its useful to keep both raw and cleaned transcripts.
- Cleaning steps: remove obvious disfluencies, fix speaker labels, and format for readability.
- How cleaned transcripts become the preferred source for summaries and repurposing, while raw transcripts support traceability.

## [00:05:49] Where teams overestimate automation
- The limits of automation on editorial judgment and audience positioning.
- Why edge casesguest name corrections, legal concerns, sensitive stories, sponsor constraintsstill need humans.
- The idea that automation should accelerate repetitive transforms, not replace responsibility for what goes live.

## [00:09:13] Practical steps to improve your pipeline this month
- Define a standard episode folder structure and naming convention.
- Create a publish checklist with a clear owner.
- Separate raw and cleaned transcripts in your workflow.
- Define a required output bundle for every episode.
- Keep a lightweight episode log to track delays, confusion, and repeated errors.
- Use automation to generate drafts, and keep humans in charge of approval and final quality.
